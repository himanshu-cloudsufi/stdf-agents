Global copper demand is driven by three converging exponential forces: electrification of transport, build-out of distributed energy infrastructure, and expansion of digital/AI compute infrastructure [C1]. These drivers are fundamentally different from legacy demand patterns—they represent step-function increases in copper intensity per unit of economic activity, not incremental growth.

**Primary Demand Drivers (2026–2036)**

Electrification of transport dominates near-term copper demand growth. Battery electric vehicles require 83 kg of copper per vehicle versus 23 kg for internal combustion engines—a 3.6× increase in copper intensity [C2]. With global EV sales accelerating past 10% market share (the tipping point), cumulative fleet copper demand will grow exponentially through 2030 and continue through vehicle fleet turnover to 2040.

Distributed energy infrastructure (solar-wind-battery systems) creates sustained copper demand across generation, storage, and grid edge. Solar installations require 4–5 tonnes of copper per MW; wind turbines require 3–4 tonnes per MW; grid-scale battery systems add 1–2 tonnes per MWh of storage capacity [C3]. As SWB systems scale to meet electricity demand growth and displace fossil generation, copper demand from this sector will compound annually.

Datacenter and AI infrastructure expansion drives copper demand through power distribution, cooling systems, and high-density interconnects. A single large-scale datacenter requires 1,000–2,000 tonnes of copper; AI training clusters with 100+ MW power draw require proportionally more [C4]. This demand vector is accelerating faster than transport or energy due to AI capability doubling every 7 months.

**Global Consumption Trajectory**

Global copper consumption reached approximately 25–26 million tonnes annually as of recent data [C5]. Electrification and infrastructure build-out will drive consumption to 35–40 million tonnes by 2035, representing 50–60% growth over the decade [C6]. This is not a forecast—it is the physical copper requirement implied by cost-curve-driven adoption of EVs, SWB systems, and AI infrastructure.

**Regional Demand Distribution**

China accounts for roughly 50% of global copper consumption due to manufacturing concentration and domestic EV/solar deployment [C7]. Europe and the USA represent 15–20% combined, with the rest of the world comprising the remainder. However, regional shares will shift as manufacturing and infrastructure investment redistribute globally in response to cost advantages and policy incentives.

**Demand Elasticity and Superabundance Logic**

Copper demand is not capped by current applications. As SWB energy costs collapse toward zero marginal cost, energy-intensive copper refining becomes cheaper, enabling expanded use in applications previously constrained by cost (e.g., replacing aluminum in grid transmission, expanding use in desalination and industrial heat). Jevons Paradox applies: falling effective copper cost (via cheaper energy) will expand total copper demand into new utility, not reduce it.

**System-Level Implication**

Copper is the critical material bottleneck for the Stellar transformation. Unlike lithium (where supply can scale via brine and hard-rock expansion), copper supply growth is constrained by ore grade decline and long lead times for new mines (7–10 years). The mismatch between exponential demand growth and linear supply response will drive copper prices higher through 2030, potentially triggering supply chain adaptations (increased recycling, substitution in non-critical applications, and accelerated mine development).

**Timeline Summary**

| Period | Demand Driver | Copper Demand Impact |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| 2026–2030 | EV fleet growth (10%→40% sales share) | +4–6 Mt/year |
| 2026–2035 | SWB infrastructure build-out | +3–5 Mt/year |
| 2026–2035 | Datacenter/AI expansion | +1–2 Mt/year |
| 2035 | Total global consumption | 35–40 Mt/year |

This analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics and physical material requirements; it excludes speculative demand from financial markets or inventory cycles. Copper demand growth is a direct consequence of the Stellar energy and transport transformation—it is not negotiable.
