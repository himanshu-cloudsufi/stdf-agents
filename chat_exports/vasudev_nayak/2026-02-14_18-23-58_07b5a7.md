The artificial labor disruption analysis process is a canonical bottom-up simulation framework that models AI and robotics convergence at the subtask level, calculating monthly Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) displacement and productivity gains from 2025 through 2030 and beyond.

**Core Methodology**

The system operates on a four-layer hierarchy: Economy → Occupation → Task → Subtask. Analysis begins at the subtask level (48,404 subtasks across 6,960 tasks spanning 186 US occupations covering ~60% of the workforce, or 93 million workers). Each subtask is characterized by time per iteration, token requirements, capability type (cognitive vs physical), importance weighting, relevance scoring, and hourly cost.

**Dual Exponential Model**

Two independent exponential curves drive the disruption:

1. **AI Capability Growth**: Empirically grounded in METR-style benchmarks showing 7-month doubling time. Baseline capability starts at 1,560 seconds (26 minutes) in January 2025, reaching 620,062 seconds (172 hours) by January 2030. Only cognitive subtasks where AI capability exceeds task duration are considered automatable.

2. **AI Cost Decline**: Wright's Law applied to inference costs. Starting at $5.00 per million tokens (January 2025), costs decline 70% annually, reaching $0.012 by 2030, with a floor at $0.01. This trajectory reflects hardware improvements, algorithmic efficiency gains, and scale economies.

**Tipping Point Economics**

Adoption accelerates when AI cost per iteration falls below 50% of human cost per iteration. Before tipping, adoption remains minimal (5%). Post-tipping, adoption follows an S-curve schedule: 5% (Y0) → 10% (Y1) → 20% (Y2) → 40% (Y3) → 80% (Y4+), with linear monthly interpolation. This captures the economic gravity that pulls rational actors toward the lower-cost solution once the threshold is crossed.

**Replacement vs Productivity Split**

Hours freed by AI automation are allocated between two outcomes: replacement (headcount reduction) and productivity (output increase per remaining worker). The default 50/50 split is configurable by year to model organizational behavior evolution. A replacement ceiling (default 80%) enforces human-in-the-loop minimums; overflow hours redirect to productivity.

**Convergence Dynamics**

The framework explicitly models the convergence of AI (cognitive capability), robotics (physical embodiment), and batteries (mobile power). As these technologies improve on independent exponential curves, their intersection creates new automation possibilities. The double-exponential improvement—simultaneous gains in hardware (compute, sensors, actuators) and software (algorithms, training data, transferable skills)—produces acceleration beyond single-technology learning curves.

**Calculation Pipeline**

For each subtask in each month:

1. Check AI capability gate (can AI complete the task duration?)
2. Calculate cost parity (AI cost vs human cost per iteration)
3. Determine tipping status and effective adoption rate
4. Calculate hours freed per 100 employees, weighted by importance and relevance
5. Apply replacement/productivity split
6. Enforce replacement ceiling
7. Scale to actual BLS employment

Aggregation proceeds from subtask → task → occupation → economy, preserving conservation of workers (no double-counting).

**Data Sources**

The system integrates:

- **O*NET task decomposition**: 186 occupations with detailed task/subtask breakdowns
- **BLS employment statistics**: Current workforce counts by occupation
- **METR capability benchmarks**: Empirical AI performance measurements (though specific doubling-time data remains proprietary, the 7-month doubling is calibrated to observed frontier model improvements)
- **Token cost trajectories**: Inference pricing data from major AI providers, projected via Wright's Law
- **Hourly wage data**: Occupation-specific compensation including overhead

**Output Formats**

The system generates:

- **Monthly time-series**: FTE replaced, FTE productivity, adoption rates, capable subtasks, tipping subtasks, AI costs, human costs saved, net savings
- **Occupation-level summaries**: Per-job displacement trajectories
- **Economy-wide aggregates**: Total FTE impact, replacement rates, productivity gains
- **Scenario sensitivity**: CLI overrides for ceiling, tipping threshold, cost decline rate, replacement split, time horizon

**Analytical Framework Principles**

1. **Capability First**: AI must be able to complete the task before any automation occurs. Physical tasks remain excluded until robotics capabilities cross their respective thresholds.

2. **Economics Drives Adoption**: Tipping points are defined by cost parity, not technical feasibility alone. A 50% cost advantage triggers rapid adoption via S-curve dynamics.

3. **Constrained Replacement**: The 80% ceiling reflects organizational realities—full automation is rare; human oversight, exception handling, and judgment remain necessary.

4. **Stock-Flow Consistency**: Workers cannot be replaced twice. The model tracks cumulative displacement and ensures conservation of labor.

5. **Scenario Transparency**: All results are labeled by scenario parameters (ceiling, tipping threshold, split percentages, time horizon). Outputs are projections conditional on assumptions, not certainties.

**Cost Trajectory Implications**

The artificial labor cost curve follows the Stellar framework trajectory: $50/hour (2021) → $10/hour (2025) → $1/hour (2035) → $0.10/hour (2045). With near-zero marginal cost Stellar energy, the real cost per labor-hour approaches zero post-ignition. This creates a 10x-500x cost advantage over human labor, making disruption inevitable once capability thresholds are crossed.

**Decoupling Productivity from Labor Hours**

The framework models the fundamental decoupling of economic output from human labor input. As AI handles cognitive tasks at near-zero marginal cost, productivity gains accrue to capital and remaining workers, while displaced workers exit the labor force or transition to non-automatable roles. This represents a phase change in economic organization, not an incremental shift.

**Validation and Limitations**

The model validates against spreadsheet methodology for hours freed, FTE calculations, cost calculations, and ceiling application. Sanity checks enforce non-negative values, replacement ≤ employment, and correct aggregation. Current limitations include: static employment (no growth/decline modeled), uniform adoption (no early/late adopter heterogeneity), constant token usage (no efficiency improvements), and USA-only geography.

**Conclusion**

This is a deterministic simulation framework grounded in empirical capability growth and cost decline trajectories. It produces occupation-specific displacement timelines driven by economic gravity, not policy or sentiment. The methodology is transparent, reproducible, and scenario-configurable, enabling rigorous sensitivity analysis. Results show that cognitive task automation will displace millions of FTEs by 2030, with acceleration continuing as capability and cost curves compound.
