## Preamble: The Analytical Lens

AI is not a product category. It is a foundational production technology — the cognitive equivalent of electricity — that will restructure every sector it touches. The analytical spine of this document is the service unit: **cost per cognitive task-hour**. When that cost collapses by 57,000× in five years [C1], the downstream consequences are not incremental. They are civilizational.

### 1.3 Technology Convergence Stack

AI does not disrupt in isolation. Three convergences amplify its impact:

1. **AI + Robotics**: Cognitive capability (AI) converging with physical execution (humanoid robots at sub-$20,000 unit cost by the 2030s [C3]) creates artificial labor that displaces both cognitive and physical work.
2. **AI + SWB Energy**: Near-zero marginal cost electricity from solar-wind-battery systems powers AI compute at collapsing cost, reinforcing the inference cost decline [C4].
3. **AI + Precision Biology**: Machine learning with deep neural networks enables annotation of databases of 100 million protein structures [C5], accelerating drug discovery, materials design, and food engineering.

The convergence of AI + Robotics + cheap energy constitutes the Artificial Labor stack — a system producing cognitive and physical work at near-zero marginal cost [C3].

### 1.4 Three Automation Waves (2026–2031)

**Wave 1 — 2026–2027** (≤1.4–4.7 hr capability): Routine cognitive and high-frequency service tasks. Customer service, data entry, scheduling, transaction processing, basic document generation. 30+ occupations cross 10% displacement by 2026 [C6].

**Wave 2 — 2027–2028** (≤4.7–15.3 hr capability): Complex cognitive and professional services. Legal drafting, financial modeling, software architecture, clinical decision support, policy analysis. All professional cognitive work crosses tipping point [C6].

**Wave 3 — 2029–2031** (≤50–540 hr capability): Deep expertise and autonomous programs. Multi-week and multi-month autonomous work execution. All remaining occupational groups reach 30%+ displacement [C6].

## SECTION 3 — POLICY: Governing the Ungovernable

### 3.1 The Policy Paradox

AI policy faces a structural paradox: the technology improves faster than any regulatory framework can be designed, debated, and implemented. By the time a regulation is enacted, the capability it targets has been superseded by two or three doublings. Policy that attempts to constrain AI capability will succeed only in relocating development to less regulated jurisdictions — it will not slow the global trajectory.

The productive policy frame is not constraint but **positioning**: which jurisdictions, sectors, and populations capture the economic surplus generated by AI, and which are left with the displacement costs without the productivity gains [C11].

### 3.2 Three Policy Domains

**3.2.1 Competition and Market Structure**
AI infrastructure exhibits extreme scale economies. The compute, data, and talent required to train frontier models concentrate in a small number of actors. Policy that enables open, competitive, and transparent markets — removing barriers that favor incumbents — accelerates the cost-curve decline and distributes benefits more broadly [C12]. Policy that protects incumbents delays the cost collapse and concentrates surplus in fewer hands.

**3.2.2 Labor Market Restructuring**
By 2030, 43.1 million US FTE are displaced or augmented by AI [C6]. The organizing system — tax codes, social insurance, education systems, labor law — was built for an economy where income derives from labor hours. That system becomes structurally incoherent when 30% of cognitive labor is performed by AI at near-zero cost. The policy question is not whether to prevent displacement (the cost curve makes this impossible) but how to restructure the organizing system to distribute the $5.6 trillion annual productivity gain [C8].

**3.2.3 Geopolitical Positioning**
China is within striking distance of the US across foundational AI sectors [C13]. The country that leads AI infrastructure deployment captures the health, wealth, and economic output that accrues to the leading position [C14]. Smaller, more adaptable jurisdictions — not the largest incumbents — are often better positioned to lead phase changes, precisely because they lack the institutional immune response that slows adoption in dominant powers [C15].
