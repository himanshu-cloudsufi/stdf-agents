## RANK #1 — FOSSIL FUEL & UTILITY BONDS
### Credit Stress Score: 9.5/10 | Rupture Window: 2026–2028 | Exposure: ~$3.1T

**Cost Curve Trajectory**

Solar PV installed cost has declined from $5,310/kW (2010) to $691/kW (2024) — an 87% collapse in 14 years [C1]. The trajectory continues at approximately −10%/yr. SWB system LCOE reaches $17/MWh by 2030 [C2], versus coal marginal cost of $35/MWh (China) and gas marginal cost of $60/MWh (Europe) [C3]. The cost advantage of SWB over new-build fossil is already 3.8x and widens to 5.3x by 2030 [C4].

**Rupture Point**

The rupture point is already behind us in wholesale electricity markets. The X-Curve execution is visible in the data: global fossil generation share falls from 44.2% (2025) to 21.8% (2030) [C5] — a halving in five years while total demand grows 23%. China coal generation revenue collapses from $161B to $21B (−87%) between 2025 and 2030 [C6]. The SWB S-curve crosses the coal X-curve in China in 2027 (SWB 38.2% vs coal 37.2% of generation) [C7].

**Death Spiral Mechanics**

The mechanism is fixed-cost economics: as SWB captures dispatch priority at zero marginal cost, fossil plant capacity factors compress from ~50% toward 25%. Fixed costs (debt service, O&M) spread over fewer kWh — doubling the effective cost per MWh delivered. At 10–15% volume loss, 100% of margin is destroyed. The death spiral is self-reinforcing: capacity factor compression → EBITDA collapse → credit downgrade → higher refinancing cost → accelerated closure → more capacity factor compression [C8].

**Stranded Asset Exposure**

$1.56T in stranded fossil generation assets globally by 2030 [C9]: 936 GW of coal capacity and 193 GW of gas capacity displaced by 4,863 TWh of SWB generation [C10]. China carries the largest single concentration — ~$1.4T of the global total. European gas peakers and CCGT project finance debt face 49% revenue collapse ($34B → $17B) [C11].

**Debt Issuer Categories at Risk**
- China state-owned coal utility bonds (China Huaneng [[COMPANY:CO1]], China Datang [[COMPANY:CO2]], China Guodian [[COMPANY:CO3]])
- European gas CCGT project finance bonds
- US coal utility bonds (Peabody Energy [[COMPANY:CO4]] — already near distressed)
- Infrastructure bank loans to fossil generation assets
- Pension/insurance portfolios with long-duration utility bond exposure

**Market Trauma Timing**

Market trauma precedes physical obsolescence by 3–5 years. Bond spread widening begins 2026 as the 2027 rupture year becomes consensus. Refinancing cliffs for 2020–2022 vintage debt materialize in 2027–2028. Rating agency downgrades — systematically lagged, as the ECB explicitly warned in November 2025 — will be sudden and violent when they occur, not gradual [C12].

## RANK #2 — ICE AUTOMOTIVE DEBT (OEM BONDS + AUTO ABS)
### Credit Stress Score: 8.5/10 | Rupture Window: 2026–2028 | Exposure: ~$2.6T

**Cost Curve Trajectory**

Lithium-ion battery pack cost: $115/kWh globally in 2025 → $43/kWh by 2030 (−63%) [C13]. China already at $84/kWh in 2024 [C14]. The learning rate is 15–18% per cumulative production doubling. At $60/kWh (2026 in China, 2028 globally), BEV sticker price parity is achieved without subsidies for mid-range vehicles [C15]. At $43/kWh by 2030, a 75kWh pack costs $3,225 — below the cost of an ICE powertrain [C16].

**Rupture Point**

BEV price parity tipping points: China 2022 (passed), Europe 2024 (passed), USA 2025 (passed) [C17]. The S-curve inflection (t₀) for China is 2026, Europe 2028, USA 2030 [C18]. Global BEV share reaches 60.8% by 2030 [C19]. China ICE share collapses from 52.6% (2024) to 16.7% (2030) — a 68% volume loss [C20]. Europe ICE falls from 73.1% to 34.1% (−53%) [C21]. USA ICE declines from 88.4% to 52.8% (−40%) [C22].

**Death Spiral Mechanics**

ICE automotive carries $8–12B in fixed costs per platform. The death spiral trigger fires at 10–15% volume loss. China ICE is already past this threshold. Europe fires in 2025–2026. USA fires in 2026–2027. ICE revenue collapses from $2.05T to $1.00T — a $1.05T destruction event in six years [C23]. The collateral mechanism: ICE vehicle residual values trend to zero as BEV share exceeds 50%, destroying the asset base backing $1.2T in auto loan ABS [C24]. ICE-specific suppliers (transmissions, exhaust, fuel injection) face 100% revenue loss — there is no EV equivalent for these components [C25].

**Stranded Asset Exposure**

$300–500B in ICE platform capex committed through 2030 generates no return [C26]. Combined OEM bond exposure: Ford [[COMPANY:CO5]] (~$100B), GM [[COMPANY:CO6]] (~$80B), Volkswagen [[COMPANY:CO7]] (~$200B), Stellantis [[COMPANY:CO8]] (~$30B) = ~$410B in OEM bonds alone [C27]. Tier 1–3 ICE supplier debt adds $200–400B [C28]. Auto ABS backed by ICE collateral: ~$1.2T [C29].

**Debt Issuer Categories at Risk**
- Legacy OEM corporate bonds (Ford Credit, GM Financial, VW Financial Services)
- ICE-specific Tier 1 supplier bonds (exhaust, transmission, fuel injection)
- Auto loan ABS (subordinate tranches backed by ICE collateral)
- Dealer floorplan credit facilities
- Commercial vehicle ICE operators (freight, logistics)

**Market Trauma Timing**

ICE margin compression begins before volume loss as OEMs discount to maintain share against BEV competition. First wave of ICE supplier bankruptcies: 2026–2027. OEM bond spread widening: 2026–2028. Auto ABS subordinate tranche downgrades: 2027–2028. Stellantis [[COMPANY:CO8]] posted its first-ever annual loss in 2025 — the X-Curve is already visible on income statements [C30].

## RANK #3 — AI LABOR DISRUPTION: STAFFING / BPO / OFFICE REIT DEBT
### Credit Stress Score: 8.8/10 | Rupture Window: 2027–2029 | Exposure: ~$930B

*Note: Ranked #3 by rupture timing (2027–2029 vs 2026–2028 for fossil/ICE), but severity score of 8.8 exceeds ICE (8.5) due to breadth of contagion pathways.*

**Cost Curve Trajectory**

AI inference cost: $5.00/M tokens (2025) → $0.012/M tokens (2030) — a 99.8% collapse in five years [C31]. Translated to cognitive labor-hours: $0.50/hr (2025) → $0.0012/hr (2030) [C32]. Human median wage: $23.11/hr (2025) → $27.45/hr (2030) [C33]. The AI cost advantage over human cognitive labor: 46x (2025) → 22,590x (2030) [C34]. This is a double-exponential improvement — hardware (GPU cost-curve dynamics) and software (model capability) improving simultaneously [C35].

**Rupture Point**

The 10x threshold (rupture trigger) was crossed in 2024–2025. The 100x threshold arrives in 2026–2027. The 10,000x threshold arrives in 2029. At each threshold, a new category of human cognitive labor becomes economically non-viable. Economy-wide FTE displacement: 8.7M (2025) → 17.4M (2026) → 27.9M (2027) → 36.9M (2028) → 43.1M (2029) → 46.6M (2030) [C36]. Peak displacement velocity: 2027 at 10.6M new FTE displaced in a single year [C37]. Economy replacement rate reaches 32.6% by 2030 [C38].

**Death Spiral Mechanics**

Staffing/BPO firms face revenue cliffs, not gradual compression. The product being sold (human labor hours) is the product being disrupted. Office & Admin Support shows 15.9% displacement by 2026 and 31.0% by 2028 [C39]. Customer Service Representatives reach 38.8% displacement — the highest rate among large-employment occupations [C40]. The ICR impact: staffing/BPO firms with base ICR of 3.5x face covenant breach at 2.5x as revenue declines 30–40% by 2028 [C41]. Office REIT vacancy rises from 20% (2025) toward 35%+ by 2030 as 6.1M office/admin FTEs and 3.97M management FTEs are displaced [C42].

**Stranded Asset Exposure**

Combined credit exposure: ~$930B across staffing/BPO corporate debt (~$650B global staffing market × 45% revenue at risk = $292B), BPO debt (~$280B market × 70% revenue at risk = $196B), and office REIT bonds (~$400B) [C43]. The office REIT exposure carries a secondary contagion pathway: CMBS tranches backed by suburban and secondary-market office properties face the most acute stress, with municipal bonds tied to downtown commercial tax bases exposed to assessed value repricing [C44].

**Debt Issuer Categories at Risk**
- Staffing firm bonds (Adecco [[COMPANY:CO9]], ManpowerGroup [[COMPANY:CO10]], Randstad [[COMPANY:CO11]])
- BPO operator bonds (Teleperformance [[COMPANY:CO12]], Concentrix [[COMPANY:CO13]])
- Office REIT bonds (Vornado [[COMPANY:CO14]], SL Green [[COMPANY:CO15]], Brookfield Office Properties [[COMPANY:CO16]])
- CMBS tranches backed by suburban office collateral
- Private credit funds with BPO/staffing exposure

**Market Trauma Timing**

Tipping points for customer service, office administration, and retail sales have already passed. Healthcare administration and legal document review tip in 2026–2027. Financial services analysis tips in 2026–2027. High-yield BPO/staffing debt faces covenant breach risk by 2026–2027. Office REIT refinancing walls in 2026–2028 collide with deteriorating NOI [C45].

## RANK #4 — COPPER SUPPLY CHAIN: ICE-ADJACENT FABRICATOR DEBT
### Credit Stress Score: 6.2/10 | Rupture Window: 2027–2030 | Exposure: ~$51B

**Cost Curve Trajectory**

Total copper demand nearly doubles from 15,128 kt (2026) to 29,335 kt (2030) — a +93.9% surge [C46]. Grid generation copper grows at +31.2%/yr CAGR, reaching 14,508 kt by 2030 [C47]. Solar PV alone consumes 11,809 kt by 2030 — more than the entire global copper demand in 2026 [C48]. EV copper intensity: 83 kg/BEV vs 23 kg/ICE — a 3.6x multiplier [C49]. China's share of global copper demand rises from 56.7% (2026) to 65.4% (2030) [C50].

**Rupture Point**

The rupture point for ICE-adjacent copper fabricators is the EV tipping point in their customer base. China ICE copper demand collapses as BEV share reaches 93.2% by 2030 [C51]. Fossil generation copper is already at 0.2% of total demand — economically irrelevant [C52]. The compositional displacement is the stress mechanism: ICE-specific copper applications (radiators, exhaust heat exchangers, fuel system components) face demand collapse proportional to ICE production decline of 60%+ [C53].

**Death Spiral Mechanics**

ICE-adjacent copper fabricators face three simultaneous pressures: (1) customer credit deterioration as ICE OEMs enter their own death spiral, (2) volume mismatch as EV copper demand flows through different supply chains, and (3) copper price spike risk from the +94% demand surge creating a ~4,335 kt supply deficit by 2030 [C54]. Fabricators unable to pass copper price increases to ICE OEM customers — themselves facing margin compression — face a cost-price squeeze that destroys EBITDA.

**Stranded Asset Exposure**

~$51B in ICE-adjacent copper fabricator debt [C55]. This is the lowest-severity stress area because aggregate copper demand growth (+94%) partially offsets compositional displacement. Well-positioned miners (Freeport-McMoRan [[COMPANY:CO17]]) benefit from the demand surge. The credit stress is concentrated in the fabrication layer, not the mining layer.

**Debt Issuer Categories at Risk**
- ICE-specific copper tube/rod fabricators (radiator, exhaust, fuel system components)
- Copper fabricators with long-term ICE OEM offtake agreements
- Legacy mining operators with ICE-supply-chain-adjacent customer bases
- Industrial conglomerates with significant ICE copper fabrication divisions

## CROSS-SECTOR CONTAGION PATHWAY

The four stress areas are not independent. The second-order feedback loop connects them into a unified credit contagion sequence:

1. **2026:** Fossil fuel HY bond spreads widen 400–600 bps → bank balance sheet impairment begins
2. **2026–2027:** ICE auto ABS subordinate tranches downgraded → auto lender credit lines tighten → dealer floorplan financing costs rise → ICE OEM revenues fall faster
3. **2027:** Peak AI displacement (10.6M FTE in one year) → BPO/staffing revenue cliffs → office REIT DSCR covenant breaches begin
4. **2027–2028:** Pension/insurance portfolios holding fossil fuel + auto bonds face simultaneous losses → forced selling → spread widening across all legacy sectors
5. **2028–2029:** Cross-sector contagion (R4 loop) connects all four areas → bank credit tightening → higher cost of capital for all incumbents → accelerated collapse timeline
6. **2028–2030:** Copper supply deficit (~4,335 kt) drives copper price spike → ICE-adjacent fabricator cost-price squeeze → $51B fabricator debt stress materializes

The aggregate credit event across all four areas represents $6.7T+ in legacy debt structures under simultaneous disruption pressure — concentrated in the 2026–2029 rupture window [C56].
