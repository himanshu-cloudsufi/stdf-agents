## Bank of England Rate Path: $200 Oil / $200 TTF Shock, 2026–2028

**Baseline at shock onset (confirmed):** MPC unanimously held Bank Rate at 3.75% on 19 March 2026 following a Middle East supply disruption that had already pushed Brent crude above $100/bbl and TTF above €50/MWh [C1]. UK CPI was 3.0% in January 2026 [C2], services CPI at 4.4% [C3], and underlying Q1 GDP growth estimated at ~0.1% [C4] — a near-stall economy with disinflation nearly complete but not finished.

The $200/barrel oil and $200/MWh TTF scenario represents a further 2x escalation from the already-elevated March 2026 levels. The transmission mechanics and MPC response path are as follows.

---

### CPI Transmission Channels

Four channels carry the shock into UK CPI:

1. **Direct energy bills:** The Q2 2026 Ofgem cap is £1,641/year [C5]. At $200 TTF (4.4x the pre-shock level), the unmitigated cap would rise to approximately £3,900/year — a £2,260/household real income destruction. The MPC's own modelling at current (pre-$200) shock levels estimated the direct energy contribution to CPI at ~¾ percentage point in Q3 2026 [C6]; at $200 TTF, this channel alone adds approximately 5–7 percentage points to CPI.

2. **Motor fuel:** Petrol rises from ~137p/litre toward ~230p/litre, adding approximately 1.5–2 percentage points to CPI through the transport sub-index.

3. **Indirect cost pass-through:** Firms pass higher energy and logistics costs into consumer prices. The MPC estimated indirect effects at ~¼ percentage point at current shock levels [C6]; at $200 oil, this channel scales to approximately 1–2 percentage points within 3–6 months.

4. **Second-round wage effects:** Wage settlements were running at 3.6% in early 2026 [C7]. A real income shock of this magnitude historically triggers re-acceleration of pay demands. The MPC explicitly flagged this as the primary risk: "the potential for second-round effects following recent events in the Middle East remains substantial" [C8]. At $200 oil, AWE (Annual Wage Earnings) likely re-accelerates from ~5.1% toward 7–8% within 6–9 months, sustaining services CPI above 5%.

**CPI peak estimate:** Absent government energy support, UK CPI peaks at approximately 10–12% within 9–12 months of shock onset. With an Energy Price Guarantee-equivalent cap (the 2022 precedent cost ~£25bn over 6 months), the peak is dampened to approximately 7–9%.

---

### The Stagflationary Trade-Off

The MPC's own framework is explicit: "monetary policy cannot reverse this shock to supply" [C9]. Rate hikes cannot produce more oil or gas. The MPC faces a textbook stagflationary dilemma:

- **Tightening** controls inflation expectations but amplifies the demand destruction already underway from the real income shock — with GDP growth already at ~0.1% [C4], a 50–100bps hike risks tipping the economy into contraction.
- **Holding or cutting** supports growth but risks unanchoring inflation expectations. The MPC's credibility on the 2% target is the critical variable — Governor Bailey's March 2026 caution against inferring a rate hike [C10] signals the MPC's initial preference is to assess rather than act.

The institutional memory of 2022 — when delayed tightening allowed second-round effects to embed — creates asymmetric pressure toward earlier action this time. The MPC's March 2026 minutes noted that "some MPC members discussed considering a longer hold, or even a hike at some point to lean against inflation" [C11].

### Inflation Control vs. Growth Support: The MPC's Revealed Preference

The MPC will prioritise **inflation expectations anchoring** over near-term growth support — but will do so with a lighter touch than 2022–2023. The key distinction:

- In 2022, the MPC hiked from 0.1% into a demand-driven inflationary environment. Here, the economy enters the shock already at near-stall with spare capacity [C4], meaning demand destruction from rate hikes is more severe and the growth cost of tightening is higher.
- The MPC's mandate requires it to return CPI to 2% "over a reasonable time horizon" — not immediately. This gives the MPC cover to hold longer before hiking, provided inflation expectations (measured via Decision Maker Panel surveys and gilt breakevens) remain anchored.
- The critical trigger for hikes is AWE re-acceleration above 6.5–7%, not CPI itself. The MPC will tolerate a CPI spike it judges as supply-side and transitory; it will not tolerate wage-price spiral dynamics.

**Government energy support is the swing variable.** If HM Treasury deploys an EPG-equivalent cap within 4–8 weeks (the 2022 precedent makes this institutionally feasible), the MPC's task becomes materially easier — CPI peak is dampened by 2–3 percentage points, reducing the pressure to hike aggressively. The fiscal-monetary interaction is the dominant uncertainty in the rate path.
