## Message 2/2 - Assistant
**Timestamp**: 2026-02-19 09:44:49

The Seba framework uses **per-km (or per-mile) as the fundamental service-unit metric**, not per-km-per-year or per-km-per-lifecycle. The correct comparison basis is **cost per unit of service delivered**—in transportation, that is $/km or $/mile.

**Methodological Rationale**

Disruption analysis requires comparing the **economic cost of delivering one unit of utility** across competing systems. In transportation, the utility is distance traveled. The framework therefore demands:

• **Service-unit basis**: $/km isolates the cost of the service itself, independent of how often or how long the asset is used.
• **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) decomposition**: All costs—capital (depreciation), energy, maintenance, insurance, financing—are normalized to $/km by dividing total lifecycle cost by total lifecycle distance.
• **Utilization-adjusted comparison**: A private car driven 15,000 km/year and a TaaS vehicle driven 100,000 km/year are compared on the same $/km basis, which automatically reflects the utilization advantage of the TaaS vehicle through lower per-km capital cost.

**Why Not Per-Year or Per-Lifecycle?**

**Per-km-per-year** conflates two dimensions (distance and time) and obscures the economic comparison. A vehicle driven 10,000 km/year vs 50,000 km/year would show different $/km/year figures even if the underlying $/km cost is identical. This metric hides the utilization effect rather than exposing it.

**Per-km-per-lifecycle** is redundant. Lifecycle cost divided by lifecycle distance already yields $/km. Adding "per-lifecycle" as a suffix adds no information and risks confusion with annualized or time-based metrics.

**Correct Formula**

For any vehicle system:

**Cost per km = (Capital Cost / Lifetime Distance) + (Energy Cost per km) + (Maintenance Cost per km) + (Insurance Cost per km)**

Where:
• **Capital Cost / Lifetime Distance** = (Purchase Price - Residual Value) / Total km driven over vehicle life
• **Lifetime Distance** = Annual distance × Vehicle lifespan in years (or until scrappage)

**Example: ICE vs BEV vs TaaS**

| System | Capital ($/km) | Energy ($/km) | Maintenance ($/km) | Insurance ($/km) | **Total ($/km)** |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Private ICE | 0.35 | 0.12 | 0.08 | 0.05 | **0.60** |
| Private BEV | 0.30 | 0.04 | 0.03 | 0.05 | **0.42** |
| TaaS (A-EV) | 0.04 | 0.02 | 0.02 | 0.01 | **0.09** |

The TaaS vehicle achieves 0.04 $/km capital cost (vs 0.30-0.35 for private vehicles) because it drives 500,000 km over its life instead of 200,000 km, spreading the capital cost over 2.5× more distance. This utilization advantage is **automatically captured** in the $/km metric without requiring separate per-year or per-lifecycle qualifiers.

**Disruption Threshold**

The 10× cost advantage (TaaS at $0.09/km vs private ICE at $0.60/km) is visible **only** when using $/km as the comparison basis. Per-year metrics would show TaaS as "more expensive" annually (because it drives more km/year), obscuring the per-service-unit economic dominance that drives disruption.

**Conclusion**

Use **$/km (or $/mile)** as the canonical metric. This is the service-unit cost that determines consumer and fleet operator decisions. Per-year and per-lifecycle are derived metrics that add complexity without improving analytical clarity. The Seba framework's predictive power depends on isolating the cost of service delivery, and $/km achieves this with mathematical precision.
