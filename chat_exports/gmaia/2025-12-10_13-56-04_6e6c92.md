# AI Labor Market Disruption Analysis - United States

## Analysis Approach

This analysis uses the Seba disruption framework to model AI's impact on US labor markets as a **non-linear phase change**, not a gradual transition. The approach recognizes that AI disruption follows exponential cost curves (Wright's Law) combined with rapid capability improvements, creating sharp tipping points when AI reaches both **cost parity** (<50% of human labor cost) and **capability parity** (matches or exceeds human performance on task-specific benchmarks).

The analysis segments labor markets by **task type** and **sector**, recognizing that disruption timing varies based on:
- AI capability maturity for specific tasks (measured via benchmarks: MMLU, GSM8K, coding accuracy, multimodal performance)
- Cost trajectories for AI inference and training
- Capital intensity and infrastructure requirements
- Regulatory barriers and adoption friction
- Job role complexity and substitutability

## Methodology Overview

### Phase 1: AI Capability & Cost Analysis
- **Capability tracking**: Analyze 68+ AI benchmarks to identify when AI reaches human-level performance across cognitive domains (reasoning, mathematics, coding, language, vision, multimodal tasks)
- **Cost curve modeling**: Track GPU economics, inference costs, and training costs using Wright's Law to project cost-per-task trajectories
- **Tipping point detection**: Identify when AI simultaneously achieves cost advantage AND capability parity by task category

### Phase 2: Sector Segmentation & Disruption Timing
- **Segment US labor market** into sectors based on task composition:
  - **Early disruption** (2025-2028): High-volume, standardized cognitive tasks (customer service, data entry, basic coding, content moderation)
  - **Mainstream disruption** (2028-2032): Professional knowledge work (legal research, accounting, middle management, junior engineering, creative production)
  - **Late disruption** (2032-2040): Complex decision-making, specialized expertise, roles requiring physical presence + cognition

### Phase 3: S-Curve Adoption Modeling
- Model AI adoption rates using **logistic S-curves** anchored to sector-specific tipping points
- Account for adoption friction: retraining costs, organizational inertia, regulatory approval, union resistance
- Differentiate between **task automation** (AI augments humans) vs **role displacement** (AI fully substitutes)

### Phase 4: Economic Impact Quantification
- **Labor displacement**: Calculate job-years at risk by sector and timeline
- **Productivity gains**: Model GDP impact from AI-driven productivity improvements
- **Wage pressure**: Analyze downward pressure on wages in automatable roles pre-displacement
- **New job creation**: Estimate new roles created in AI deployment, oversight, and emergent industries

### Phase 5: Cascade Effects & Second-Order Impacts
- **Income inequality**: Concentration of gains in capital vs labor, skill-biased disruption
- **Geographic disparities**: Regional economic impacts based on industry concentration
- **Feedback loops**: Cheaper AI → more adoption → more data → better AI → cheaper AI (reinforcing cycle)
