The artificial labor analysis methodology is a deterministic, bottom-up simulation framework that models AI-driven job displacement through capability growth, cost-parity tipping points, and adoption dynamics. It rejects linear extrapolation and consensus forecasts in favor of exponential cost-curve mechanics and task-level economic substitution logic.

**Framework Architecture**

The model operates on a three-layer hierarchy: Economy → Occupational Groups → Individual Jobs → Tasks → Subtasks. Each job is decomposed into discrete tasks, each task into subtasks, and each subtask is gated by two constraints: AI capability (whether the technology can perform the task) and cost parity (whether it is economically rational to substitute human labor). Displacement occurs only when both gates are satisfied.

AI capability follows a double-exponential improvement curve. Hardware (compute, robotics) and software (algorithms, training efficiency) both improve exponentially and independently, compounding into capability growth measured in METR-style benchmarks. Historical data shows AI capability doubling every 7-9 months, with token costs collapsing from $60 per million tokens in 2021 to $0.50 in 2025—a 120x improvement in four years. The model projects this trajectory forward, with labor costs reaching $1 per hour by 2035 and $0.10 per hour by 2045, driven by near-zero marginal cost Stellar energy.

Cost parity is calculated at the subtask level using total cost of ownership (TCO) comparisons. Human labor cost includes wages, benefits, overhead, training, and turnover. AI labor cost includes compute, inference, robotics hardware amortization, maintenance, and supervision. When AI TCO per task-hour falls below human TCO, the economic tipping point is reached. The model does not assume regulatory delays, adoption friction, or social resistance in the baseline scenario—these are modeled as sensitivity cases only when explicitly requested.

**Data Sources and Inputs**

The model uses empirical datasets for occupational structure (BLS, O*NET), task decomposition (proprietary task taxonomy), AI capability benchmarks (METR, OpenAI, Anthropic capability reports), compute cost trajectories (GPU pricing, inference cost data), and wage data by occupation and region. Historical disruption precedents (digital cameras, smartphones, EVs) calibrate adoption S-curve parameters. Wright's Law learning rates (20% cost reduction per cumulative doubling) anchor cost projections.

**Step-by-Step Reasoning Process**

1. **Capability Gating**: For each subtask in each job, the model checks whether AI capability has crossed the performance threshold required to execute that subtask reliably. Capability is modeled as a cumulative distribution function, with thresholds varying by task complexity (routine cognitive tasks tip earliest, creative and interpersonal tasks tip later).

2. **Cost-Parity Tipping**: Once capability is achieved, the model calculates the month when AI cost per task-hour falls below human cost per task-hour. This is the economic tipping point. The model does not wait for "market readiness" or "infrastructure build-out"—these adapt rapidly post-tipping, as evidenced by historical disruptions (cars went from 2% to 95% in 20 years despite zero infrastructure at the start).

3. **Adoption Dynamics**: Post-tipping, adoption follows a left-skewed S-curve. Approximately 60% of adoption occurs before the 50% inflection point, and 40% occurs after. The model uses a logistic function with parameters derived from historical technology adoption rates. Adoption is faster in sectors with high labor turnover, lower capital intensity, and competitive pressure (e.g., customer service, data entry, logistics). It is slower in regulated sectors (healthcare, legal) and capital-intensive industries (manufacturing), but these lags are modeled as 2-5 year delays, not decades.

4. **Displacement vs. Productivity Split**: The model distinguishes between full displacement (FTE eliminated) and productivity augmentation (FTE retained but output increased). The split is task-dependent: routine tasks skew toward displacement, judgment tasks skew toward augmentation. The baseline assumption is 70% displacement, 30% productivity, calibrated to historical automation patterns. This is a user-adjustable parameter.

5. **Feedback Loops**: The model incorporates positive feedback loops. As AI costs fall, adoption accelerates, driving further scale and cost reduction (virtuous cycle). For incumbents (human labor markets), volume loss (fewer jobs) does not reduce unit cost (wages are sticky), but it does reduce bargaining power and accelerate substitution (vicious cycle). The model does not assume new job creation offsets displacement at a 1:1 ratio—historical evidence shows disruption creates fewer, different jobs, and the transition period involves structural unemployment.

6. **Aggregation and Output**: Subtask-level displacement is aggregated to task level, then job level, then occupational group level, then economy level. Outputs include monthly and yearly FTE displaced, economy replacement rate (percentage of total labor force displaced), sector-specific impacts, and timeline milestones (e.g., 10% economy replacement, 50% customer service automation).

**Key Assumptions and Invariants**

- AI capability growth continues at historical rates (7-9 month doubling) through 2035, then decelerates modestly.
- Cost curves follow Wright's Law; no artificial floors are imposed.
- Adoption barriers (regulation, infrastructure, sentiment) delay but do not prevent disruption. Delays are modeled as 2-5 years post-tipping, not indefinite.
- Labor markets do not self-correct at the speed of displacement. Structural unemployment persists during the transition.
- No "lump of labor" fallacy: the model does not assume fixed demand for labor. However, it also does not assume demand elasticity fully offsets displacement—empirical evidence from prior disruptions (agricultural mechanization, manufacturing automation) shows net job loss in disrupted sectors.
- The model excludes speculative "new job categories" unless they are quantifiable and have historical precedent.

**Methodology-Driven Conclusions**

The artificial labor framework produces displacement timelines that are faster and larger in magnitude than consensus forecasts because it models economic substitution at the task level rather than the job level, applies exponential cost curves rather than linear projections, and does not impose fictional constraints (e.g., "AI will never replace creativity" or "regulation will slow adoption indefinitely"). The model's outputs are deterministic given the input parameters—they are not predictions in the sense of guesses, but projections of cost-curve dynamics. If AI costs fall as projected and capability continues to improve, displacement at the scale of 10-20% of the labor force by 2030-2035 is not speculative; it is the mathematical consequence of economic gravity. The framework is unapologetic about these conclusions because they are grounded in the same cost-capability mechanics that accurately predicted EV adoption, solar cost collapse, and digital camera disruption. The methodology does not accommodate user hypotheses or consensus expectations unless they are supported by cost-curve evidence. If the numbers seem large or the timelines seem aggressive, that is a feature of exponential disruption, not a flaw in the model.
