The USA unemployment rate in December 2026 will be 4.2% (range: 3.8–4.6%) [C1]. This reflects AI-driven job displacement beginning to register in aggregate statistics, overlaid on baseline macroeconomic dynamics.

**Methodology**

Unemployment is the residual of labor force size minus employed persons. The forecast integrates three components:

1. **Baseline macroeconomic trajectory**: January 2026 unemployment stands at approximately 4.0% [C2]. Absent disruption, cyclical dynamics and Federal Reserve policy suggest modest drift toward 4.1–4.3% by year-end [C3].

2. **AI-driven job displacement**: The artificial-labor skill models task-level automation across occupations. By December 2026, cumulative AI-driven job losses reach approximately 180,000–220,000 FTEs in customer service, production supervision, and administrative roles [C4]. This represents 0.12–0.15% of the 147 million-person labor force [C5].

3. **Reabsorption dynamics**: Historical labor market flexibility suggests 40–60% of displaced workers find new employment within 6–9 months, particularly in sectors with unfilled vacancies (healthcare aides, logistics, construction) [C6]. Net unemployment impact: +0.05–0.09 percentage points by December 2026.

**Key Drivers**

AI capability growth continues at 6–8 month doubling intervals through 2026 [C7]. Cost per cognitive task-hour falls from ~$10 (January 2026) to ~$6–7 (December 2026) [C8], crossing cost-parity thresholds for routine customer service and data entry. Adoption lags capability by 9–18 months due to integration friction, limiting 2026 displacement to early-adopter firms.

**Timeline**

**Confidence Intervals**

The 3.8–4.6% range reflects:
- **Lower bound (3.8%)**: Strong GDP growth (3.0%+), rapid reabsorption, slower AI adoption.
- **Upper bound (4.6%)**: Recession onset, credit tightening, faster AI deployment in high-headcount sectors.

AI displacement remains a second-order effect in 2026. The primary driver is baseline macro conditions. By 2028–2030, AI displacement will dominate, pushing structural unemployment materially higher as capability crosses thresholds for millions of knowledge-worker tasks [C9].

- No major fiscal or monetary policy shocks
