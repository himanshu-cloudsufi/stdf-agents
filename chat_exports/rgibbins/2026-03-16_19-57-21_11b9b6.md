Is 20% Reabsorption Realistic?
The 20% assumption is the most historically grounded estimate for a permanent structural displacement shock C1:

10% reabsorption: Consistent with 1980s UK deindustrialization in concentrated regions (South Wales, Northeast England), where 5-year reabsorption was below 30%. For a 12-month window with permanent displacement, this is the lower bound.
20% reabsorption: Consistent with the pace of UK labor market adjustment post-2008 financial crisis (~5 years to recover employment levels, implying ~20% annual reabsorption). This is the most defensible single-point estimate. AI displacement is permanent — it does not reverse when demand recovers, unlike cyclical unemployment.
35% reabsorption: Requires ~700K new roles created in the UK in 2026 alone. Consistent with post-COVID recovery pace (2020–2022), but that was demand-driven. AI displacement is supply-side substitution — demand for the displaced workers' existing skills does not return.
50% reabsorption: Requires ~920K net new UK jobs in 2026. The UK has never created 920K net new jobs in a single calendar year C1. This is a theoretical lower bound on impact, not a realistic scenario.
The 20% reabsorption assumption in the prior analysis is defensible. The 50% assumption is not supported by any UK historical precedent.

Historical Precedent for 9-Month Displacement Windows

London's gross displacement rate *exceeds* the national average by 2032 (38.5% vs 33.7%) because it is over-indexed in the highest-displacement sectors: Business & Financial (2.1× national weight), Legal (2.3×), Computer & IT (1.9×), Management (1.8×). These sectors reach 37–37.5% displacement rates by 2031 [C1].

The paradox: London's highest-displacement sectors are also its highest-reabsorption sectors. Financial services workers pivot to AI governance and fintech. Legal professionals move to AI-augmented compliance. The same sector concentration that drives London's gross displacement rate also funds its reabsorption capacity. London's effective reabsorption rate: 39.8% × 1.85 = **73.6%** [C1].

North England and Wales face the inverse: over-indexed in Production (1.5–1.6× national weight), Transportation (1.3–1.4×), and Construction (1.2×) — sectors with moderate displacement rates but near-zero reabsorption pathways. Wales effective reabsorption rate: 39.8% × 0.45 = **17.9%** [C1].

The split is the **primary determinant** of UC system breach — more consequential than any other single parameter [C1]:

- A **10pp shift toward productivity (50→40)** eliminates the UC breach entirely, reduces cumulative exposure by £27bn, keeps peak claimants below 7.5M
- A **10pp shift toward replacement (50→60)** advances the breach to 2028, adds £27bn to cumulative exposure, pushes peak claimants to 9.3M
- A **20pp shift toward replacement (50→70)** advances the breach to 2027, adds £54bn, creates a sustained 6-year breach period

The 50/50 canonical split reflects current organizational behavior in the absence of policy. The 40/60 scenario reflects what is achievable with statutory incentive intervention. **The difference between those two scenarios is the entire UC breach** [C1].
