**LPC — Low Pay Commission**

The **Low Pay Commission** is the UK's independent statutory advisory body responsible for recommending the level of the National Living Wage (NLW) and National Minimum Wage (NMW) to the government.

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Legal basis** | National Minimum Wage Act 1998; mandate extended to NLW from 2016 |
| **Mandate** | Recommend NLW/NMW rates that are as high as possible without damaging employment or the economy |
| **Composition** | 9 commissioners: 3 employer representatives, 3 worker/union representatives, 3 independent economists — chaired by an independent academic |
| **Reporting cycle** | Annual — research conducted Jan–Sep; recommendation published **November**; government implements the following **April** |
| **Data vintage** | Recommendations are based on mid-year labour market data from the *prior* year — creating a structural 12–18 month lag between economic conditions and rate implementation |
| **Government relationship** | Advisory, not binding — but government has accepted every LPC recommendation since 1999 in practice |

**Relevance to the NLW-stickiness thesis:**

The LPC is the institutional mechanism that creates the dominant transmission lag in this analysis. The April 2025 NLW rate (£12.21/hr, +6.7%) was recommended by the LPC in November 2024, based on mid-2024 labour market data — data collected before AI displacement generated any measurable slack in the UK labour market. The LPC's mandate to set rates "as high as possible without damaging employment" means it responds to *observed* unemployment, not *projected* displacement.
