**Starting positions (confirmed):** BoE Bank Rate 3.75% (held unanimously, 19 March 2026) [C1]; Fed funds rate 3.50–3.75% (held 11-1, 18 March 2026) [C2]. Both central banks are now operating in a confirmed stagflationary pincer — not a theoretical one.

### 1. Transmission Mechanism: Oil/Gas Shock → CPI

**The asymmetry is structural and decisive.**

The Hormuz closure removes 14.7 mb/d of oil and 105 BCM/year of LNG from global markets simultaneously — a 14.3% disruption to global oil supply and 25% of global LNG trade [C3]. The UK and US face fundamentally different transmission channels:

**United States — Gasoline-Dominated, Contained**
The US is a net oil exporter (~3 mb/d net) with domestic shale supply entirely Hormuz-independent. The transmission is almost entirely through globally-priced refined products. At Brent $115/bbl (+$40/bbl above the pre-shock baseline), US gasoline rises +$0.96/gallon to ~$4.36/gallon (+28%) [C3]. Wholesale electricity is barely affected (+$2.9/MWh) because Henry Hub rises only ~20% to ~$4.20/MMBtu — the US gas market is domestically supplied [C3]. Total CPI impact: **+1.44 percentage points** [C3], dominated by transport fuel (3.4% basket weight). The BoE's own March minutes estimate the direct oil price effect on UK CPI at ~¾pp in Q3 2026 [C4] — the US equivalent is lower still given smaller energy basket weights.

**United Kingdom — Gas-Dominated, Severe**
The UK is structurally exposed on both vectors simultaneously. Approximately 18% of UK gas demand originates from Qatari LNG — directly Hormuz-dependent [C3]. TTF wholesale gas rises ~150% to ~€88/MWh [C3]. The Ofgem price cap introduces a one-quarter lag: the full retail gas price shock hits households in the Q3 2026 July reset. Residential gas (2.5% CPI weight) rises ~105% at retail, contributing 2.62pp of direct CPI pressure alone [C3]. Petrol adds 0.33pp; electricity adds 0.26pp [C3]. With second-order pass-through (supply chain, food, services), total UK CPI impact: **+4.82 percentage points** [C3].

The BoE's March 2026 assessment already revised Q2 CPI up to ~3% versus the 2.1% projected in February [C4]. The full gas shock hits in Q3. UK CPI trajectory: **3.0% (Jan 2026 actual) → ~3.5–4.0% (Q2 2026) → ~6.5–7.5% (Q3–Q4 2026)** if the Hormuz disruption persists through summer [C4][C3].

US CPI trajectory: **~2.4% (Feb 2026 estimate) → ~3.5–4.0% (Q2–Q3 2026)** — elevated but not crisis-level [C3].

### 2. The Stagflationary Dilemma — How Each Central Bank Resolves It

**The dilemma is real but asymmetric in severity.**

Both central banks face the same textbook conflict: a supply shock raises inflation while simultaneously crushing growth. Hiking to fight inflation deepens the recession; cutting to support growth risks embedding second-round inflation. The resolution differs because the severity of each horn of the dilemma differs by country.

**Federal Reserve — Growth Concern Dominates, Hold Bias**
The US growth picture is already deteriorating independently of the oil shock: Q4 2025 GDP was revised down to 0.7% QoQ SAAR [C5], and February non-farm payrolls contracted by 92,000 jobs [C5]. The oil shock adds ~1.44pp to CPI — painful but not existential for a central bank with a dual mandate. Powell has explicitly rejected 1970s stagflation comparisons [C6], signaling the Fed will not reflexively hike into a supply shock. The dot plot still shows one cut in 2026, though seven of 19 officials now favor holding all year [C7]. The Fed's revealed preference is to **look through the energy-driven CPI spike** unless core services inflation (wages, shelter) re-accelerates. With February payrolls already negative, the growth leg of the dual mandate is flashing red. The Fed holds, watches core PCE, and does not hike unless wage growth re-accelerates above 4.5%.

**Bank of England — Inflation Concern Dominates, Hike Risk Real**
The BoE's dilemma is more acute. UK CPI was already at 3.0% in January 2026 [C8] — above the 2% target — before the shock. The incoming gas price surge will push headline CPI toward 7% by Q3 2026 [C3][C4]. The MPC's March minutes explicitly flagged "second-round effects in wage and price-setting" and noted that "the recent experience of high inflation may make households and businesses more sensitive to a new inflationary shock" [C4]. UK wage settlements are running at 3.6% [C4] — not yet dangerous, but the MPC is watching for acceleration. The BoE has a **single mandate** (2% CPI) with no explicit growth leg. When CPI is heading toward 7%, the political and institutional pressure to act is severe — even if the MPC intellectually understands the shock is supply-driven. The February split (5 hold, 4 cut) [C9] has already reversed: money markets now price Bank Rate **rising** rather than falling in 2026 [C8].

The BoE's resolution: **hold through Q2, hike in Q3 if second-round effects materialize in wage data.** The hike is not certain — it depends on whether the July Ofgem reset triggers wage renegotiations — but it is the base case given the MPC's single mandate and the magnitude of the incoming CPI print.
