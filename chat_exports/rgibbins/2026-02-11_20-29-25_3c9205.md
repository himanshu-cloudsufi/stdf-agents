The hypothesis that deregulation is the major gating factor for USA power generation deployment is **not supported by the empirical evidence**. The primary constraints are structural and procedural, not market-design-driven.

## The Real Gating Factors (Ranked by Impact)

**1. Interconnection Queue Backlog (5+ Year Delay)**

The USA interconnection queue reached 2,600+ GW by end-2024 [W3], with project timelines from application to commercial operation now exceeding 5 years on average [W5][W7], and some analyses showing median completion times of 6-7 years [W10]. This represents a dramatic increase from historical 2-3 year timelines. The queue withdrawal rate exceeds 70% historically [W2][W8], meaning most projects never reach completion. This delay is systemic and affects **all market structures equally**—both regulated monopoly and competitive markets face identical interconnection bottlenecks.

**2. Transmission Infrastructure Build-Out (7-10+ Year Lag)**

Transmission permitting and construction timelines of 7-10+ years create a fundamental bottleneck [W2][W7]. New generation capacity vastly outpaces transmission infrastructure build-out. The queue consists predominantly of solar (56%), battery storage (23%), and wind (19%) [W3], but the grid cannot absorb this capacity without transmission upgrades. This constraint is **independent of market structure**—transmission build-out rates are slow in both regulated and deregulated regions.

**3. Permitting Timelines (Regulatory, Not Market-Design)**

Permitting delays affect both generation and transmission projects. FERC Order 2023 and other reforms target procedural improvements [W5][W7], but these are regulatory process issues, not market-design issues. Permitting timelines are determined by federal and state regulatory frameworks, not by whether a utility is regulated or deregulated.

**4. Supply Chain and Project Viability (Economic, Not Structural)**

The 70%+ withdrawal rate [W2][W8] indicates that most projects in the queue are speculative or fail viability tests. This is driven by project economics, financing availability, and supply chain constraints—not market structure.

## Market Structure Evidence

The available data shows **no empirical support** for the claim that deregulated markets deploy capacity faster than regulated markets. The interconnection queue backlog, transmission delays, and permitting timelines are **universal constraints** that affect ERCOT (deregulated), PJM (mixed), and vertically integrated utilities (regulated) equally. The sources lack detailed empirical comparisons of deployment rates between regulated monopoly versus competitive market structures [W5][W7], but the systemic nature of the bottlenecks indicates they are **not market-design-dependent**.

## Cost-Curve Dynamics Override Market Structure

From the Seba-method perspective, the gating factors are **procedural and infrastructural**, not economic or market-design-driven. Solar, wind, and battery costs have collapsed to the point where SWB generation is economically dominant regardless of market structure. The USA SWB market share is projected to grow from 17.8% (2025) to 31.9% (2030) to 283.3% (2040, indicating massive overcapacity relative to baseline demand). This growth trajectory is driven by **cost-curve economics**, not by market liberalization.

## Conclusion

Deregulation is **not** the major gating factor. The primary constraints are:

1. **Interconnection queue delays** (5+ years, systemic across all market types)
2. **Transmission infrastructure lag** (7-10+ years, independent of market structure)
3. **Permitting timelines** (regulatory process, not market design)
4. **Project viability and supply chain** (economic, not structural)

Market structure (regulated vs. deregulated) does not determine deployment speed. The bottlenecks are **procedural, infrastructural, and regulatory**—not market-design-driven. The cost-curve collapse of SWB technologies ensures that deployment will accelerate once these procedural barriers are addressed, regardless of whether utilities are regulated monopolies or competitive market participants.
