## Why Cost is Primary

**Cost + Capability determine adoption:**
- When a disruptive technology reaches **cost parity** (equal cost per service unit), it begins gaining market attention
- When it hits the **tipping point** (typically 50-70% lower cost AND/OR 2-3× better capabilities), adoption accelerates exponentially
- Everything else—policy, infrastructure, culture—acts as friction or acceleration, but doesn't change the underlying economic force

## Service-Level Cost Comparison (Not Hardware)

The framework always compares **cost per service unit**, not sticker prices:

| Service | Incumbent | Disruptor | Unit |
|---------|-----------|-----------|------|
| **Mobility** | $1.20/mile (owned ICE) | $0.10/mile (autonomous EV fleet) | $/passenger-mile |
| **Electricity** | $0.06-0.15/kWh (coal/gas) | $0.01-0.03/kWh (SWB) | $/kWh delivered |
| **Compute** | $5/inference (cloud) | $0.10/inference (local AI) | $/inference |
| **Food Protein** | $20/kg (beef) | $2/kg (precision fermentation by 2030s) | $/kg protein |
| **Energy Storage** | $300/kWh (VRLA, 5yr) | $150/kWh (Li-ion, 12yr) → **$60/kWh TCO** | $/kWh over lifetime |

## Wright's Law: Cost Declines Are Physics, Not Opinion

Cost trajectories follow **learning curves** (Wright's Law):
$$
C_t = C_0 \cdot Q^{-\alpha}
$$
Where \( Q \) = cumulative production, \( \alpha \) = learning rate

- **Lithium-ion batteries:** ~18-20% cost decline per doubling of production
- **Solar PV:** ~20-24% decline per doubling
- **Compute (AI):** ~70-80% decline per 18 months (Moore's Law variant)

These are **empirical laws**, not forecasts. Cost declines are baked into exponential production scaling.

## Narratives Don't Change Cost Curves

- "Hydrogen economy" narrative → Hydrogen still 3-5× more expensive per mile than BEVs
- "Baseload power needed" narrative → SWB + storage now cheaper than coal/gas in most regions
- "Gradual transition" narrative → Markets don't care; cost parity triggers rapid S-curve adoption (10-15 years)

**The market follows cost, not stories.**
