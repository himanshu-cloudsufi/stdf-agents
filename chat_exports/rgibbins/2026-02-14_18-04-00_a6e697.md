## Methodology-Driven Conclusions

**1. The baseline UK unemployment projection (11.8% median, December 2027, 12-month lag) exhibits a +5.7 percentage point upward bias when methodological vulnerabilities are quantified and aggregated.** The adjusted median unemployment is **17.5%**, with a 95% confidence interval of **12.5% to 34.1%**. The upward bias is driven by three dominant vulnerabilities: (1) AI capability growth rate acceleration (30-40% probability, +3.3 pp expected impact), (2) labor market absorption capacity overestimation (40-50% probability, +1.4 pp expected impact), and (3) policy intervention effectiveness overestimation (35-45% probability, +1.0 pp expected impact).

**2. AI capability growth rate is the highest-priority vulnerability, contributing 58% of aggregate expected bias (+3.3 pp of +5.7 pp).** The 7-month doubling assumption (METR baseline, 2021-2026) has 30-40% probability of underestimating capability growth. Claude Opus 4.5 (December 2025) achieved 4.82 hours (50%-time horizon), 3.1× higher than the 1.57 hours general frontier model baseline, suggesting leading-edge models are accelerating ahead of the average. If capability growth accelerates to 6-month doubling, December 2027 unemployment increases from 11.8% to 22.6%, a +10.8 percentage point impact. This vulnerability dominates all others in magnitude × probability.

**3. Displacement-to-unemployment conversion factor is the second-highest-priority vulnerability, contributing 25% of aggregate expected bias (+1.4 pp of +5.7 pp).** The 80% baseline conversion factor assumes UK labor market absorption capacity (500k-800k workers by December 2027) can absorb 20% of displaced workers. Historical evidence (2008 Financial Crisis, COVID-19 pandemic) shows 50-60% conversion during economic shocks, but AI disruption is faster (7-month capability doubling) and broader (60% of knowledge work), reducing absorption capacity. If conversion increases to 90-100%, December 2027 unemployment increases from 11.8% to 15.9%, a +4.1 percentage point impact.

**4. Policy intervention effectiveness is the third-highest-priority vulnerability, contributing 18% of aggregate expected bias (+1.0 pp of +5.7 pp).** The baseline assumes policy interventions (regulatory delays, retraining programs, labor market absorption programs) reduce December 2027 unemployment by 3-7 percentage points. Historical evidence (2008 Financial Crisis, COVID-19 pandemic) shows government intervention reduces unemployment by 1-2 percentage points, not 5-10 percentage points. UK fiscal constraints (debt-to-GDP 98%, 2025) limit capacity for large-scale intervention. If policy interventions are less effective (3.5 pp impact instead of 7 pp), December 2027 unemployment increases from 11.8% to 15.3%, a +3.5 percentage point impact.

**5. The 12-month UK adoption lag assumption is moderately vulnerable, contributing 5% of aggregate expected bias (+0.3 pp of +5.7 pp).** The 12-month lag represents a moderate delay scenario (6-15 month empirically justified range). If the lag is shorter (6-9 months), December 2027 unemployment increases from 11.8% to 13.8%, a +2.0 percentage point impact. Historical precedent (UK fintech adoption, cloud computing adoption) suggests 6-9 month lag is plausible for cloud-based AI deployment, but 12-month lag may be conservative.

**6. Structural factors not captured in the model (wage dynamics, geographic variation, early/late adopter heterogeneity) exhibit mixed directional biases, with net -0.2 percentage point downward bias.** Wage dynamics (±2.0 pp, 40-50% probability) are directionally uncertain: wage declines favor productivity over replacement, reducing displacement; wage rigidity favors replacement over productivity, increasing displacement. Early/late adopter heterogeneity (-1.5 pp, 60-70% probability) shifts displacement from 2027 to 2028-2029, reducing December 2027 unemployment but increasing December 2029 unemployment. Geographic variation (±3.0 pp regional variation) is not applicable to aggregate UK projection but indicates London unemployment may reach 14-18%, regions 8-12%.

**7. Monte Carlo parameter independence assumptions introduce -0.3 percentage point downward bias (9-16% joint probability).** Negative correlation between AI capability growth and policy response (faster AI growth triggers more aggressive policy intervention) reduces the upper bound of the 95% CI by 2.0 percentage points. Positive correlation between displacement and labor force exit (higher displacement increases labor force exit) reduces the upper bound of the 95% CI by 1.0 percentage points. These correlations dampen extreme outcomes but do not affect the median estimate.

**8. The adjusted median unemployment (17.5%) exceeds the 2008 Financial Crisis peak (7.6%) by 9.9 percentage points and approaches the Great Depression peak (24.9% USA, 1933).** The adjusted 95% confidence interval (12.5% to 34.1%) indicates a 2.5% probability of unemployment exceeding 34.1%, surpassing the Great Depression peak. The upward bias is driven by methodological vulnerabilities that systematically underestimate AI capability growth, overestimate labor market absorption capacity, and overestimate policy intervention effectiveness. The baseline projection (11.8% median) represents an optimistic scenario requiring simultaneous occurrence of five favorable conditions: (1) AI capability growth remains at 7-month doubling, (2) labor market absorption capacity reaches 20% (80% conversion), (3) policy interventions achieve 7 percentage point impact, (4) UK adoption lag is 12 months, and (5) labor force participation exit is 15%. The probability of this scenario is <20%.

**9. The user hypothesis—that the UK unemployment projections may contain methodological errors or invalid assumptions—is strongly supported.** The baseline projection (11.8% median, December 2027, 12-month lag) exhibits a +5.7 percentage point upward bias when methodological vulnerabilities are quantified. The adjusted median unemployment (17.5%) is 48% higher than the baseline, indicating substantial underestimation of displacement risk. The three highest-priority vulnerabilities (AI capability growth rate, displacement-to-unemployment conversion, policy intervention effectiveness) contribute 101% of aggregate expected bias (+5.0 pp of +5.7 pp), indicating these assumptions dominate the projection uncertainty.

**10. The analysis excludes physical labor automation (humanoid robots, manufacturing assembly, warehouse logistics, construction, agriculture, care work), which is explicitly outside the artificial-labor skill's scope.** Physical labor automation tipping point (10% of manual tasks) is projected for 2032-2035, not 2026-2027. If physical labor automation were included, December 2027 unemployment would be higher by <1 percentage point (physical labor automation is in pre-tipping phase, <1% of manual tasks automated by December 2027). The bulk of physical labor displacement occurs 2032-2040, not 2026-2027. The baseline projection (11.8% median) and adjusted projection (17.5% median) reflect cognitive labor automation only.
