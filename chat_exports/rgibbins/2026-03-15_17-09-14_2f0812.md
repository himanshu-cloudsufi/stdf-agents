## UK Labour Market: Unemployment Rate Trajectory 2026–2028

**Scope:** UK national aggregate. Analysis integrates ONS current-state data [C1], AI displacement modelling (cognitive automation only, 341 occupations, 89,040 subtasks), and cyclical macro drivers. Regulatory/adoption lags excluded.

---

### 1. Current State: March 2026 Baseline

The ONS Labour Market release for February 2026 recorded a UK unemployment rate of **5.2%** [C1], with youth unemployment at 14% [C1]. This is materially above the 4.2% trough seen in 2023–2024, reflecting a cyclical deterioration driven by elevated employer National Insurance contributions (effective April 2025), wage cost pressures, and a hiring freeze across mid-market UK employers. The hiring environment as of early 2026 is characterised by caution with tentative signs of stabilisation [C2].

---

### 2. Key Structural and Cyclical Drivers (2026–2028)

**Cyclical headwinds (moderating by 2027):**
- **Employer NIC shock:** The April 2025 NIC rate increase from 13.8% to 15% and threshold reduction to £5,000 directly raised the cost of employment, suppressing hiring and accelerating automation adoption.
- **Wage growth vs. inflation:** UK wage growth has been running at 5–6% YoY against CPI declining toward 2.5–3%. Real wage recovery is positive for consumption but sustains employer pressure to substitute labour.
- **Bank of England policy:** With CPI approaching target, the BoE has room to cut rates through 2026–2027, providing a modest cyclical tailwind. However, rate cuts cannot offset structural displacement.
- **Fiscal drag:** OBR projections indicate continued public sector spending constraint through 2026, limiting government as an employment buffer.

**Structural accelerants (dominant by 2027):**
- **AI cost-capability convergence:** AI inference cost has collapsed to an effective equivalent of **£0.024/hour** by 2026 [C3] — a 600x–1,825x cost advantage over every UK wage tier from hospitality (£11.50/hr) to legal (£35.00/hr) [C3]. This is not approaching a tipping point; it crossed it in 2025.
- **Immigration/labour supply:** Post-Brexit tightening of EU labour flows has reduced the low-wage labour buffer, paradoxically accelerating AI adoption in hospitality, logistics, and food service — the sectors most exposed to automation.

### 6. Upside and Downside Scenarios

**Upside (lower unemployment):** New job creation in AI deployment, maintenance, and orchestration roles absorbs 50%+ of displaced workers (vs. 30% base). Reabsorption rate rises if UK government implements large-scale retraining at pace. December 2026 unemployment: ~7.0–7.5%.

**Downside (higher unemployment):** Employer NIC shock persists longer; BoE cuts delayed by sticky services inflation; AI adoption accelerates faster than 7-month capability doubling (hardware improvements outpace model). December 2026 unemployment: ~9.5–10.5%.
