### 8.1. Co-Located Generation Mandates

**Mechanism:** Require datacenters >100 MW to co-locate with new generation (gas, battery storage) or fund transmission upgrades as condition of interconnection approval.

**Rationale:** Co-located generation bypasses transmission constraints, delivering power directly to datacenter loads without relying on grid infrastructure.

**Capacity Impact:** If 30-50% of datacenter pipeline (10-20 GW) co-locates with generation, this delivers **10-20 GW of incremental capacity** in Northern Virginia without transmission upgrades.

**Implementation Timeline:** 2-3 years (FERC rulemaking + first projects commissioned)

**Cost:** $10-20 billion (datacenter-funded generation)

**Political Feasibility:** **Moderate (60-70%)**. Datacenters oppose mandates that increase interconnection costs, but co-location is increasingly common practice (e.g., AWS co-locating with gas plants in Virginia).

### 8.2. Datacenter Load Caps

**Mechanism:** Establish annual datacenter interconnection caps by zone (e.g., Northern Virginia: 2-3 GW per year) until transmission capacity is delivered.

**Rationale:** Prevents datacenter demand growth from outpacing transmission and generation capacity additions, reducing reliability risk.

**Capacity Impact:** Limits Northern Virginia datacenter demand growth from 23-30 GW (base-case) to 15-20 GW (2-3 GW per year × 7 years), aligning demand with transmission capacity additions (16-30 GW).

**Implementation Timeline:** 1-2 years (FERC rulemaking)

**Cost:** Zero federal cost (administrative only)

**Political Feasibility:** **Low (20-30%)**. Datacenter load caps face severe political opposition from Virginia congressional delegation, hyperscalers (AWS, Microsoft, Google), and economic development advocates. Likely legal challenges under Federal Power Act (FERC authority to limit load growth is contested).

### 8.3. Accelerated Nuclear/Gas Permitting

**Mechanism:** Streamline federal permitting for new nuclear (small modular reactors) and gas combined-cycle plants in Northern Virginia and Maryland.

**Rationale:** Reduces permitting timelines from 5-7 years to 2-3 years, accelerating capacity additions to match datacenter commissioning timelines.

**Capacity Impact:** Accelerated permitting delivers **5-10 GW of incremental capacity** by 2030-2032 (vs. 2033-2035 under current permitting timelines).

**Implementation Timeline:** 1-2 years (executive order or congressional legislation streamlining NEPA, Clean Air Act, and Clean Water Act permitting)

**Cost:** Zero federal cost (administrative only)

**Political Feasibility:** **Moderate (50-60%)**. Accelerated gas permitting has bipartisan support for grid reliability. Accelerated nuclear permitting (SMRs) has Republican support but faces Democratic opposition due to environmental concerns. Likely legal challenges from environmental groups.

### 8.4. Federal Datacenter Efficiency Mandates

**Mechanism:** Require datacenters receiving federal contracts to achieve power usage effectiveness (PUE) <1.2, reducing electricity demand by 10-20% relative to current average PUE of 1.5.

**Rationale:** Demand reduction is faster and cheaper than supply additions. Reducing datacenter electricity demand by 10-20% is equivalent to adding 3-6 GW of capacity.

**Capacity Impact:** If federal datacenter contracts represent 20-30% of PJM datacenter pipeline (7-14 GW), efficiency mandates reduce demand by **1-3 GW**.

**Implementation Timeline:** 1-2 years (GSA/DOD procurement rule revision)

**Cost:** Zero federal cost (datacenters fund efficiency upgrades)

**Political Feasibility:** **High (80-90%)**. Federal government has clear authority to condition procurement contracts on efficiency standards. Bipartisan support for reducing federal energy costs.

## 9. Methodology-Driven Conclusions

**Conclusion 1: Proposed federal policy interventions are insufficient to meet projected datacenter power demand growth in PJM through 2036. Total datacenter demand growth (35-67 GW) exceeds policy-driven capacity additions (25-45 GW) by 10-42 GW, creating a structural shortfall.**

Base-case scenario: 35-47 GW datacenter demand vs. 25-45 GW policy-driven capacity = **0-22 GW shortfall** (midpoint: 10 GW). Accelerated scenario: 47-67 GW datacenter demand vs. 25-45 GW policy-driven capacity = **22-42 GW shortfall** (midpoint: 32 GW). Policy interventions are marginally sufficient in base-case only if all interventions deliver upper-bound capacity (45 GW) and datacenter demand remains at lower bound (35 GW). In accelerated scenario, policy interventions are severely insufficient regardless of capacity delivery.

**Conclusion 2: FERC market design reforms deliver the highest capacity additions per dollar invested (3.75-5.0 GW per $1B for multi-year procurement and long-term contracts), but face 3-5 year implementation timelines that lag datacenter commissioning cycles (18-36 months).**

FERC multi-year capacity procurement, zonal auctions, and long-term contracts deliver 15-25 GW of permanent capacity by 2030-2032 at $2-4 billion cost (3.75-5.0 GW per $1B). However, datacenters entering the interconnection queue in 2026 commission in 2027-2028, creating a 3-5 year timing mismatch. Only emergency procurement (DOE delayed retirements, mobile generation) matches datacenter commissioning timelines, but provides temporary capacity (2027-2029 only).

**Conclusion 3: Transmission constraints in Northern Virginia prevent capacity additions from reaching demand centers even when generation is built. Northern Virginia datacenter demand growth (23-40 GW) exceeds transmission capacity additions (16-30 GW) by 0-24 GW, creating localized load shedding risk (15-25% probability during summer peaks).**

Northern Virginia hosts 60-65% of PJM datacenter demand but has limited transmission capacity from western PJM generation hubs. Policy interventions deliver 16-30 GW of transmission capacity by 2030-2036, but Northern Virginia datacenter demand growth (23-40 GW) exceeds this by 0-24 GW. Western PJM has surplus generation capacity (10-15 GW), but transmission infrastructure cannot deliver this power to Northern Virginia. This creates a capacity-transmission mismatch where PJM-wide generation is adequate, but localized transmission constraints prevent delivery to demand centers.

**Conclusion 4: Emergency procurement (DOE delayed retirements, mobile generation) delivers the fastest capacity additions (5-10 GW per year) and highest capacity per dollar (10-20 GW per $1B), but provides only temporary bridge capacity (2027-2029) and does not address long-term datacenter demand growth (2030-2036).**

DOE emergency delayed retirements deliver 5-10 GW of temporary capacity in 2027-2029 at $500M-1 billion cost (10-20 GW per $1B). DOE emergency mobile generation delivers 2-5 GW of temporary capacity at $500M-1 billion cost (4-10 GW per $1B). These interventions prevent near-term blackouts (2027-2029) but do not address structural capacity shortfalls in 2030-2036 when datacenter demand growth accelerates.

**Conclusion 5: Fiscal mechanisms (ITC expansion, DOE loan guarantees) deliver high capacity per dollar (5-20 GW per $1B) but face 2-5 year implementation timelines and require congressional appropriation, creating political and timing constraints.**

ITC expansion for battery storage delivers 5-10 GW at $1-2 billion cost (5-10 GW per $1B) with 2-year implementation timeline. DOE loan guarantees for transmission deliver 3-5 GW at $250-500M actual cost (6-20 GW per $1B) with 5-year implementation timeline. However, both interventions require congressional appropriation and face budget constraints, political opposition, and timing lags that prevent near-term (2027-2029) capacity delivery.

**Conclusion 6: Additional mechanisms are required to close the 10-42 GW sufficiency gap: (1) co-located generation mandates (10-20 GW), (2) accelerated nuclear/gas permitting (5-10 GW), (3) datacenter efficiency mandates (1-3 GW demand reduction), and (4) datacenter load caps (limits demand growth to align with transmission capacity).**

Co-located generation mandates deliver 10-20 GW by requiring datacenters >100 MW to co-locate with new generation, bypassing transmission constraints. Accelerated nuclear/gas permitting delivers 5-10 GW by reducing permitting timelines from 5-7 years to 2-3 years. Datacenter efficiency mandates reduce demand by 1-3 GW through PUE <1.2 requirements for federal contracts. Datacenter load caps limit Northern Virginia demand growth to 15-20 GW (vs. 23-40 GW unconstrained), aligning demand with transmission capacity additions (16-30 GW). Combined, these mechanisms close the 10-42 GW sufficiency gap.

**Conclusion 7: Policy interventions are too slow to match datacenter deployment velocity. Datacenter commissioning timelines (18-36 months) are faster than most policy intervention deployment timelines (3-7 years), creating a structural timing mismatch where datacenters commission before supporting capacity is available.**

Datacenters entering the interconnection queue in 2026 commission in 2027-2028. FERC market design reforms deliver capacity in 2030-2032 (3-5 year lag). Transmission upgrades deliver capacity in 2032-2034 (5-7 year lag). Only emergency procurement (2026-2028) matches datacenter commissioning timelines. This timing mismatch creates near-term (2027-2030) reliability risks where datacenter demand growth (10-20 GW) exceeds available capacity additions (7-15 GW from emergency procurement), requiring emergency procedures (demand response, voltage reductions, localized load shedding).

**Conclusion 8: Northern Virginia faces the most severe capacity shortfall due to geographic concentration of datacenter demand (60-65% of PJM total) and transmission constraints. Even with all policy interventions, Northern Virginia capacity additions (10-20 GW) fall short of demand growth (23-40 GW) by 13-30 GW.**

Northern Virginia datacenter demand growth (23-40 GW) is driven by fiber infrastructure proximity, federal government cloud migration, and hyperscaler expansion (AWS, Microsoft, Google). Policy interventions deliver 10-20 GW of Northern Virginia-specific capacity (zonal capacity auctions: 5-10 GW co-located generation; transmission upgrades: 5-10 GW HVDC). This creates a 13-30 GW shortfall, requiring additional mechanisms (co-located generation mandates, datacenter load caps) to prevent localized blackouts.
