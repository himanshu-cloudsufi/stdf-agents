**Displacement vs. Official Unemployment: The Measurement Gap**

The 14.4 million FTE displacement figure does NOT directly translate to 14.4 million unemployed workers [C7]. Three structural factors create a measurement gap between technological displacement and official unemployment statistics:

1. **Productivity Absorption**: 35-40% of displaced tasks are absorbed as productivity gains within existing roles rather than full job elimination [C8]. Workers retain employment but perform higher-value tasks while AI handles routine subtasks.

2. **Sectoral Reallocation**: 20-25% of displaced workers transition to adjacent roles or sectors experiencing AI-driven demand growth (AI training/supervision, healthcare expansion, infrastructure) [C9]. These transitions occur with wage compression but not unemployment.

3. **Labor Force Exit**: 15-20% of displaced workers exit the labor force entirely (early retirement, caregiving, education), removing them from unemployment statistics while still representing economic displacement [C10].

Applying these factors: 14.4M displaced × (1 - 0.375 productivity - 0.225 reallocation - 0.175 exit) = **3.2 million net unemployed attributable to AI displacement by mid-2027** [C11]. This represents a 1.9 percentage point addition to the unemployment rate (assuming 168M labor force) [C11].

As of January 2026, the official US unemployment rate stands at 4.0% [C12]. The AI-attributable component is embedded within this figure but not separately reported by BLS methodology. The model projects unemployment rising to 5.9% by June 2027 if AI displacement continues at baseline pace without offsetting job creation [C13].
