The 20x cost advantage threshold ($15,000) arrives by 2032, accelerating adoption into logistics, warehousing, and light manufacturing [W2][W5].

**Component Cost Dynamics**

Four primary components drive the cost curve, each following distinct learning rates validated by historical data:

**Actuators** (35% of 2025 cost): Decline from $13,500 (2026) to $76 (2046) at 22% annual reduction. Actuators represent the largest cost component and exhibit the steepest learning curve due to novel manufacturing techniques and materials science advances [W1][W6]. Current pricing reflects low-volume specialty production; transition to automotive-scale manufacturing (100,000+ units annually) drives exponential cost collapse [W1][W2].

**Compute** (12% of 2025 cost): Decline from $4,500 (2026) to $11 (2046) at 25% annual reduction. This trajectory mirrors GPU performance-per-dollar improvements, which increased 73x from 2008-2022 (26.4% annually) [Dataset: Artificial_Intelligence_Max_GPU_Computational_Performance_Per_Dollar_Global]. AI inference chips benefit from smartphone and datacenter supply chain economies, with costs approaching near-zero as compute becomes commoditized [W1][W10].

**Sensors** (12% of 2025 cost): Decline from $4,900 (2026) to $74 (2046) at 18% annual reduction. Sensor suites (cameras, LiDAR, IMUs, force sensors) leverage smartphone supply chain scale, where unit costs have declined 15-20% annually for a decade [W1][W6][W10]. Vision systems converge toward automotive-grade pricing as production volumes reach millions of units [W2][W5].

**Batteries** (8% of 2025 cost): Decline from $3,400 (2026) to $105 (2046) at 15% annual reduction. Lithium-ion pack costs fell from $1,436/kWh (2010) to $115/kWh (2024), a 16.8% annual decline [Dataset: Lithium_Ion_Battery_Pack_Median_Cost_Global]. Humanoid robots require 20-30 kWh packs; at $50/kWh (projected 2035), battery costs drop below $1,500 per unit [W1][W6].

**Rupture Point Economics**

The primary rupture occurs when total cost of ownership (TCO) for humanoid robots falls below human labor TCO. At 50% utilization (12 hours/day, 5 days/week), a humanoid robot operating 15,000 hours over 10 years competes with $225,000 in human wages. The 2030 cost of $19,000 delivers 12x cost advantage, triggering mass adoption in structured environments (warehouses, factories, retail) [W2][W5][W7].

Secondary rupture points emerge as costs decline further:

- **$10,000 (2034)**: Consumer market opens; household robots become economically viable for cleaning, elder care, and personal assistance [W2][W8].
- **$5,000 (2038)**: Commodity pricing enables ubiquitous deployment; robots outnumber humans in commercial settings [W5][W7].
- **$1,600 (2046)**: Near-zero marginal cost when combined with Stellar energy; robots become infrastructure rather than capital goods.

**Manufacturing Scale Effects**

Production volume amplifies learning curve effects through three mechanisms:

1. **Supply chain maturation**: Transition from specialty robotics suppliers to automotive-tier suppliers reduces component costs 30-50% [W1][W6].
2. **Automation of assembly**: Robot assembly lines (robots building robots) reduce labor content from 15% to <5% of total cost [W1][W9].
3. **Design optimization**: Cumulative production enables iterative design improvements, reducing material usage and part count [W1][W10].

Industry projections indicate production scaling from 10,000 units (2025) to 100,000 units (2030) to 1 million units (2035) to 10 million units (2045) [W2][W5][W7]. This 1,000x volume increase over 20 years drives an additional 15-20% cost reduction beyond component learning curves [W1][W2].
