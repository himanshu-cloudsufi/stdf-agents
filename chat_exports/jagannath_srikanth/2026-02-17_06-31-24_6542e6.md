## 1. Mathematical/Statistical Methods

### Tipping Point Detection

For each segment, the model detects the year when the disruptor achieves cost/price parity with the incumbent:

**B2C (Consumer) segments** (passenger cars, two-wheelers, three-wheelers):
- Compare **vehicle prices**: BEV_Price ≤ ICE_Price
- Apply 3-year rolling median smoothing to historical price data
- Tipping_Year = first year where smoothed BEV price ≤ smoothed ICE price

**B2B (Business) segments** (commercial vehicles, forklifts, UPS):
- Compare **total cost of ownership (TCO)**: EV_Cost ≤ ICE_Cost
- Include upfront cost + operating cost + maintenance cost
- Tipping_Year = first year where EV TCO ≤ ICE TCO

**Detected tipping points** (from the run):
- **China passenger cars**: 2022 (price parity detected)
- **USA passenger cars**: 2026 (fallback, parity not yet crossed in historical data)
- **Europe passenger cars**: 2024 (price parity detected)
- **Rest of World passenger cars**: 2028 (fallback, parity not yet crossed)
- **Commercial vehicles**: 2024-2027 (cost parity by region)
- **Forklifts**: 2015-2023 (cost parity by region, already past tipping)

### S-Curve Adoption Dynamics

Post-tipping, adoption follows a logistic S-curve:

```
share_EV(t) = L / (1 + exp(-k × (t - t₀)))
```

Where:
- **L** = ceiling (0.95-1.0 for full adoption)
- **k** = steepness parameter (fitted to historical data)
- **t₀** = inflection point (midpoint of S-curve)

**Fitting method**:
1. **Primary**: Fit k and t₀ to historical adoption data using weighted least squares
2. **Fallback**: If insufficient data, use heuristic (10% to 80% adoption over 10 years post-tipping)

**Fitted parameters** (from the run):
- **China passenger cars**: k=0.3355, t₀=2026.5 (fitted_weighted)
- **USA passenger cars**: k=0.3200, t₀=2030.8 (fitted_weighted)
- **Europe passenger cars**: k=0.2902, t₀=2028.6 (fitted_weighted)
- **Rest of World passenger cars**: k=0.4970, t₀=2029.8 (fitted_weighted)

The S-curve captures the acceleration phase (10% → 80% over ~10-15 years) that occurs after tipping points trigger market-driven adoption.

### Stock-Flow Model (OEM vs Replacement Demand)

For each vehicle segment, the model tracks fleet evolution:

```
Fleet(t+1) = Fleet(t) + Sales(t) - Scrappage(t)
Scrappage(t) = Fleet(t) / Vehicle_Lifetime
```

**Lead demand** is calculated from the fleet:

```
OEM_Lead(t) = ICE_Sales(t) × Lead_per_Vehicle × LAB_Share / 1,000,000 → kt
Replacement_Lead(t) = ICE_Fleet(t) × Replacement_Rate × Lead_per_Vehicle × LAB_Share / 1,000,000 → kt
Total_Lead(t) = OEM_Lead(t) + Replacement_Lead(t)
```

Where:
- **Lead_per_Vehicle**: 11 kg (passenger cars), 21 kg (commercial vehicles)
- **Replacement_Rate**: 25%/year (4-year battery life for SLI batteries)
- **LAB_Share**: 1 - Li_ion_Share (from auxiliary battery analysis)
- **Vehicle_Lifetime**: 15 years (passenger cars), 20 years (commercial vehicles)

This creates a **two-channel collapse**:
1. **OEM demand** declines immediately as EV sales displace ICE sales
2. **Replacement demand** declines with a 4-year lag as the ICE fleet shrinks through scrappage

## 5. Stock-Flow Model for OEM vs Replacement Demand

The model tracks **two demand channels** separately:

Where:
- **ICE_Fleet(t)** = Fleet(t-1) + ICE_Sales(t) - Scrappage(t)
