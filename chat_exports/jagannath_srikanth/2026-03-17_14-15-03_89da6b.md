# Data Center Optical Interconnect: Scenario-Based Winner/Loser Analysis (2025–2035)

## Thesis

The data center optical interconnect market is undergoing a phase change driven by AI compute scaling at 12% CAGR datacenter electricity demand growth [C1]. Every GPU generation forces 100% replacement of the optical interconnect layer — this is not discretionary IT spending but a mandatory infrastructure upgrade cycle [C2]. The service unit is **cost per Gbps transported per watt consumed** ($/Gbps/W). The technology that minimizes this metric at each bandwidth tier (800G → 1.6T → 3.2T → 6.4T) captures the market. Cost-curve dynamics favor silicon photonics and co-packaged optics over EML pluggables by 2028, with the tipping point arriving at the 1.6T generation [C3].

Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded unless specifically requested.

## Scenario 1: Silicon Photonics Dominance

**Cost-curve dynamics:** SiPh fabricates optical components on standard CMOS lines. At a ~20% learning rate (consistent with semiconductor historical data), each cumulative doubling of production reduces cost per Gbps by ~20%. From 2025 to 2030, cumulative volume doubles 4–5x, implying **60–70% cost reduction** in the SiPh stack [C9]. This mirrors the cost-curve dynamics that drove solar PV from $76/W to $0.20/W [C10].

**Tipping point:** 2027–2028 at the 1.6T generation, when SiPh cost per Gbps crosses below EML [C6].

**Key signal:** Marvell [[COMPANY:CO1]] acquired Celestial AI for $5.5B (December 2025) — the clearest market signal that SiPh IP commands premium valuations [C11]. OFC 2026 showing 1.6T SiPh products from multiple vendors confirms technology is exiting the lab [C12]. The 400G-per-lane race at OFC 2026 pits EML, SiPh, and thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) directly against each other [C13].

**Yield risk:** SiPh thermal drift and coupling loss keep yields below 85% vs >95% for standard CMOS — this is the primary delay risk [C14].

## Technology Architecture Resolution

The cost-curve dynamics resolve the three competing hypotheses:

1. **EML pluggables dominate 2025–2027** — this is the current state, not a prediction. EML captures the 800G deployment wave. But EML hits a physics wall at 1.6T+ (power, signal integrity). Peak EML is 2026–2027.

2. **Silicon photonics reaches tipping point 2027–2028** — CMOS manufacturing learning curves drive 60–70% cost reduction by 2030. SiPh becomes the dominant optical engine technology for 1.6T and 3.2T transceivers.

3. **CPO becomes dominant architecture 2030–2032** — the 30–40% power reduction per port is decisive at 3.2T+ bandwidths. CPO integrates SiPh engines into ASIC packages, collapsing the distinction between "networking equipment" and "optical equipment."

The resolution: **These are not competing outcomes — they are sequential phases of the same disruption arc.** EML → SiPh → CPO is the temporal sequence. The investment question is not "which technology wins" but "when does each phase change occur" and "which companies are positioned across multiple phases."

| Near-term | 2025–2027 | EML pluggable (800G) | Lowest $/Gbps at 800G |
| Mid-term | 2027–2030 | SiPh + LPO (1.6T) | Lowest $/Gbps/W at 1.6T |
