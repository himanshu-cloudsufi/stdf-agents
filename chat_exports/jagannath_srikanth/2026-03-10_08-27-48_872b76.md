The binding constraint on displacement is now AI capability (task duration reliably completable), not economics.

### Second-Order Impacts & Policy Feedback Loops

**Consumer Demand Contraction**
The 21.5M net job loss generates **$840B/year in aggregate demand destruction** by 2030 [C14]. At a 1.5x GDP multiplier, the total GDP drag reaches **$1.26 trillion/year** [C15]. Consumer behavior bifurcates: displaced workers collapse discretionary spending; augmented workers increase precautionary savings. The uniform displacement across skill tiers destroys the professional middle class consumption base — the engine of 70% of U.S. GDP [C16].

**Tax Base Erosion**
Payroll and income tax base erosion reaches **$424B/year** by 2030 [C17]. Simultaneously, social safety net costs expand by **$163B/year** (expanded UI, Medicaid, SNAP) [C18]. The structural fiscal gap: **$587B/year** — and growing, as the government's revenue base erodes faster than spending can be cut [C19].

**Corporate Labor-to-Capital Transfer**
Corporations capture **$936B/year in labor cost savings** by 2030 (21.5M FTE × $21,728/FTE cost advantage) [C20]. This is a 3.1% of GDP transfer from labor income to corporate capital — the largest labor-to-capital income shift in U.S. history [C21]. These savings do not automatically recirculate as consumer demand, creating the net GDP drag identified above.

**Social Safety Net Stress**
The Q1 2028 peak displacement rate of **2.6 million FTE per quarter** (10.4M annualized) overwhelms the absorption capacity of existing UI and retraining systems [C22]. UI was designed for cyclical unemployment; structural displacement at this velocity has no institutional precedent.

### Policy Reaction Modeling

**Government (Strength: 0.65 | Lag: 6–18 months)**
The government follows the predictable incumbent response pattern: denial through 2026, reactive escalation as displacement becomes undeniable by 2027–2028. Expected policy sequence [C23]:
- WIOA expansion and community college AI upskilling mandates (2027)
- AI attribution tracking legislation (Sen. Hawley bill, Dec 2025 leading edge)
- Automation tax / AI payroll equivalent tax proposals (2028)
- UBI pilot programs in high-displacement metros (2028–2029)
- Emergency safety net expansion (2028–2030)

The critical constraint: the fiscal gap is $587B/year, but the government's own tax base is simultaneously eroding. Policy lags disruption by 6–18 months [C24].

**Federal Reserve (Strength: 0.45 | Lag: 3–12 months)**
The Fed faces a structural dilemma with no historical precedent: AI-driven displacement creates deflationary pressure (labor costs collapse, goods prices fall) simultaneously with demand contraction. Rate cuts stimulate demand but cannot address structural unemployment. The Fed cannot solve structural unemployment with monetary tools alone [C25].

**Corporations (Strength: 0.85 | Lag: 3–12 months)**
Corporations are the primary beneficiaries, capturing $936B/year in savings. The corporate response: accelerated AI capex substituting for labor, workforce restructuring toward augmented roles, and lobbying against automation taxes. The bifurcation between AI-native firms and laggard firms creates a winner-take-most dynamic [C26].

### Key Feedback Loops

**Accelerator R1 — AI Cost-Curve Loop (Strength: 0.90)**
AI cost falls → corporate adoption accelerates → AI revenue grows → more R&D → AI capability improves → AI cost falls further. This is the primary driver. Strength 0.90 means it overwhelms all brake loops in the 2026–2030 window [C27].

**Accelerator R3 — Demand Destruction → Deflationary Spiral (Strength: 0.65)**
AI displaces workers → income falls → consumer demand contracts → consumer-facing firms lose revenue → firms cut more labor → more displacement. The 1.5x multiplier amplifies each iteration [C28].

**Accelerator R5 — Tax Base Erosion → Safety Net Underfunding → Social Instability (Strength: 0.60)**
Displacement → tax base erodes → safety net underfunded → social instability → investment declines → growth slows → more displacement. This is the most dangerous second-order loop — self-reinforcing on a multi-year lag [C29].

**Brake B1 — Government Fiscal Intervention (Strength: 0.50)**
Emergency fiscal programs partially restore consumer demand. Real but structurally limited by the eroding tax base [C30].

**Brake B4 — AI ROI Failure (Strength: 0.40)**
The finding that 95% of enterprises generate zero return on AI investment as of early 2026 suggests significant misallocation. If AI productivity gains fail to materialize at scale, corporate adoption slows [C31].
