**Sanctions Escalation (November 2025 – February 2026)**

The US executed at least four distinct sanctions rounds against Iran's oil trade infrastructure in rapid succession [W1][W2][W3][W4][W5]:
- November 25, 2025: State Dept sanctioned entities trading Iranian petroleum [W5]
- December 18, 2025: Treasury targeted Iran's sanctions-evading shadow fleet [W4]

- February 10, 2026: State Dept targeted illicit Iranian oil traders and shadow fleet [W2]
- February 26, 2026: Treasury broadened action to include ballistic missile supply networks alongside shadow fleet [W1]

This cadence — one major enforcement action every 3–4 weeks — represents a structural, not episodic, pressure campaign. Shadow fleet disruption directly constrains Iran's ability to export discounted crude to China, which had been absorbing ~1.3–1.5 mb/d of Iranian barrels under the prior sanctions-lite environment.

**Military Escalation (Early March 2026)**

Multiple sources reference US strikes against Iran and a documented "2026 Iran war" with active economic impact analysis [W6][W10]. CNBC coverage from March 2, 2026 specifically addresses Chinese energy security implications of US strikes on Iran [W10], and Carnegie Endowment analysis from March 5, 2026 documents that US actions against Iran are redirecting oil flows — with Chinese buyers shifting away from sanctioned Iranian barrels toward Russian crude, boosting Russian export revenues [W9].

This is a critical supply-side development: Iranian barrels that were previously flowing to China (~1.3–1.5 mb/d) are being displaced. Even partial displacement of 0.5–0.8 mb/d represents a meaningful tightening in a market where OPEC+ spare capacity is the primary buffer.

**Market Reaction (March 11, 2026)**

CNBC reported crude prices closing higher on March 11 as markets weighed tanker threats against a potential IEA emergency stockpile release [W1]. The IEA release signal is itself a confirmation that supply tightness is real and recognized by institutional actors — IEA releases are not triggered by comfortable supply conditions.

**Supply Disruption Scenario (cost-curve logic, not scenario hedging):**

The structural logic is straightforward:
1. Shadow fleet disruption reduces Iranian export capacity by 0.5–1.0 mb/d
2. Chinese buyers shift to Russian crude (Carnegie analysis [W9]), tightening the non-sanctioned supply pool
3. OPEC+ spare capacity exists but requires a political decision to deploy — Saudi Arabia has incentive to allow prices to firm before releasing barrels
4. Strait of Hormuz risk premium is active (tanker threats cited [W1]) — even without physical closure, insurance and routing costs elevate effective crude prices
5. IEA stockpile release, if executed, provides a 1–3 month buffer but does not resolve the structural disruption
