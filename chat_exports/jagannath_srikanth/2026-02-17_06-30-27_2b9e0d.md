Global lead demand peaked in 2018 at 6,866 kt and is now in terminal decline, driven by lithium-ion displacement across all battery applications. The trajectory shows three distinct phases: gradual erosion (2024-2030), accelerated collapse (2031-2035), and sustained decline (2035-2040). By 2040, demand falls to 1,317 kt—an 81% reduction from peak.

**Peak Year and Magnitude**

Lead demand for battery applications reached its maximum in 2018 at 6,866 kt. Current demand (2024) stands at 6,693 kt, just 2.5% below peak, masking the imminent disruption. The peak reflects maximum ICE vehicle fleet size before electrification and the dominance of lead-acid across all battery segments.

The 2031 inflection marks the rupture point where lithium-ion SLI batteries reach cost parity in China and Rest of World (representing 70% of global demand). Annual decline accelerates from -4.6% (2030) to -14.7% (2031) as S-curve adoption triggers across multiple segments simultaneously.

**Displacement Mechanisms by Segment**

**1. SLI (ICE Cars) – 51.9% of 2024 demand**

Lithium-ion SLI batteries displace lead-acid in ICE vehicles. Tipping point: 2031 (cost parity). Li-ion share rises from 2.8% (2024) to 93.3% (2040). Each ICE vehicle contains 11.0 kg of lead in its SLI battery. As Li-ion adoption follows the S-curve (10% → 80% over 10 years post-tipping), this segment collapses from 3,157 kt (2024) to 61 kt (2040)—a 98% decline.

**2. SLI (Commercial Vehicles) – 28.1% of 2024 demand**

Dual displacement: (a) EV adoption eliminates SLI batteries entirely (EVs use Li-ion auxiliary), and (b) remaining ICE/NGV vehicles transition to Li-ion SLI. Commercial EVs reach 95% share by 2040. ICE/NGV vehicles carry 21.13 kg of lead per SLI battery. Demand falls from 1,712 kt (2024) to 62 kt (2040)—a 96% decline.

**3. Forklift Batteries – 12.2% of 2024 demand**

Lithium-ion forklift batteries displace lead-acid. Tipping points already passed (2015-2022 by region). Li-ion share rises from ~30-60% (2024, varies by region) to 95% (2040). Demand declines from 744 kt (2024) to 469 kt (2040)—a 37% decline. This segment becomes the largest lead consumer by 2040 (50.8% of total) as other segments collapse faster.

**4. EV Auxiliary (LAB) – 3.1% of 2024 demand**

BEV/PHEV 12V auxiliary batteries initially use lead-acid, then transition to lithium-ion. This segment shows temporary growth to 500 kt (2030) as EV fleet expands, then collapses as Li-ion auxiliary adoption accelerates (2.8% in 2024 → 95% in 2040). Demand falls to 65 kt (2040). This is a chimera segment: grows during EV transition, then declines as Li-ion displaces lead-acid within EVs.

**5. E2W Traction Batteries – 4.7% of 2024 demand**

Two-stage disruption: (a) ICE → Electric two-wheelers (EV share reaches 75% by 2040), and (b) within electric, LAB → Li-ion traction batteries (Li-ion share reaches 95% by 2040). Net effect: lead-acid traction demand declines from 287 kt (2024) to 266 kt (2040)—modest 7% decline because electrification partially offsets Li-ion chemistry shift in the near term.

**Methodology**

Bottom-up vehicle-count model using lithium-ion-demand skill. Each segment calculates:

- **OEM demand**: New vehicle sales × lead content × (1 - Li-ion share)
- **Replacement demand**: Fleet size × replacement rate × lead content × (1 - Li-ion share)
- **Tipping points**: Cost parity (B2B) or price parity (B2C) detection from historical data
- **S-curve adoption**: Logistic fit to historical Li-ion penetration, fallback to 10% → 80% over 10 years
- **EV transition**: Separate S-curves for powertrain shift (ICE → BEV/PHEV)

Lead content assumptions: ICE cars 11.0 kg, commercial vehicles 21.13 kg, forklift 40 kWh equivalent, E2W/E3W traction 2.5-8.0 kg. Replacement rates: 25%/year (4-year battery life) for SLI, 14%/year (7-year life) for forklifts.

**2031 Inflection Point**

The 2031 rupture is the convergence of three tipping points:

1. **Li-ion SLI cost parity** in China and Rest of World (70% of global demand)
2. **S-curve acceleration** as adoption crosses 10% threshold
3. **EV fleet reaching critical mass** (China BEV share 55%, USA 35%, Europe 45%)

This triggers positive feedback: declining lead-acid production volumes → rising unit costs → accelerated customer defection → further volume loss. Annual decline jumps from -4.6% (2030) to -14.7% (2031) and sustains at -13% to -16% through 2037.

**Conclusion**

Global lead demand peaked in 2018 at 6,866 kt. The 2031 inflection point marks the transition from gradual decline to terminal collapse, driven by lithium-ion cost parity across SLI batteries and accelerating EV adoption. By 2036, demand falls to 2,233 kt (-67.5% from peak). By 2040, demand reaches 1,317 kt (-80.8% from peak), with forklift batteries becoming the dominant remaining application (51% of total) as automotive and stationary segments approach near-zero. The trajectory is driven by cost-curve dynamics, not policy or ideology: lithium-ion batteries achieve superior cost per service unit ($/cycle, $/year) across all applications, making displacement inevitable.
