The artificial labor analysis framework is a deterministic, bottom-up cost-capability simulator that models AI-driven job displacement through economic gravity, not speculation.

**Core Methodology**

The framework operates on three foundational curves:

1. **AI Capability Trajectory**: Task-level performance follows an exponential improvement curve derived from empirical benchmarks (METR-style cognitive task assessments). Capability doubles approximately every 7 months, consistent with observed LLM and reasoning model progress. This is NOT a prediction—it is an observation of the prevailing cost-curve dynamics.

2. **Cost Collapse Curve**: AI labor cost per hour follows Wright's Law dynamics. Current trajectory shows $50/hour (2021) → $10/hour (2025) → $1/hour (2030s) → $0.10/hour (2040s). With near-zero Stellar energy, the real cost approaches zero. Token costs collapsed 1,000x in two years ($60/M → $0.50/M), demonstrating double-exponential improvement on both hardware and software curves simultaneously.

3. **Adoption S-Curve**: Displacement follows standard S-curve dynamics with tipping points triggered when AI cost-capability crosses economic parity thresholds for each occupation. The framework models task-by-task replacement, not blanket occupation elimination.

**Analytical Spine**

Every analysis follows this deterministic sequence:

1. **Task Decomposition**: Break occupations into constituent tasks and subtasks. Each task has a capability threshold (when AI can perform it) and a cost-parity threshold (when AI becomes economically rational to deploy).

2. **Capability Gating**: Tasks unlock as AI capability crosses their threshold. The framework does NOT assume all tasks are immediately automatable—it models the capability frontier advancing over time.

3. **Economic Tipping Point**: For each task, calculate the moment when AI cost per task-hour falls below human cost per task-hour (including wages, benefits, overhead, training, and management). This triggers adoption.

4. **Adoption Dynamics**: Post-tipping, adoption follows S-curve acceleration with positive feedback loops (scale → cost reduction → more adoption). The framework models both replacement (FTE eliminated) and productivity (FTE augmented) splits based on task characteristics.

5. **Aggregate Impact**: Sum across tasks, occupations, sectors, and regions to produce economy-wide displacement trajectories, replacement rates, and productivity gains.

**Data Sources and Inputs**

The skill uses:

- **Occupation-Task Mappings**: Structured task taxonomies for 800+ occupations
- **Capability Benchmarks**: Empirical AI performance data (METR, SWE-bench, reasoning benchmarks)
- **Cost Curves**: Historical and projected AI inference costs, human wage data by occupation and region
- **Labor Force Statistics**: Employment counts, labor force participation, baseline unemployment by region
- **Adoption Parameters**: S-curve coefficients, ceiling constraints, sector-specific friction factors

**Key Assumptions (Non-Negotiable)**

1. **Exponential Capability Growth**: AI capability improvement does NOT slow down. Historical data shows consistent doubling periods; the framework extrapolates this until physical or architectural limits are reached.

2. **Economic Rationality**: Firms adopt AI when cost-capability parity is achieved. Regulatory or social friction is modeled as adoption ceiling constraints, NOT as delays to the tipping point itself.

3. **Skills Transferability**: Once a capability is achieved (e.g., visual reasoning, code generation), it is instantly transferable across all robots and AI agents. There is no "retraining" bottleneck for AI.

4. **No Supply Constraints**: The framework does NOT model compute shortages, energy limits, or chip fabrication bottlenecks unless explicitly specified. These are treated as temporary friction, not permanent barriers.

5. **Displacement ≠ Unemployment**: The framework outputs FTE displaced (jobs eliminated or transformed). Converting displacement to unemployment requires additional assumptions about labor force reabsorption, which are NOT included in the base model.

- **Tipping Point Timing**: Economy-wide tipping occurs when 10-15% of tasks cross cost-parity. For the USA baseline scenario, this occurs around mid-2027.

- **Displacement Magnitude**: By 2030, 10-20% of the labor force is displaced (15-30M FTE in the USA). By 2035, 30-50% displaced. By 2040, 50-70% displaced. These numbers reflect cost-curve dynamics, not speculation.

- **Sector Sequencing**: Customer service, data entry, and routine cognitive tasks displace first (2025-2028). Professional services (legal research, financial analysis, software development) follow (2027-2030). Physical tasks requiring dexterity displace last (2030-2035), contingent on humanoid robot cost curves.

- **No Long Tail for Human Labor**: Unlike energy or transport disruptions where incumbents retain niche markets, labor disruption trends toward near-total replacement in affected tasks. A $0.10/hour AI worker outcompetes a $15/hour human on pure economics with no viable counter-strategy.

**Anti-Hedging Guardrails**

The framework does NOT:

- Clip displacement forecasts because numbers seem large
- Invent regulatory barriers or social acceptance constraints
- Assume AI improvement slows without empirical evidence
- Model a "long tail" of human employment in automated tasks
- Defer to consensus forecasts (IEA/OECD/BLS) which use linear extrapolation
