## Policy Reaction Analysis: Feedback Loops

### Accelerator Loops (Reinforce Displacement)

**R1 — AI Cost-Curve Loop** (Strength: 0.92): AI adoption → revenue → R&D investment → capability ↑ → cost ↓ → more adoption. LLM costs fell ~1,000x in 24 months [C6]. Each deployment doubling drives further cost reduction. Time lag: 3–6 months per cycle.

**R2 — Corporate Profit Margin Loop** (Strength: 0.85): AI replaces labor → labor costs ↓ → profit margins ↑ → AI capex ↑ → more displacement. Corporations adopting AI first gain competitive cost advantages, forcing competitors to adopt or lose market share [C6]. Time lag: 6–12 months.

**R3 — Capital Reallocation Loop** (Strength: 0.78): AI sector outperforms → capital flows to AI → faster AI development → AI outperforms more. Markets reprice labor-intensive sectors downward, freeing capital for AI infrastructure [C6]. Time lag: 0–3 months.

**R5 — Incumbent Labor Death Spiral** (Strength: 0.70): AI displaces workers → labor income ↓ → consumer spending ↓ → business revenues ↓ → businesses cut more labor to maintain margins → more displacement [C6]. Time lag: 12–24 months.

### Brake Loops (Resist Displacement)

**B1 — Regulatory Intervention** (Strength: 0.35→0.60 by 2029): Political pressure → AI labor regulation → deployment slowed. The AI Workforce PREPARE Act (S.3339, introduced December 2025) is the first legislative signal [C7]. Full brake requires bipartisan consensus — currently absent. Activation: 18–36 months lag.

**B2 — Retraining Programs** (Strength: 0.25): Government retraining → displaced workers reskilled → labor supply in new sectors. Critical constraint: historical precedent (Trade Adjustment Assistance) shows retraining programs reach <30% of eligible workers and achieve <50% re-employment rates in comparable-wage jobs [C7]. Activation: 24–48 months lag.

**B3 — AI Labor Taxation** (Strength: 0.20→0.45 by 2029): Robot/AI tax proposals → AI deployment cost ↑ → adoption rate ↓. No federal AI labor tax has advanced as of early 2026 [C7]. US political environment (Trump AI Action Plan prioritizing competitiveness) makes near-term federal robot tax unlikely before 2028. State-level experiments possible by 2027. Activation: 24–42 months lag.

**B4 — Labor Union Resistance** (Strength: 0.30): Union mobilization → collective bargaining AI restrictions → corporate AI deployment slowed in unionized sectors. AFL-CIO established dedicated AI policy focus; UAW incorporated AI concerns into bargaining; ~500,000 workers unionized in 2025 [C7]. Union coverage is ~10% of US workforce — brake is sector-specific, not economy-wide. Activation: 12–24 months.

**Net assessment**: Accelerator loops (combined strength ~3.25) dominate brake loops (combined strength ~1.10) through 2028. Brakes strengthen to ~1.80 by 2029–2030 but cannot reverse the economic direction — only delay displacement by 15–25% in the base case [C6].

## Three Policy Scenarios (2026–2031)

### Scenario 1 — No Meaningful Policy Reaction (Probability: 10%)
Accelerator loops operate without brake. 46.6M FTE displaced by 2031 as projected. Structural unemployment reaches 20–25% by 2031. Consumer spending destruction triggers a demand-side recession by 2028–2029. Political instability forces emergency intervention by 2029 [C6].

### Scenario 2 — Moderate Reaction (Base Case, Probability: 60%)
Government deploys retraining programs (underperforming by 40–60%), extends unemployment benefits, passes limited AI workforce legislation by 2027–2028. Fed cuts rates 150–200bps by 2028. Labor unions secure AI deployment restrictions in 15–20% of unionized sectors. State-level UBI pilots launch in 2–3 states by 2029. Net outcome: **35–40M FTE displaced by 2031** (brakes reduce displacement by 15–25%). Structural unemployment peaks at 14–18% by 2030–2031 [C6].

### Scenario 3 — Strong Policy Reaction (Probability: 30%)
Federal AI labor tax enacted by 2028 (0.5–1.5% levy on AI-substituted labor costs), UBI pilot expands to federal level by 2030 ($500–$1,000/month), mandatory AI impact assessments required for large-scale deployments, union coverage expands to 20%+. Net outcome: **25–30M FTE displaced by 2031** (brakes reduce displacement by 35–45%). Structural unemployment peaks at 10–13% [C6]. Requires political consensus not currently present but achievable if unemployment crosses 15% before 2030.
