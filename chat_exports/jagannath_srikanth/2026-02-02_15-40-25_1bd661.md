Jevons Paradox describes the counterintuitive phenomenon where efficiency improvements that lower the cost of a service drive such large increases in demand that total resource consumption rises rather than falls. This is not a theoretical curiosity—it is the dominant pattern in every major energy disruption in history and the central dynamic driving the Stellar transformation.

**Historical Context**

William Stanley Jevons observed in 1865 that James Watt's steam engine improvements made coal-powered work dramatically cheaper per unit of output. Rather than reducing coal consumption, Britain's coal use exploded because the cost collapse unlocked entirely new applications: factories, railways, ships, and urban heating. The efficiency gain did not reduce demand—it created new markets [C1].

The same pattern repeated with electricity (1880s-1920s), automobiles (1900s-1930s), and computing (1970s-present). Each time, a 10x-100x cost improvement triggered 100x-1000x market expansion as the technology moved from niche luxury to ubiquitous infrastructure [C2].

**Mechanism: Cost Collapse → Demand Explosion**

Jevons Paradox operates through three reinforcing dynamics:

1. **Substitution**: Cheaper energy displaces expensive alternatives (e.g., EVs displace ICE vehicles as electricity becomes cheaper than gasoline per mile).

2. **Market expansion**: Cost collapse enables entirely new use cases previously uneconomical (e.g., desalination, carbon removal, vertical farming, AI training become viable at <$0.01/kWh).

3. **Rebound effect**: Lower operating costs increase utilization rates (e.g., autonomous EVs operating 10x more hours per day than human-driven cars).

The result: total energy consumption grows even as energy cost per service unit falls 10x-100x [C3].

**Contemporary Application: The SWB Superpower**

Solar-wind-battery (SWB) systems built for peak winter demand create 3-5x overcapacity during the rest of the year, producing electricity at near-zero marginal cost. This "superpower" triggers Jevons Paradox at civilizational scale [C4].

Applications unlocked by superabundant energy:
- **Artificial labor**: AI compute and robotics manufacturing (energy-intensive processes become economical)
- **Desalination**: 1 billion people gain access to fresh water
- **Carbon removal**: Direct air capture becomes cost-effective at scale
- **Materials processing**: Energy-intensive industries (steel, aluminum, cement) electrify
- **Vertical farming**: Food production decouples from arable land

Each application was previously constrained by energy cost. As SWB drives electricity toward $0.01-0.02/kWh, these markets expand from niche to dominant [C5].

**Why Efficiency Projections Fail**

Mainstream forecasts (IEA, EIA) assume fixed demand and model efficiency as a reduction lever. This violates 200 years of empirical evidence. Jevons Paradox is not an edge case—it is the rule. Cost-curve disruptions do not reduce consumption; they explode addressable markets and create new demand categories that did not previously exist [C6].

The Stellar economy is not about using less energy more efficiently. It is about producing so much energy so cheaply that entirely new civilizational capabilities become economically viable. Jevons Paradox is the engine of transformation, not a barrier to it.
