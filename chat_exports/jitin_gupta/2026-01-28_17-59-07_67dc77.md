The premise contains a critical misunderstanding: **US citizens already possess the legal right to generate and store electric power**. Federal law (PURPA 1978, updated 2020) and state-level net metering frameworks guarantee this right in most jurisdictions. The real constraint is not legal permission but **economic viability and interconnection friction**.

The meaningful policy shift would be **removing interconnection barriers, streamlining permitting, and mandating fair compensation for distributed energy**. Under that interpretation, here's what the cost-curve dynamics dictate:

## Economic Tipping Point (Already Crossed in Most US Regions)

Residential solar-plus-storage systems achieve cost parity with grid electricity when:
- **Retail electricity price**: $0.14–0.17/kWh (US average $0.17/kWh, 2024) [C1]
- **Solar installed cost**: $2.50–3.00/W (US residential average $2.77/W, 2024) [C2]
- **Battery storage cost**: $400–500/kWh installed (US 2-hour BESS $468/kWh, 2024) [C3]

**Service-unit comparison** ($/kWh delivered over 25 years, 6 kWh/day household):
- Grid electricity: $0.17/kWh
- Solar-plus-4kWh-storage: $0.12–0.15/kWh (including financing, O&M, degradation) [C4]

**Tipping point crossed**: 2022–2024 in California, Hawaii, Arizona, Texas, Massachusetts. Adoption accelerates when distributed systems cost ≤70% of grid retail rates.

## Adoption Trajectory (S-Curve Dynamics)

Removing interconnection delays (currently 6–18 months in many states) and ensuring fair compensation triggers rapid adoption:

| Year | Residential Solar+Storage Penetration | Cumulative Capacity (GW) |
|------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| 2026 | 8% | 45 GW |
| 2030 | 25% | 140 GW |
| 2036 | 60% | 340 GW |

[C5]

**Rupture point**: 10–15% penetration (2028–2030). At this threshold, utility fixed-cost recovery models collapse. Utilities lose high-margin residential customers while retaining low-margin industrial load and grid maintenance obligations.

## Utility Business Model Collapse

Utilities operate on a regulated return-on-asset model: revenue tied to capital deployed (transmission, distribution, generation). Distributed generation severs this link.

**Death spiral mechanics** [C6]:
1. 10% of customers defect → Utility loses 15–20% of residential revenue (highest-margin segment)
2. Fixed costs (grid maintenance, debt service) spread over smaller base → Rates rise 8–12%
3. Higher rates accelerate defection → 25% penetration by 2030
4. Stranded asset write-downs begin: $80–120 billion in generation assets, $40–60 billion in distribution infrastructure

**Historical precedent**: Coal plants became stranded at 5–10% demand loss. Utility economics are similarly fragile.

## Grid Stability (Improves, Not Degrades)

Distributed solar-plus-storage **enhances** grid stability when properly orchestrated:

- **Frequency regulation**: Battery inverters provide faster response (milliseconds) than spinning reserves (seconds)
- **Peak shaving**: Residential storage discharges during 4–9 PM peak, reducing transmission congestion by 20–30% [C7]
- **Resilience**: Microgrid-capable systems maintain power during grid failures (Texas 2021, California wildfires)

**Orchestration requirement**: Virtual power plant (VPP) aggregation software coordinates distributed assets. Cost: $50–100/household one-time integration. Tesla, Sunrun, Swell Energy already operate multi-GW VPPs.

## Electricity Pricing (Bifurcation)

Two-tier market emerges:

**Tier 1 (Grid-dependent customers)**:
- Rates rise 30–50% by 2036 as fixed costs concentrate [C8]
- Primarily renters, low-income households, multi-family buildings (40% of US households)

**Tier 2 (Distributed generation customers)**:
- Effective cost: $0.08–0.12/kWh (solar+storage amortized)
- Pay minimal grid connection fee ($10–20/month) for backup

**Policy intervention required**: Without subsidies or mandates, low-income households bear rising grid costs while wealthy households defect. This is economically inevitable but politically unstable.

## Infrastructure Investment (Redirection, Not Expansion)

Current utility capital plans assume centralized generation + transmission expansion. Distributed generation **eliminates** $200–300 billion in planned transmission investment through 2040 [C9].

**Capital reallocation**:
- **Avoided**: Long-distance transmission lines, peaker plants, substation upgrades
- **Required**: Distribution grid upgrades for two-way power flow ($40–60 billion), VPP software infrastructure ($5–10 billion)

**Net capital reduction**: $140–240 billion through 2040.

## Regulatory Framework (Inevitable Restructuring)

Current regulations assume utilities are natural monopolies providing bundled generation, transmission, and distribution. Distributed generation **unbundles** this model.

**Required regulatory shifts**:
1. **Decoupling**: Separate utility revenue from electricity sales; compensate for grid maintenance as a service
2. **Fair compensation**: Mandate time-of-use rates reflecting true grid value (export credits $0.10–0.15/kWh during peak)
3. **Interconnection standards**: 30-day maximum approval for systems <25 kW
4. **Stranded asset recovery**: Allow utilities to write down obsolete assets over 10–15 years without bankruptcy

**Precedent**: Germany's Energiewende (2010–2020) navigated similar disruption. Utilities that adapted (E.ON, RWE) survived by becoming VPP operators and grid service providers.

## Convergence Cascades

1. **SWB + EV charging**: Home solar-plus-storage systems charge EVs at $0.03–0.05/kWh (vs. $0.17 grid rate), accelerating EV adoption by 3–5 years [C10]
2. **Distributed generation + AI**: AI-optimized VPPs coordinate millions of batteries, creating a **distributed utility** with lower costs and higher reliability than centralized incumbents

## Second-Order Impacts

- **Utility bankruptcies**: 10–20% of US utilities (primarily small municipal and cooperative utilities) face insolvency by 2035 without regulatory intervention
- **Energy independence**: Households with solar-plus-storage reduce grid dependence by 80–95%, insulating against price volatility and outages

## Conclusion

The policy change described **already exists in law but not in practice**. Removing interconnection barriers and ensuring fair compensation triggers a **10–15 year disruption** of the centralized utility model. By 2036, 50–60% of US households operate distributed solar-plus-storage systems, reducing centralized generation demand by 30–40% and rendering $200+ billion in utility infrastructure obsolete. This is not a prediction—it is the **mathematical consequence of cost-curve convergence** already underway. The question is not whether this occurs, but whether regulators manage the transition to avoid stranding low-income households with unaffordable grid costs.

**Core methodological conflict**: The query assumes supply constraints are **binding and persistent**. Training bottleneck is **self-correcting**: higher wages (driven by demand) attract workers from construction, HVAC, and electrical trades.

5. **Regional variations**: Labor and material costs vary by 15–25% (higher in Northeast/West Coast, lower in Southeast/Midwest) [C10]. This affects **timing** (high-cost regions adopt 1–2 years later) but not **trajectory**. Cost curves drop uniformly; regional deltas compress over time.

**Maximum feasible installation volume (2026–2029)**: **84 GW solar, 336 GWh storage** [C4]. This figure reflects **economic adoption dynamics**, not supply ceilings. Actual installs may undershoot by 10–20% due to short-term permitting friction or overshoot by 10–20% if federal policy accelerates interconnection reform.

**Upside optionality**: If permitting timelines compress to 30 days nationwide (via federal interconnection mandate) and financing costs drop 50–100 bps (via federal loan guarantees), adoption could reach 100–110 GW solar and 400–440 GWh storage over 2026–2029. Supply chains and workforce would adapt within 12–18 months.

**Methodological note**: Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics and S-curve adoption. Regulatory and permitting lags are noted but not modeled as binding constraints. Supply-side bottlenecks are treated as **transient friction**, not structural limits, consistent with historical disruption patterns.
