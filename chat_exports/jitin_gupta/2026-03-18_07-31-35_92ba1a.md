### Structural Conclusions

The 10% oversupply threshold is not a question of if — only when.** The economic gravity of lithium-ion cost-curve dynamics (batteries now below $84/kWh in China [C3], on trajectory to $50/kWh by 2028) makes the lead demand collapse mathematically inevitable. The rupture window is 2028–2031 depending on TaaS penetration speed.

**2. Secondary supply is the time bomb.** The 4-year recycling lag means secondary lead supply stays elevated for years after battery demand collapses. This is the primary mechanism creating the oversupply: supply cannot self-correct fast enough. By 2031, the market is producing 1,377 kt more lead than it can consume.

**3. The replacement market creates a false floor.** 86% of SLI car lead demand in 2026 is replacement batteries (not new-car OEM). This creates a structural lag — even as ICE sales collapse, the large installed ICE fleet continues generating replacement demand for 4–6 more years. **5. Commercial vehicles are the last structural floor.** SLI commercial vehicle demand declines more slowly (−50% vs. −68% for passenger cars) due to longer asset lives and later EV tipping points. By 2032, commercial SLI overtakes passenger car SLI as the largest single lead demand segment — a composition shift that will reshape lead recycling economics.

**6. The annual demand destruction rate quintuples.** Battery lead demand destruction accelerates from −156 kt/yr (2026) to −505 kt/yr (2036) in the base case, and exceeds −700 kt/yr under TaaS. This is the X-curve of incumbent collapse in motion — not a gradual erosion but an exponential acceleration driven by the prevailing cost-curve dynamics of lithium-ion batteries.
