## Displacement Drivers (Base Scenario, 2026–2030)

The 1,077.5 kt reduction from 2026 to 2030 is driven by three mechanisms:

### 1. SLI Battery Displacement (ICE → EV Transition)

Passenger car SLI batteries account for **88.1%** of total lead demand reduction. The ICE passenger car fleet declines as BEV adoption accelerates post-tipping (price parity reached in most regions by 2024–2026). Each ICE vehicle requires an 11 kg lead-acid SLI battery with a 4-year replacement cycle.

- **Passenger Cars (D01):** -948.9 kt (88.1% of total reduction)
- **Commercial Vehicles (D02):** -319.6 kt (29.7% of total reduction)
- **Two-Wheeler Starter (D06):** -72.1 kt (6.7% of total reduction)
- **Three-Wheeler Starter (D07):** -6.0 kt (0.6% of total reduction)
- **Subtotal:** -1,346.6 kt

### 2. Li-ion Penetration in Stationary/Industrial Applications

Datacenter UPS, telecom UPS, and forklift batteries show **lagged** lithium-ion adoption. VRLA and LAB demand in these segments continues to grow through 2030 despite cost parity being reached, reflecting longer replacement cycles and slower B2B adoption dynamics.

- **Datacenter UPS (D09):** +77.0 kt (VRLA → Li-ion transition lag)
- **Telecom UPS (D10):** +12.1 kt (VRLA → Li-ion transition lag)
- **Forklift (D08):** +2.1 kt (LAB → Li-ion transition lag)
- **Subtotal:** +91.2 kt

### 3. EV Auxiliary Battery Growth

The BEV and PHEV fleet grows rapidly, and most EVs still use lead-acid 12V auxiliary batteries as of 2030. Lithium-ion 12V adoption reaches only ~4.3% by 2030, so the expanding EV fleet temporarily increases lead-acid auxiliary demand.

- **EV Auxiliary LAB (D03):** +177.2 kt

**Net Battery Subtotal Change:** -1,077.5 kt

## Methodology

The analysis uses a bottom-up stock-flow model across 10 lead demand drivers:

1. **SLI Passenger Cars (D01):** ICE fleet size from passenger car adoption model × 11 kg lead per battery × 25% annual replacement rate × lead-acid share (1 - lithium-ion 12V adoption).
2. **SLI Commercial Vehicles (D02):** ICE + NGV commercial fleet × 21.13 kg lead per battery × 25% replacement rate × lead-acid share.
3. **EV Auxiliary LAB (D03):** BEV + PHEV fleet × lead-acid 12V share × replacement rate.
4. **E2W/E3W LAB Traction (D04/D05):** Electric two-wheeler and three-wheeler fleet × LAB share (vs lithium-ion) × pack lead content.
5. **ICE 2W/3W Starter (D06/D07):** ICE two-wheeler and three-wheeler fleet × starter battery lead content × replacement rate.
6. **Forklift LAB (D08):** Forklift fleet × LAB share (vs lithium-ion) × 40 kWh equivalent lead content.
7. **Datacenter VRLA (D09):** Datacenter UPS capacity × VRLA share (vs lithium-ion) × lead content per kWh.
8. **Telecom VRLA (D10):** Telecom UPS capacity × VRLA share (vs lithium-ion) × lead content per kWh.

Each driver incorporates tipping-point detection (price parity for B2C, cost parity for B2B) followed by S-curve adoption of lithium-ion alternatives. The TaaS scenario overlays a separate S-curve for autonomous ride-hailing fleet penetration (regulatory approval 2026, 100% ceiling by 2040) on top of the base passenger car model.

## Conclusion

Lead demand falls to 90% of its 2026 baseline in **2030** (base scenario) or **2029** (TaaS scenario). The primary driver is the collapse of the ICE passenger car fleet as BEV adoption accelerates post-tipping. Passenger car SLI batteries account for 88% of the 2026–2030 reduction.
