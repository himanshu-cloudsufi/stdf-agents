**Global Lead Supply-Demand Balance: 10% Oversupply Threshold**

The 10% surplus year is **2026** — and this holds across both the base disruption scenario and the accelerated TaaS scenario [C1].

| Scenario | 10% Surplus Year |
|---|---|
| Base Scenario | **2026** |
| TaaS Scenario | **2026** |

**Geographic scope:** Global (as modeled by the lead demand stack).

---

**Why 2026?**

The lead demand stack is being disrupted simultaneously across all major end-use segments [C1]:

- **SLI (automotive starter batteries):** The primary demand driver. As BEV adoption scales — BEVs eliminate the SLI battery entirely — and as 48V mild-hybrid and full-EV architectures replace 12V lead-acid systems, SLI demand collapses structurally. This is the largest single demand segment and its disruption alone is sufficient to drive the 10% surplus.
- **VRLA / industrial / telecom backup:** Lithium-ion (LFP in particular) is displacing VRLA in datacenter UPS, telecom towers, and industrial standby applications on cost and cycle-life economics. This segment's demand destruction compounds the SLI decline.
- **Other lead end-uses** (pigments, alloys, radiation shielding): These are smaller and slower-moving, but they do not offset the structural demand loss in batteries, which represent ~85% of global lead consumption.

The convergence of both scenarios on 2026 is the critical signal: the 10% oversupply threshold is **not contingent on TaaS acceleration** [C1]. It is already embedded in the base cost-curve trajectory of lithium-ion displacing lead-acid across SLI and VRLA segments. TaaS deepens the surplus post-2026 but does not move the trigger year.

---

**Supply-side context:** Lead supply (primary mine output + secondary recycling) cannot contract fast enough to match the demand destruction rate. Secondary lead (recycled from spent batteries) is particularly sticky — it continues flowing as the existing installed base of lead-acid batteries reaches end-of-life, even as new installations collapse. This supply inertia is what creates the structural overhang.
