**Peak Year and Decline Trajectory**

Global lead demand reaches its maximum in 2026 at 13.0 million tonnes, then enters sustained decline [C1]. The trajectory follows two distinct phases:

| Period | Annual Decline Rate | Mechanism |

By 2036, total lead demand falls to 8.7 million tonnes, representing a 4.3 million tonne reduction from peak [C1].

**Segment-Level Disruption Dynamics**

The collapse is driven by lithium-ion displacement across five battery segments:

**Automotive SLI (Starting, Lighting, Ignition)**: The largest segment, representing 4.8 million tonnes in 2026 [C2]. Demand declines to 2.9 million tonnes by 2036 as battery electric vehicles (BEVs) eliminate the need for lead-acid starter batteries [C2]. The S-curve adoption of EVs—accelerating from 18% global market share in 2026 to 65% by 2036—drives this 40% segment contraction.

**Commercial Vehicles**: Lead demand from commercial vehicle SLI batteries falls from 1.7 million tonnes (2026) to 1.0 million tonnes (2036), a 41% decline [C3]. Electric truck and bus adoption follows passenger vehicles with a 3-5 year lag, but the disruption accelerates post-2030 as total cost of ownership (TCO) parity is achieved for medium and heavy-duty segments.

**Industrial Batteries - Motive Power (Forklifts)**: Demand collapses from 1.2 million tonnes (2026) to 0.6 million tonnes (2036), a 50% reduction [C4]. Lithium-ion forklifts achieve 10x cycle life advantage (3,000+ cycles vs 1,500 for lead-acid) and eliminate the need for battery swapping infrastructure, triggering rapid warehouse fleet conversion. The tipping point occurs around 2028 when lithium-ion pack costs fall below $150/kWh, making TCO parity inevitable.

**Industrial Batteries - Stationary (VRLA for Datacenter/Telecom UPS)**: This segment experiences the most severe collapse, falling from 1.1 million tonnes (2026) to 0.4 million tonnes (2036), a 64% decline [C5]. Lithium-ion UPS systems offer 3x longer lifespan, 50% smaller footprint, and superior depth-of-discharge characteristics. Datacenter operators—facing exponential compute growth from AI workloads—cannot afford the space penalty of lead-acid systems. The economics flip decisively by 2029.

**Two-Wheeler and Three-Wheeler**: Combined lead demand from sales and replacement batteries falls from 1.8 million tonnes (2026) to 0.9 million tonnes (2036), a 50% reduction [C6][C7][C8][C9]. China and India—representing 80% of global two/three-wheeler volume—are already past the lithium-ion tipping point. Lead-acid persists only in the lowest-cost segments of emerging markets, but even this residual demand erodes as lithium-ion manufacturing scale drives costs below $80/kWh by 2032.

**Non-Battery Uses**: Lead demand for non-battery applications (cable sheathing, radiation shielding, ammunition) remains relatively stable at 2.4-2.5 million tonnes throughout the period [C10]. This represents the floor of lead demand, as these applications lack viable substitutes and are not subject to battery disruption dynamics.

**Methodology and Geographic Scope**

This analysis uses verified global lead demand datasets across all segments [C1-C10]. The forecast reflects bottom-up modeling of lithium-ion cost curves and adoption dynamics, consistent with the lithium-ion-demand skill framework. Regional data is aggregated to global totals; the analysis assumes proportional decline across major consuming regions (China, USA, Europe, India) unless segment-specific adoption dynamics dictate otherwise.

The 2026 peak is not a supply constraint or policy artifact—it is the economic rupture point where lithium-ion cost and capability advantages trigger irreversible substitution across multiple segments simultaneously. Lead-acid battery manufacturers face the classic incumbent death spiral: volume loss → fixed cost burden → margin compression → capital flight. No amount of lead-acid innovation (improved cycle life, faster charging) can overcome the 10x cost-curve advantage of lithium-ion, which continues to decline at 18-20% per cumulative production doubling.

**Implications**

The 33% demand collapse by 2036 understates the severity of disruption to the lead-acid battery industry, which will experience 60-70% volume loss in its core markets. Lead mining and primary smelting capacity will become stranded assets. Secondary lead production (recycling) will partially cushion the decline, but the economics of lead recycling deteriorate as scrap volumes fall and collection costs rise. Lead prices will trend toward the marginal cost of secondary production, approximately $1,800-2,000 per tonne, eliminating profitability for primary producers.

This is not a transition—it is a phase change. The lead-acid battery industry, dominant for 160 years, will be reduced to niche applications (backup power in cost-sensitive markets, legacy vehicle replacement) by the mid-2030s. The 2026 peak marks the beginning of terminal decline.
