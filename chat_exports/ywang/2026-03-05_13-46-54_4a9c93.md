To build a rigorous investment thesis for Celestica [[COMPANY:CO1]]'s datacenter business, you need a framework that evaluates both the demand-side exponential (AI compute + cloud infrastructure) and the supply-side positioning (Celestica's role, margins, and disruption risk). Here's the analytical structure:

## 1. Datacenter Demand Drivers & Growth Trajectories

**Questions to answer:**
- What is the hyperscaler capex trajectory (2026-2035)? Track Amazon [[COMPANY:CO2]], Microsoft [[COMPANY:CO3]], Google [[COMPANY:CO4]], Meta [[COMPANY:CO5]] quarterly capex guidance and decompose into AI training vs inference vs traditional cloud.
- What is the AI compute demand curve? Model GPU cluster buildout (H100/H200/B200 deployment schedules), inference scaling post-ChatGPT, and the shift from training-heavy to inference-heavy workloads.
- What is the datacenter electricity demand growth rate? AI datacenters consume 10-50 MW per facility vs 5-10 MW for traditional cloud. This drives cooling, power distribution, and rack density requirements—all areas where manufacturing complexity (and Celestica's value-add) increases.
- What is the battery storage attach rate? Datacenters are adding lithium-ion UPS and backup systems to replace lead-acid VRLA. This creates a new revenue stream for ODM/EMS providers with power management expertise.

**Required data:**
- Hyperscaler capex as % of revenue (historical 2020-2025, projected 2026-2030)
- GPU shipment forecasts (NVIDIA, AMD) and average selling prices
- Datacenter power density trends (kW per rack) and cooling architecture shifts (air vs liquid)
- Global datacenter capacity additions (MW, square footage) by region

## 2. Celestica's Supply Chain Position

**Questions to answer:**
- What is Celestica's customer concentration? If >50% of datacenter revenue comes from 1-2 hyperscalers, you have concentration risk. If diversified across 4-6 hyperscalers plus enterprise customers, you have resilience.
- What is Celestica's manufacturing scope? Does it assemble full racks, white-box servers, storage arrays, or just sub-assemblies (PCBAs, power supplies)? Higher integration = higher value capture.
- What is Celestica's exposure to AI infrastructure specifically? AI servers require custom cooling, high-speed interconnects (NVLink, InfiniBand), and power delivery at 400-800W per GPU. If Celestica manufactures AI-optimized platforms, it captures the fastest-growing segment.
- What is Celestica's geographic footprint? Hyperscalers are diversifying supply chains (US, Mexico, Southeast Asia) to reduce China exposure. Does Celestica have capacity in the right regions?

**Required data:**
- Revenue by customer (top 5 customers as % of datacenter segment revenue)
- Revenue by product category (servers, storage, networking, power/cooling)
- Manufacturing locations and capacity (MW of datacenter equipment per year)
- Design win pipeline (new platforms entering production in next 12-24 months)

## 3. Cost-Curve Dynamics & Competitive Positioning

**Questions to answer:**
- What is Celestica's cost position vs competitors (Flex [[COMPANY:CO6]], Jabil [[COMPANY:CO7]], Sanmina [[COMPANY:CO8]], Wistron, Quanta)? ODM/EMS is a scale game. Cost per unit falls with volume due to procurement leverage, factory utilization, and process learning.
- What is the learning curve in datacenter manufacturing? Unlike consumer electronics (mature, commoditized), AI datacenter platforms are evolving rapidly. Early-mover advantage exists for companies that master liquid cooling, high-density power delivery, and GPU cluster integration.
- What is the competitive moat? If Celestica's moat is "we're cheaper," that's fragile. If the moat is "we co-design with hyperscalers and have proprietary thermal/power IP," that's durable.

**Required data:**
- Gross margin trends (datacenter segment, quarterly, 2020-2025)
- Operating margin vs peers (compare Celestica datacenter EBIT margin to Flex, Jabil equivalents)
- Capacity utilization rates (>85% is healthy; <70% signals overcapacity or demand weakness)
- R&D spend as % of datacenter revenue (higher R&D suggests co-design partnerships, not just assembly)

## 4. Margin Structure & Capital Intensity

**Questions to answer:**
- What is the gross margin trajectory? Datacenter manufacturing gross margins typically range 6-12%. If Celestica is at the low end, it's a volume play with limited pricing power. If at the high end, it has differentiation (custom design, complex integration).
- What is the working capital requirement? ODM/EMS businesses are working-capital-intensive (inventory, receivables). Calculate cash conversion cycle (days). Shorter cycle = better capital efficiency.
- What is the capex intensity? Datacenter manufacturing requires factory expansion, SMT lines, and test equipment. Track capex as % of revenue. If capex is rising faster than revenue, ROIC will compress.
- What is the ROIC? Return on invested capital is the ultimate metric. If ROIC > 15%, Celestica is creating value. If ROIC < 10%, it's a commodity business destroying value.

**Required data:**
- Gross margin, operating margin, net margin (quarterly, datacenter segment)
- Working capital metrics (inventory turns, DSO, DPO)
- Capex (datacenter-specific if disclosed, otherwise total capex allocated by revenue mix)
- ROIC calculation (NOPAT / invested capital, where invested capital = equity + debt - cash)

## 5. Disruption Risks

**Questions to answer:**
- What is the vertical integration risk? Hyperscalers (especially Amazon, Google, Microsoft) are designing their own chips (Graviton, TPU, Azure Maia) and may eventually bring manufacturing in-house or direct to Taiwanese ODMs, bypassing EMS providers like Celestica.
- What is the technology shift risk? If datacenter architecture shifts (e.g., from GPU clusters to custom ASICs, or from centralized datacenters to edge computing), Celestica's installed base becomes obsolete.
- What is the commoditization risk? As AI server designs mature, manufacturing becomes commoditized. Early-stage AI infrastructure (2024-2028) has high margins due to complexity. Late-stage (2030+) margins compress as competition intensifies.
- What is the customer bargaining power? Hyperscalers have massive scale and can pit ODM/EMS providers against each other. If Celestica has no differentiation, it's a price-taker.

**Required data:**
- Hyperscaler in-house manufacturing announcements (track capex allocated to owned facilities)
- Customer contract terms (duration, exclusivity, pricing mechanisms)
- Competitive win/loss analysis (which platforms did Celestica win vs lose to competitors?)
- Technology roadmap (next-gen server platforms, cooling architectures, power delivery standards)

## 6. Quantitative Metrics to Track (Quarterly)

**Core metrics:**
- Datacenter revenue growth (YoY, QoQ)
- Datacenter revenue as % of total Celestica revenue (track mix shift)
- Gross margin (datacenter segment)
- Operating margin (datacenter segment)
- Customer concentration (top 3 customers as % of datacenter revenue)
- Capacity utilization (if disclosed)
- ROIC (annual)
- Free cash flow (operating cash flow - capex)
- Inventory days (rising inventory = demand slowdown or supply chain inefficiency)

**Leading indicators:**
- Hyperscaler capex guidance (forward 4 quarters)
- Design wins announced (new platforms entering production)
- Factory expansion announcements (new capacity coming online)
- Competitor earnings (Flex, Jabil datacenter segment performance)

## Investment Thesis Decision Tree

**Bull case (justified if):**
- Datacenter revenue growing >20% annually (2026-2030)
- Gross margin expanding or stable at >10%
- Customer concentration decreasing (diversification across 5+ hyperscalers)
- ROIC >12% and rising
- Celestica has differentiated capabilities (AI server integration, liquid cooling, co-design partnerships)
- Hyperscaler capex growing >15% annually

**Bear case (justified if):**
- Datacenter revenue growth decelerating (<10% annually)
- Gross margin compressing (<8%)
- Customer concentration increasing (>60% from top 2 customers)
- ROIC <10%
- Hyperscalers announcing vertical integration or shifting to direct ODM relationships
- Capex intensity rising faster than revenue (capital efficiency deteriorating)

**Neutral case (hold/wait):**
- Revenue growth 10-15%, margins stable, ROIC 10-12%
- No clear differentiation vs competitors
- Hyperscaler capex guidance mixed or uncertain

The thesis hinges on whether Celestica is riding a durable exponential (AI compute demand) with defensible positioning (co-design, integration capabilities, diversified customer base) or merely participating in a commoditized assembly business with compressing margins and concentration risk. The data will tell you which scenario is true.
