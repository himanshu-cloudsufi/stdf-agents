## TONY'S INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY

### Cost Curves as Gravity

Tony's most fundamental belief: cost curves are an unstoppable force, analogous to gravity.

- **Mar 20:** "Cost curves are like gravity, and they will continue until of course they don't. But it's not up to you or me to determine when that is the case. We will hit limits of physics or biology, but we don't really know when that's gonna be the case."
- **Mar 20:** Told the Moore's Law parable -- moved to Silicon Valley in 1992, everyone at Intel said the cost curve couldn't continue. "Moore's Law is dead, but the cost curve of information processing is still going, and in fact, it's accelerating."
- **Mar 20:** "Don't introduce ideas that are not intrinsic in the SIBA framework. Total cost of ownership is not a thing either. Just because they taught you in business school does not mean that I use it."
- **Mar 20:** Interrupted the team when they proposed adding cost floors to battery projections: "Wrong wrong. Do not do that, please." The fact that something looks "too cheap" doesn't mean it won't happen.

This is not just analysis -- it is epistemology. Tony believes that human intuition about cost limits is systematically wrong because intuition is trained on the old system. The model should follow the math, not the intuition.

### Disruption-First, Always

The SIBA framework (Second-order Implications, Bottlenecks, Accelerators) is not one analytical lens among many -- it is THE lens.

- **Mar 6:** "You always say it's always a capability cost curve. That's how it flows."
- **Mar 11:** "It's important to always do the SIBA framework... that's not optional."
- **Mar 20:** Erupted when a UK unemployment query returned Phillips curve analysis: "I don't really care about Phillips curve and Laffer curve... that's mainstream economics. To me, it really adds no value." And: "That is our edge. And if we don't do it, then we're not doing anything."
- **Mar 20:** "Don't introduce ideas that are not intrinsic in the SIBA framework."
- **Mar 26:** The SIBA framework is the foundation for the autonomous agent's proactive insight generation.

Mainstream economics is not banned -- it provides context for understanding policymaker reaction functions. But it must always come second. The edge is in seeing what mainstream economists cannot: disruption dynamics.

### Edge Timing -- The Window is Now

Tony believes the gap between Stellar's analytical capability and the market's understanding is at its maximum, and this gap is closing.

- **Mar 11:** "The usefulness, the value of this tool is never gonna be bigger than now... there's never gonna be as big a difference between our edge and the market than now."
- **Mar 11:** The mainstream narrative -- "every disruption in history has created new jobs" -- still has "probably another year" of life. Once that narrative breaks, the edge shrinks.
- **Mar 20:** "Our opportunity is now... as long as we can do this right."

This creates enormous urgency. The tool is not just analytically interesting; it is time-sensitive.

### Non-Linear Disruption

Tony consistently emphasizes that disruption is non-linear and discontinuous.

- **Mar 6:** "Market trauma" -- when the new product is cheaper than the incumbent but supply is not there. "That's happening in solar and in batteries."
- **Mar 6:** "Be very dogmatic about certain things because I just see that by the time we get to DEC 26, Iran war, yes or no, certain shit's going to happen."
- **Mar 11:** "We're gonna have both GDP growth and unemployment growth." The market expects one or the other, not both simultaneously.
- **Mar 26:** S-curves have rupture points at 5-10% market penetration. "We want to know ahead of the game."

### Recursiveness of Disruption

Tony uses lead as a template specifically because of recursive dynamics.

- **Mar 20:** "If we can get the lead disruption right, it's a good template to get other disruptions right. That's why I'm emphasizing lead -- because of the recursive nature of that disruption."
- The recursion: falling battery costs drive EV adoption, which displaces lead-acid batteries, which creates lead surplus, which (for byproduct commodities) cannot be reduced, which accelerates price collapse.

### Macro Fund Perspective

- **Mar 20:** "We're a macro fund. We trade not just stocks, but also commodities, currencies, rates. We care about inflation and unemployment and GDP growth and reaction functions."
- This shapes what the system must do: not stock-picking, but cross-asset disruption analysis.

### Conviction Despite Uncertainty

Tony is willing to be "dogmatic" about certain disruptions even when near-term data is noisy.

- **Mar 6:** "ICE vehicles are done. It's not bringing ICE vehicles back, right? It's just it's over."
- **Mar 6:** "Be very dogmatic about certain things."
- **Mar 20:** "Ideas, not edicts" -- but his ideas carry very strong conviction.

### Openness to Challenge

Despite conviction, Tony is not closed to feedback.

- **Mar 6:** Wants open innovation challenge to discover blind spots: "To see what we're missing."
- **Mar 11:** Acknowledges his "horses" essay has limits: "We're not quite horses -- not yet."
- **Mar 20:** "Feel free to push back... these are new ideas that we should consider."
- **Mar 6:** Recognizes the model is simplistic and needs feedback loops, reaction functions, nuance about new job creation.

## ROBERT'S ANALYTICAL PHILOSOPHY

### Empiricism First

Robert's defining characteristic is radical empiricism -- theory must earn its place through data.

- **Mar 6:** "I can be very excited about the technology, right? That's a separate statement to: given the last six months of official data, there is no applicability of this model."
- **Mar 6:** "There's no reason to believe that anything we predict for DEC 26 will take place. Nothing. Zip. Because the last three months and last six months, zip of what we predicted came true."
- **Mar 6:** Has made real trading decisions based on model outputs -- "very big mistakes" and "significant risk" taken on the back of outputs that had no predictive power.

### Models Must Prove Themselves

- **Mar 6:** "Why are we pretending that this is insightful and predictive when it actually today has no predictive power?"
- Robert insists on back-testing not as a nice-to-have but as a prerequisite for using model output in trading.
- **Mar 6:** Wants "wealth warnings" -- the model must admit when it doesn't know. "Put it aside and come back and look at it in three months."

### Non-Linear Disruption Thresholds

Robert's most original intellectual contribution is the hallucination-interval framework.

- **Mar 6:** "At 16 minutes [between hallucinations], maybe it doesn't show up. At 20 minutes, maybe it's an augmenter of my productivity. At four hours, it's a fucking beast. And at 64 hours, I'm gone."

This frames AI disruption not as a smooth curve but as a step function -- there are specific capability thresholds where the nature of the disruption qualitatively changes from irrelevant to augmenting to replacing.

### Pre-Tipping Point Framing

- **Mar 6:** Robert uses the model's own language: "In the model language, this is pre-tipping point." He acknowledges the framework may be correct long-term but inapplicable now.

### Hypothesis Generation Under Failure

When the model showed zero predictive fit, Robert generated 9+ hypotheses for why (Mar 6):
1. Global economy in upswing masking displacement
2. Complete thesis could be wrong for current period
3. Non-linear disruption threshold not yet reached
4. GPU supply / infrastructure lag bigger than expected
5. Legal/regulatory barriers slowing adoption
6. Install time for companies longer than modeled
7. Startups disrupting incumbents need time to grow
8. Labor regulations bigger than thought
9. Current AI capacity only sufficient for augmentation, not replacement

This is rigorous analytical thinking -- not abandoning the thesis, but systematically testing its failure modes.

### Caution from Experience

- **Mar 6:** Robert has had "significant issues trading this labor disruption into this Iraq/Iran war." Near-term macro dominated, and being too dogmatic about the disruption thesis caused real losses.

## KEY METAPHORS AND MENTAL MODELS

### "We're Not Horses -- Not Yet"
Tony's essay and recurring reference. Current AI disruption has horse-like dynamics (entire categories of work becoming obsolete) but hasn't reached the inflection point where it's obviously irreversible. The model sits in the ambiguous middle.

### "Cars at 5% Utilization"
Tony's capacity factor concept (Mar 26). Cars are parked 95% of the time. Transport as a Service takes the 5% capacity factor to 50-80%, fundamentally disrupting vehicle ownership. Applied broadly: "100% adoption of 1% of the capability" describes the current state of AI usage at Stellar itself.

### "Cost Curves Are Like Gravity"
Tony's core axiom (Mar 20). Cost curves are a force of nature that will continue until physics or biology stops them. Human intuition about when they'll stop is systematically wrong. Do not impose cost floors. The Moore's Law parable is the supporting evidence.

### "Data Is Poison"
Dr. Waleed's framing (Mar 11). Internet data is fundamentally untrustworthy. The data factory must be a "highly guarded embassy" where only curated, verified data enters.

### "Market Trauma"
Tony's term (Mar 6) for the moment when a disruptor is cheaper than the incumbent but supply cannot meet demand. This is happening in solar and batteries.

### The Hallucination Interval as Disruption Threshold
Robert's framework (Mar 6). The time between AI hallucinations maps to qualitatively different disruption outcomes: 16 minutes = irrelevant, 20 minutes = augmentation, 4 hours = transformation, 64 hours = total replacement. The disruption is a step function, not a smooth curve.

### "Rupture Points"
Tony's term (Mar 26) for the 5-10% market penetration threshold where disruption becomes visible and self-reinforcing. "When the new is cheaper than the old even if it's in one market."

### "Correlations Breaking = Investment Opportunity"
Tony's recurring theme (Mar 20, Mar 26). When established correlations between technologies or markets break, that signals a structural shift and a trading opportunity.

## HOW THINKING EVOLVED: MAR 6 TO MAR 26

### The Arc of Model Confidence

**Mar 6:** Crisis of confidence. Robert discovers zero predictive fit. The team confronts the gap between theoretical elegance and practical utility. Tone is diagnostic -- what's wrong and how do we fix it.

**Mar 11:** Rebuilding with discipline. Tony sets priorities (unemployment > GDP, US > UK > China > EU). Back-testing becomes a formal requirement. Task clustering is proposed. The mood shifts from "the model is broken" to "the model needs iteration."

**Mar 20:** Methodological rigor intensifies. No cost floors. SIBA framework must be primary in every analysis. Convergence identified as a gap. Lead model becomes the canonical template for recursive disruption. Tony demands unprompted insights -- first articulation of the autonomous agent concept.

**Mar 26:** Paradigm shift. Tony articulates the full vision of flipping from chat-based to 24/7 autonomous agent. The model should prompt the users, not the other way around. Portfolio monitoring, correlation break detection, rupture point identification -- the system becomes proactive. AL model goes to 4 clusters with K-Medoids, back-tested against 5 months of data. Under-predicts but is improving.

### The "Flip" -- From Chat to Autonomous Agent

This is the single biggest idea evolution across the four meetings.

**Mar 6 (Seeds):** Tony asks for observability into how agents work. Stellar Sense / cron jobs already exist for automated queries. But the paradigm is still user-driven.

**Mar 11 (Frustration):** Tony can't use the system in real-time meetings because queries take too long. Wants a toggle for urgency. The newsletter should add intelligence, not just news. The system is too passive.

**Mar 20 (First Articulation):** "What I really want is unprompted self-initiated insights from the software." Tony lists 5 categories: correlation breaks, threshold alerts, risk flags, scaling property changes, questions nobody is asking. "Let's start with five things." This is the conceptual prototype of the autonomous agent.

**Mar 26 (Full Vision):** "We need to flip Stellar Edge from essentially a chat-based kind of exercise to around the clock, infinite loop kind of software. Basically, we don't prompt the software, it prompts us." Tony lays out the complete vision:
- Software works 24/7: learning, gathering data, doing portfolio risk, looking for investment ideas, tracking positions, writing PTIPs
- Humans remain in the loop but AI drives the workflow
- Multi-channel alerts: email, WhatsApp, voice
- "If it has a question, then WhatsApp me or Robert. Hey, I found this. What do you think? And then it keeps going."
- "We know data doesn't sleep, we should update it every day. Now we need to realize that the intelligence also does not sleep."
- "Even Robert sits in front of this like hours at a time, but even four hours is just one sixth of the day."

The philosophical shift is from AI as tool to AI as analyst. The human role changes from operator to supervisor.

### Precision as the Bottleneck

Across all four meetings, precision is the constraint that prevents the vision from being realized.

- **Mar 6:** Robert discovers the model has zero predictive power.
- **Mar 11:** Tony acknowledges "we're gonna have to keep iterating and learning."
- **Mar 20:** "The model is improving in terms of thinking but not in terms of precision."
- **Mar 26:** "It has to be predictive. If it's not predictive, we don't want to introduce complexity."

The vision of a 24/7 autonomous agent is gated on the model being reliable enough to trust. Until precision improves, the autonomous agent cannot be deployed without risk of triggering false signals that lead to bad trades.

### From Uniform to Differentiated Modeling

The analytical approach evolved significantly:

- **Mar 6:** Uniform parameters across all 90,000 subtasks. Single S-curve for all tasks.
- **Mar 11:** Two clustering approaches proposed (rule-based, unsupervised). Parameters vary per cluster.
- **Mar 20:** ML-based clustering into 4 clusters. Task-level aggregation replaces subtask-level. Different S-curves per cluster.
- **Mar 26:** K-Medoids with 12 features. Labeled clusters with specific parameters. Back-testing against actual data. Under-predicts -- needs calibration.

This represents real analytical progress: from a toy model to a structured, testable, differentiable framework.

## UNDERLYING STRATEGIC TENSION

The deepest tension in these meetings is between **conviction and calibration**.

Tony brings conviction: cost curves are gravity, ICE is dead, the edge is now, be dogmatic about certain disruptions. This conviction is what makes the fund different from every other macro fund running mainstream models.

Robert brings calibration: show me the fit, admit when the model doesn't work, be humble about predictions, don't lose money on a thesis that hasn't materialized in the data yet.

The team must build a system that embodies both: bold enough to follow cost curves where they lead (even when the implications seem extreme), but honest enough to flag when those curves haven't yet manifested in tradeable signals. The autonomous agent vision (Mar 26) is the synthesis -- a system that continuously monitors for the moment when the theoretical edge becomes a practical signal, and alerts the humans when it does.
