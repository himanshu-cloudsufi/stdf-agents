## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### One-Liner
SWB (Solar-Wind-Battery) displaces ALL fossil generation globally by 2037,
driven by $0 marginal cost merit-order dispatch.

### Key Findings
  - Global SWB generation grows from 5,239 TWh (2025) to 260,373 TWh (2040)
  - All fossil generation eliminated globally by 2037 (coal 8,781 to 0 TWh, gas 3,636 to 0 TWh)
  - China transitions fastest: coal from 4,612 TWh to 0 in 6 years (2025-2031)
  - USA transitions slowest to gas-free: cheap gas ($35/MWh, 1,906 TWh) persists until 2036
  - Global electricity demand nearly doubles to 54,849 TWh by 2040
  - Heat pumps are the #1 demand growth driver (~6,800 TWh incremental by 2040)
  - Datacenter growth at 12% CAGR adds ~1,850 TWh by 2040

### Confidence
Medium overall. Direction is certain; timing +/-3-5 years for key milestones.
Post-2035 numbers are increasingly speculative due to exponential extrapolation.
Model is more aggressive than IEA WEO and BNEF by 5-10 percentage points at 2030.

## S01 -- DISRUPTION MAP

### Disruption Overview
  Disruptor       Solar-Wind-Battery (SWB) -- clean generation with $0 marginal cost
  Incumbent       Coal + Natural Gas power generation
  Mechanism       Merit-order dispatch -- lowest marginal cost dispatches first
  Key Insight     SWB has $0 marginal cost (no fuel). In merit-order dispatch, SWB
                  always runs first. Fossil fuels only generate when SWB cannot meet
                  demand. As SWB capacity grows exponentially, fossil generation is
                  progressively squeezed out.

### Displacement Sequence by Region
  China           Gas first, then coal. Gas $70/MWh >> Coal $35/MWh. Gas already at 0%.
  USA             Coal first, then gas. Coal $40/MWh > Gas $35/MWh. Gas persists longest.
  Europe          Gas first, then coal. Gas $60/MWh > Coal $50/MWh.
  Rest of World   Gas first, then coal. Gas $55/MWh > Coal $45/MWh.

  Global pattern: In 3 of 4 regions, gas is displaced before coal (it's more expensive).
  The USA is the exception -- US gas at $35/MWh is the cheapest fossil fuel globally,
  so coal exits first. This means gas persists in the USA until 2036.

### Demand Drivers
  - SWB Capacity Growth (supply): Exponential growth in solar PV, wind, and battery
    storage capacity. Directly increases SWB generation share.
  - EV Charging Load (demand): Electric vehicle fleet growth adds 3,195 TWh globally
    by 2040. Increases total electricity demand, partially offset by SWB growth.
  - Datacenter Load (demand): AI-driven datacenter growth at 12% CAGR, adds ~1,850 TWh
    by 2040. Concentrated in USA (largest datacenter market).
  - Heat Pump Electrification (demand): Building heating shift from gas/oil to electric
    heat pumps. Largest incremental demand driver globally (~6,800 TWh by 2040).
  - Battery Cost Decline (enabling): Li-ion batteries from $1,200/kWh (2010) to ~$120/kWh
    (2024). Enables SWB to provide dispatchable power (storage fills intermittency gap).
  - Fossil Stranded Assets (reinforcing): As utilization drops, fossil plants become
    uneconomic and retire. Accelerates fossil exit once SWB share exceeds ~50%.
  - Grid Modernization (enabling): Smart grid, interconnection, and storage integration.
    Enables higher SWB penetration before curtailment limits.
  - Policy & Subsidy (accelerating): Renewable mandates, carbon pricing, fossil phase-out
    targets. Not modeled in base case -- represents upside risk to SWB adoption.

### Chimera Identification
  Gas peaker plants     Provide backup generation during SWB intermittency gaps.
                        Peak relevance 2025-2035. Eventually replaced by battery + overcapacity.
  Hybrid solar+storage  Solar with co-located battery -- bridges day/night gap.
                        Peak relevance 2025-2030. Becomes standard deployment model.

### Limitations
  - No hydrogen production or consumption modeled
  - No new nuclear capacity -- existing nuclear in non-SWB category
  - No transmission & distribution (T&D) constraints
  - No curtailment economics -- SWB can exceed 100% of demand in model
  - No carbon pricing scenarios -- base case only
  - No policy change scenarios (tariffs, subsidy removal)
  - Merit-order dispatch is simplified -- no hourly/seasonal resolution


## S02a -- COST CURVE -- SWB

### Key Insight
SWB marginal cost = $0/MWh. This is the fundamental driver of the disruption.
Unlike LCOE (which includes capital costs), marginal cost determines dispatch priority.
Since SWB has no fuel cost, it ALWAYS dispatches first in merit-order systems.
The tipping point already passed -- SWB is already the cheapest generation at the margin.

### LCOE vs Marginal Cost
LCOE includes capital costs spread over lifetime. For dispatch decisions, only
marginal cost matters. A solar farm already built has LCOE of ~$30/MWh but
marginal cost of $0/MWh. Coal at $35-50/MWh marginal cost will always lose to
SWB in dispatch, regardless of SWB's LCOE.

New SWB capacity continues to be built because LCOE is competitive with fossil.
But existing SWB dispatches at $0 regardless. The question is not IF SWB wins --
it's HOW FAST capacity is built.

  Gas     $70/MWh  Most expensive fossil globally. Already fully displaced.

  Displacement: gas exit already 0 in 2025, coal exit 2031
  Carbon pricing: China ETS ~$10-15/tCO2. If $50, coal effective cost -> ~$80/MWh. Not modeled.

### USA
  Coal    $40/MWh  Second-cheapest coal. Marginal fossil -- displaced before gas.
  Gas     $35/MWh  Cheapest gas AND cheapest fossil globally. Baseload -- persists longest.
  Merit order: SWB ($0) > Non-SWB (~$10-15) > Gas ($35) > Coal ($40)
  Displacement: coal exit 2032, gas exit 2036
  Unique: Only region where gas is cheaper than coal. Gas at $35/MWh is the "last fossil standing" globally.
  Carbon pricing: No national carbon price. If $50/tCO2: gas -> ~$60, coal -> ~$90. Not modeled.

### Europe
  Coal    $50/MWh  Most expensive coal globally. Persists longer than gas despite higher cost.
  Gas     $60/MWh  Second-most expensive gas. Marginal fossil -- displaced first.
  Merit order: SWB ($0) > Non-SWB (~$10-15) > Coal ($50) > Gas ($60)
  Displacement: gas exit 2033, coal exit 2035
  Carbon pricing: EU ETS ~$50-80/tCO2 (2024). Effective: coal ~$95, gas ~$85. Conservative base case ignores this.

### Rest of World

  Displacement: gas exit 2034, coal exit 2037
  Carbon pricing: Varies widely. Minimal aggregate effect. Not modeled.

## S07 -- FEEDBACK LOOPS

### Reinforcing Loops

R1: Wright's Law Manufacturing Scale (very high strength)
  Cycle: SWB cost decline -> more deployment -> more manufacturing scale -> lower cost
  Evidence: Solar LCOE declined 91% (2010-2024), battery costs declined 90%
  Every doubling of cumulative solar production reduces cost by ~20%. With global
  solar capacity doubling every 2-3 years, this creates a powerful self-reinforcing loop.

R2: Battery-Grid Synergy (high strength)
  Cycle: Battery cost decline -> grid storage viable -> more solar+wind viable -> more battery demand
  Evidence: Battery costs $1,200/kWh (2010) to $120/kWh (2024)
  Cheaper batteries make SWB dispatchable (not just intermittent). This increases SWB's
  effective capacity factor and market share, driving more battery demand and further cost reduction.

R3: EV-Grid Reinforcement (high strength)
  Cycle: EV adoption -> electricity demand growth -> more SWB built -> lower grid prices -> more EV adoption
  Evidence: EV charging adds 616 TWh globally by 2030, 3,195 TWh by 2040
  A cross-sector reinforcing loop accelerating both transitions.

R4: Fossil Stranded Assets (medium to high strength)
  Cycle: Falling utilization -> uneconomic fossil plants -> retirement -> less fossil capacity -> SWB fills gap
  Onset: Begins when SWB share exceeds ~30-40%

R5: Datacenter Direct PPA (medium strength)
  Cycle: Data center growth -> direct solar/battery PPAs -> accelerates utility-scale SWB -> lower costs
  Regional focus: Strongest in USA
  Tech companies sign multi-GW PPAs for solar+storage. Datacenter demand grows at 12% CAGR.

### Balancing Loops

B1: Grid Integration Limits (high strength)
  Cycle: High SWB share -> intermittency challenges -> grid stability concerns -> slower approval
  Threshold: SWB 50-60% share
  Most significant balancing loop. Battery storage and grid modernization partially offset.

B2: Permitting & NIMBY (medium strength)
  Cycle: More SWB -> community opposition -> longer permitting -> slower deployment
  Regional variation: Strongest in Europe/USA, weakest in China

B3: Critical Mineral Supply (medium strength)
  Cycle: SWB demand -> mineral demand -> supply constraints -> higher costs
  Critical minerals: lithium, copper, cobalt, rare earths, silver, polysilicon
  Mining expansion lags demand by 5-7 years. Creates delays, not stops.

B4: Policy Reversal Risk (low to medium strength)
  Cycle: Political change -> subsidy removal or tariffs -> slower deployment
  SWB is now competitive without subsidies ($30/MWh vs $35-70 fossil).

B5: Utility & Incumbent Resistance (low to medium strength)
  Cycle: SWB growth -> utility revenue decline -> lobbying -> regulatory barriers
  Regional variation: Strongest in USA (regulated utilities)

### Net Assessment
Reinforcing loops dominate the near-to-medium term (2025-2035):
  - R1 (Wright's Law) + R2 (battery synergy) provide structural cost advantages
  - R3 (EV-grid) and R5 (datacenter) create demand justifying more SWB
  - R4 (stranded assets) accelerates fossil exit once SWB exceeds ~40%

Balancing loops become more relevant post-2030:
  - B1 (grid integration) is the most significant constraint
  - B3 (minerals) creates temporary supply bottlenecks

Overall: reinforcing loops >>>> balancing loops. The transition is self-reinforcing
and unlikely to reverse. The question is timing, not direction.


## S08 -- CONFIDENCE

Overall: Medium

Medium confidence reflects: (1) strong directional certainty that SWB will displace
fossil fuels, (2) moderate uncertainty in timing (+/-3-5 years for key milestones),
(3) significant uncertainty in post-overcapacity dynamics (SWB >100%).
Model strongest in 2025-2032 range. Beyond 2035, increasingly speculative.

### High Sensitivity Parameters
  SWB capacity growth rate (blended CAGR)
    Current: Blended 3yr/5yr/10yr CAGR (equal weight 0.333 each)
    Impact: +/-2% CAGR shift moves fossil-free date by +/-2-3 years globally
    Single most impactful parameter. Confidence: medium.

  Battery storage duration
    Current: China 2h, USA/Europe/RoW 4h
    Impact: Affects SWB dispatchability. Outsized importance for SWB share above 50%.
    Confidence: low to medium.

  Datacenter demand CAGR
    Current: 12% (IEA-derived)
    Impact: At 15-20%, total demand 5-15% higher. Slows SWB share but increases absolute deployment.
    Confidence: low.

### Medium Sensitivity Parameters
  Capacity factors (solar/wind)
    Current: Solar 0.11-0.15, Wind 0.21-0.32
    Impact: +/-10% CF shift changes generation from same capacity. Confidence: medium.

  Fossil fuel marginal costs
    Current: Coal $35-50, Gas $35-70 by region
    Impact: $10/MWh increase accelerates displacement 1-2 years. Confidence: medium to high.

  Heat pump electrification rate
    Current: Derived as residual
    Impact: Largest incremental component. If slower -> lower demand -> faster SWB share.
    Confidence: low to medium.

### Low Sensitivity Parameters

    Current: Slowly declining (existing hydro/nuclear/biomass)
    Impact: Modest -- declines ~1%/yr regardless. Confidence: medium.

  - Hydrogen Economy: Minimal for grid generation (low likelihood for electricity)
  - T&D Constraints: Could limit SWB in constrained grids (high locally, moderate globally)
  - Curtailment Economics: Actual SWB utilization < potential post-overcapacity (certain once SWB >100%)
  - Carbon Pricing: Would accelerate fossil displacement by 2-5 years (medium, EU ETS exists)
  - Policy Changes: Tariffs/subsidies shift timing +/-2-3 years (medium)
  - Long-duration Storage: Seasonal storage could extend SWB utilization (low before 2035)

## S09 -- PROVENANCE

### Data Sources
  Primary:
    - IEA Electricity Data: Historical electricity generation and demand by region (2006-2024)
    - IEA Energy & AI Report: Datacenter electricity demand and growth rate (12% CAGR from 2024)
    - IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics: Solar and wind installed capacity (2006-2024)

  Derived:
    - Battery Disruption Skill: EV fleet and electricity demand
    - Baseline Demand: Historical CAGR extrapolation from IEA data
    - Heat Pump Demand: Residual (Total - Baseline - EV - Datacenter, lower confidence)

### Model Parameters
  Source file: config/parameters.json
  Forecasting: start 2025, end 2040, last actual 2024, CAGR weights 3yr/5yr/10yr equal (0.333)
  Capacity factors: solar 0.11-0.15, wind onshore 0.21-0.32, wind offshore 0.38-0.42
  Marginal costs: coal $35-50/MWh, gas $35-70/MWh
  Battery: methodology solar_capacity, duration 2-4 hours by region

### Methodology
  1. Load historical energy generation (solar, wind, total)
  2. Calculate blended CAGR (3yr/5yr/10yr, equal weight)
  3. Project SWB capacity forward using blended CAGR
  4. Convert capacity to generation using regional capacity factors
  5. Size battery storage from solar capacity x duration hours
  6. Load total electricity demand (baseline + EV + DC + HP)
  7. Calculate residual demand (total - SWB - non-SWB)
  8. Allocate residual to fossil via merit-order (cheapest first)
  9. Compute market shares and identify displacement milestones

### Computation
  Skill: energy-sector
  Command: python3 run_forecast.py
  Output: validation/baseline/swb_results.json
  Deterministic: true
  Timestamp: Pre-computed baseline
