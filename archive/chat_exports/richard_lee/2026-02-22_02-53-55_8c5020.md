At this milestone, SWB generates 6,590,060 GWh annually while coal and gas generation drop to zero [C1]. Non-SWB sources (hydro, nuclear, biomass) contribute 1,015,831 GWh, bringing total generation to 7,605,892 GWh [C1].

By 2040, SWB market share reaches **283%**—meaning SWB capacity alone generates 2.83 times the electricity it would need to serve if it were the only source [C1]. This reflects the Clean Energy U-Curve optimization: it is economically rational to overbuild solar and wind generation capacity 3-5x beyond peak demand rather than build massive battery storage [C2][C3]. The system is sized for the worst-case demand period (coldest, darkest winter days), creating intentional surplus during the rest of the year [C2].

This is 183% of the base demand SWB is sized to serve—equivalent to nearly doubling the entire electricity supply with energy that costs effectively nothing to generate once infrastructure is built [C1][C3].

## Applications of Superpower

This massive surplus enables applications that were previously uneconomic [C3][C4]:

- **Desalination**: Industrial-scale freshwater production from seawater
- **Carbon removal**: Direct air capture and sequestration powered by free electricity
- **Heavy compute**: AI training, data centers, and computational workloads that can flex to surplus availability
- **Hydrogen production**: Electrolysis for industrial feedstock and energy storage
- **Materials processing**: Energy-intensive manufacturing (aluminum, steel, cement) that can time-shift to surplus periods

The economic logic is unambiguous: solar and wind costs have fallen so dramatically that building 3x generation capacity is cheaper than building equivalent battery storage to cover seasonal gaps [C2][C5]. This is not waste—it is optimal system design [C2]. The U-Curve creates a fundamentally different energy paradigm where the question shifts from "how do we allocate scarce energy?" to "what do we do with energy superabundance?" [C3][C6].
