## UK Unemployment, Disinflation & Bank of England Rate Path: 2026–2036

*Analysis scoped to the United Kingdom nationally. Sub-regional and sectoral scope assumptions stated explicitly where applied. Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics and empirical transmission relationships; regulatory/adoption lags excluded unless specifically noted.*

### Thesis

The UK labour market has crossed into meaningful negative territory relative to equilibrium: unemployment at 5.2% sits 1.1 percentage points above the OBR's NAIRU estimate of 4.1% [C1], generating a disinflation impulse that is already transmitting through private-sector wages but has not yet fully passed through to services CPI. The 0.3–0.5pp material contribution threshold will be crossed in the base case by Q1 2027, with a geopolitical energy shock now the primary variable determining whether the timeline compresses toward Q3 2026 or extends toward Q4 2027.

### 1. Phillips Curve: Threshold, Slope & Non-Linearity

**Threshold:** Labour market slack begins transmitting materially into wage disinflation when the unemployment gap (actual minus NAIRU) exceeds approximately 0.5–0.7pp on a sustained basis. The UK crossed this threshold in mid-2025 and now sits at a 1.1pp gap [C1].

**Slope estimates:**
- Pre-pandemic (1998–2019): approximately −0.25 per 1pp unemployment gap — meaning a 1.1pp gap generates a ~0.28pp wage disinflation impulse [C2]
- Post-pandemic (steeper, convex): approximately −0.45 per 1pp gap — generating a ~0.50pp impulse [C2]

**Non-linearity confirmed:** Bank of England staff research (Bunn et al., updated October 2025) uses micro-level panel data from UK and US firms to demonstrate that the Phillips Curve is **convex** — it steepens above potential and flattens below [C3]. This is not a symmetric relationship. OMFIF analysis (December 2025) finds that incorporating the steeper post-pandemic slope reduces the NAIRU required to explain 2023–2024 UK wage dynamics by 0.7–0.8pp [C4], implying the current 1.1pp gap is generating a disinflation impulse closer to the post-pandemic estimate of −0.50pp than the pre-pandemic −0.28pp.

**Flattening caveat:** The National Living Wage (NLW) at £12.21/hour (+6.7% from April 2025) creates a structural wage floor in low-wage services. High-NLW-exposure firms reported realised wage growth of 5.1% in the three months to November 2025, 1.0pp above low-exposure firms (4.1%) [C5]. This compresses the Phillips Curve's effective slope in hospitality, retail, and care sectors — the very sectors where services CPI is most sensitive to labour costs.

### 2. Current Unemployment Trajectory vs. Equilibrium

**Latest data (ONS, released 19 March 2026):**
- Unemployment rate: **5.2%** (November 2025–January 2026), up from 4.4% a year earlier — a +0.8pp year-on-year increase [C6]
- Unemployed persons: 1.87 million, up 323,000 year-on-year [C6]
- Payrolled employees: fell 96,000 (−0.3%) between January 2025 and January 2026 [C6]
- Vacancies: 721,000 (December 2025–February 2026), below pre-pandemic levels [C7]
- Vacancies-to-unemployed ratio: 2.5 (August–October 2025), highest since 2015 excluding the pandemic period [C8]

**Equilibrium estimates:**
- OBR NAIRU: **4.1%** (March 2026 EFO); unemployment expected to peak at ~5.33% in 2026 before returning to 4.1% by 2030 [C1]
- Bank of England: NAIRU range unresolved (4.0–5.0%); November 2025 MPR flagged the possibility that equilibrium unemployment has risen, citing Brexit-related supply constraints, but reached no firm conclusion [C4]
- Unemployment gap vs. OBR NAIRU: **+1.1pp** — the largest gap since the post-COVID period [C1]

**Youth unemployment:** 732,000 aged 16–24 unemployed (November 2025–January 2026), up 99,000 year-on-year; youth unemployment rate 16.0%, up from 14.5% [C6]. This cohort carries limited wage-setting power and does not materially affect aggregate AWE, but signals demand weakness is broad-based.

### 4. Structural Factors: Accelerators & Brakes

**Brakes on disinflation transmission:**

1. **NLW structural floor:** The 6.7% NLW increase to £12.21/hour (April 2025) creates a persistent 1.0pp wage growth differential between high- and low-NLW-exposure sectors [C5]. Hospitality, retail, and social care — which together account for a significant share of services CPI — face a binding wage floor that the unemployment gap cannot fully override. This is the single most important structural brake on disinflation transmission in 2026.

2. **Brexit labour supply composition shift:** Brexit reduced EU-origin employees by approximately **785,000 by 2024 (−2.6% of total workforce)**, partially offset by +992,000 non-EU workers (net: +207,000, +0.6%) [C18]. The compositional shift matters: EU workers were concentrated in flexible, lower-wage sectors (hospitality, agriculture, construction) where labour market tightness remains acute despite aggregate loosening. Non-EU workers are concentrated in healthcare and professional services — different sectoral distribution, different wage-setting dynamics.

3. **Immigration policy tightening:** Work-related non-EU migration slowed sharply in 2025 as the government tightened rules [C18]. This removes the safety valve that had partially offset Brexit-related tightness in specific sectors, sustaining above-average wage growth in healthcare and hospitality even as aggregate unemployment rises.

4. **Sectoral heterogeneity:** Healthcare, hospitality, and construction remain structurally tight despite aggregate loosening. The aggregate unemployment rate of 5.2% masks significant sectoral variation — the disinflation impulse from aggregate slack is diluted by persistent tightness in services-heavy sectors.

**Accelerators of disinflation transmission:**

1. **Employer NICs margin compression:** 64% of firms absorbed the April 2025 NICs increase via lower margins rather than higher prices [C17]. As margins compress, firms face stronger incentives to resist future wage demands — amplifying the unemployment gap's disinflationary effect on wage settlements.

2. **Vacancy collapse:** Vacancies at 721,000 (below pre-pandemic levels) signal that the labour market's forward-looking tightness is unwinding faster than the unemployment rate alone suggests [C7]. Workers' bargaining power is eroding.

3. **Public-sector base effect unwinding:** The 7.6% public-sector AWE figure (three months to November 2025) is a base-effect artefact from earlier-than-usual 2025 pay awards [C9]. As this unwinds in 2026, headline AWE will mechanically decelerate — accelerating the apparent disinflation signal even before private-sector dynamics fully transmit.

The 50% of displaced FTE that flows to productivity augmentation increases output per worker. GDP growth can remain positive even as employment falls — a configuration where growth and unemployment rise simultaneously. This complicates Bank of England policy: cutting rates risks re-igniting asset price inflation while unemployment remains structurally elevated [C1].

---

### 2.3 NLW Floor: The Primary Domestic Brake (Unchanged)

The National Living Wage at £12.21/hour (+6.7% from April 2025) creates a structural wage floor in low-wage services [C9]. High-NLW-exposure firms reported realised wage growth of 5.1% in the three months to November 2025, 1.0pp above low-exposure firms [C9]. This brake operates in hospitality, retail, and social care — sectors where the Artificial Labor displacement is also highest (rupture 2027). The NLW floor delays but does not prevent disinflation transmission: once displacement crosses 20–30% in retail and hospitality (2028–2029), the NLW floor becomes less binding as the remaining workforce is smaller and the cost-per-service-unit is dominated by AI, not human labor [C1].

---

### 2.4 Geopolitical Energy Shock: Exogenous Variable (Unchanged)

The March 2026 Middle East conflict caused a unanimous MPC hold at 3.75%, with BoE staff projecting the direct contribution of energy prices to CPI inflation in 2026 Q3 at approximately 0.75pp [C10]. This temporarily offsets the disinflation impulse from the labour market channel — raising headline CPI while simultaneously compressing real incomes and demand, which deepens the disinflationary labour market impulse. The energy shock accelerates the Artificial Labor disinflation channel even as it temporarily delays headline CPI convergence to target [C10].

### 2.6 Bank of England Policy Implication: Framework Obsolescence

The Artificial Labor disruption renders the NAIRU-based policy framework structurally obsolete by 2029–2031 [C2]. The traditional mechanism — raise rates to cool wage inflation, cut rates as unemployment rises — inverts:

- Unemployment rises to 11–15% (2029–2031) without generating wage pressure (AI wage ceiling effect)
- Services CPI decelerates toward 2.0% and below despite elevated unemployment
- The Bank of England faces a persistent below-target inflation environment with structurally elevated unemployment — a configuration for which the current policy toolkit has no precedent
- **Rate path implication:** Structurally lower Bank Rate through the 2030s, with the terminal rate potentially approaching 1.5–2.0% as the Artificial Labor disinflation channel dominates [C2]

The geopolitical energy shock (March 2026) creates a temporary stagflationary configuration that delays this dynamic by 2–4 quarters — but does not alter the structural trajectory [C10].
