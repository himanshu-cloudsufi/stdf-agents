## Foundational Assumptions: UK Artificial Labor Disruption Analysis

Before presenting the quantitative outputs, the model's foundational assumptions are stated explicitly. These are **SKILL OUTPUT** parameters unless labeled DERIVED.

---

### 1. Definition & Scope of Artificial Labor

Artificial labor is defined as AI inference systems (large language models, multimodal models, agentic AI) performing **cognitive subtasks** that currently require human workers. The model covers **341 occupations, 12,996 tasks, and 89,040 cognitive subtasks** drawn from the O*NET occupational database, scaled to the UK workforce. **Physical tasks are excluded** — manual assembly, patient lifting, construction labor, and physical logistics remain outside the automation envelope. The model captures only the cognitive layer of each occupation.

---

### 2. Occupational & Sectoral Coverage

The UK labor market is modeled at **33.5 million total workforce** [C1], scaled from the US O*NET occupational taxonomy using a **0.2342 scaling ratio** (UK/US workforce proportion) with a **+5% upward adjustment** for the UK's higher service-sector concentration relative to the US. Sectors covered include healthcare, office & admin, sales, transport & logistics, management, education, food service, financial services, IT, construction, manufacturing, legal, and media.

---

### 3. Automation Timeline: METR-Based Capability Progression

AI task capability is modeled using the empirically validated **METR doubling curve**: capability doubles every **7 months**, starting from a baseline of **1,560 seconds (26 minutes)** in January 2025 [C2]. This is the primary gating mechanism — subtasks with durations exceeding current AI capability are excluded from automation regardless of cost. The capability unlock schedule is:

| Date | AI Capability | Tasks Unlocked |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| Jan 2025 | 26 min | Data entry, email, basic queries |
| Jan 2026 | 1.4 hours | Financial analysis, short code review |
| Jul 2027 | 8.5 hours | Full-workday cognitive tasks |
| Jan 2028 | 15.3 hours | Multi-session research, complex reports |
| Jan 2029 | 50.2 hours | Multi-day project work |
| Jan 2030 | 164.9 hours | Week-long analyses, complex legal work |
| Jan 2031 | 541 hours | Month-long strategic planning |

AI inference costs decline at **70% annually** from a 2025 baseline of $5.00/million tokens, with a floor of $0.01/million tokens [C3].

---

### 4. Substitution Dynamics: Task-Level Displacement

Displacement operates at the **subtask level**, not the job level. Each occupation's 89,040 subtasks are individually assessed against the current AI capability threshold. A job is partially displaced when a fraction of its subtasks become automatable — the displacement rate for each occupation equals the share of its cognitive subtask-hours that AI can perform. The model applies a **50/50 split** between:
- **Replacement FTEs**: Direct headcount reduction (role eliminated)
- **Productivity FTEs**: Augmentation (worker remains, output increases)

This split reflects the empirical observation that organizations adopt AI both to reduce headcount and to amplify remaining workers' output. The **80% replacement ceiling** reflects the irreducible physical and relational components of most occupations.

---

### 5. Macroeconomic Feedback Assumptions

The model applies an **S-curve adoption schedule** post-tipping: 10% adoption floor rising to 80% ceiling over approximately 4 years. The cost tipping threshold (AI < 50% of human labor cost) was crossed for **every UK sector in 2025** [C4] — the adoption constraint is now purely organizational and capability-based. The model does **not** endogenously model new job creation offsets or wage elasticity effects; these are addressed in the unemployment reabsorption scenarios as external parameters. Market expansion effects (demand superabundance from lower-cost AI services) are acknowledged but not modeled within the skill's scope.

---

### 6. UK-Specific Structural Factors

The UK adjustment factors applied are: (a) **+5% service-sector concentration premium** relative to the US baseline; (b) **£16.50/hour average total employment cost** (wages + National Insurance + benefits) [C5]; (c) **3.9% baseline unemployment rate** (ONS 2024) [C6]. No policy-environment constraints are applied — the analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory or adoption lags are excluded.

---

## SKILL OUTPUT (DETERMINISTIC): Core Displacement Results

**Economy-Wide Displacement by 2036:** 12.1 million FTE displaced from the UK workforce, representing a **34.4% economy-wide displacement rate** [C7].

**The rupture window is 2027–2030.** In this 3-year span, **8.5 million additional UK FTEs are displaced** — the steepest labor market disruption in UK economic history [C8]. The trigger is AI capability crossing the full-workday cognitive threshold (8+ hours) in mid-2027.

## Unemployment Impact (DERIVED — Reabsorption Scenarios)

> The displacement skill provides FTE displaced. Unemployment rate requires additional assumptions about reabsorption. The following applies three reabsorption rates to the **replacement FTE** component (50% of total displaced = 6.05M by 2036) [C11].

| Year | Replacement FTEs | Low Reabsorption (15%/yr) | Moderate (25%/yr) | High (40%/yr) |
|------|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Baseline | — | 3.9% | 3.9% | 3.9% |
| 2026 | 1,069,292 | 6.6% | 6.3% | 5.8% |
| 2027 | 2,136,558 | 9.3% | 8.7% | 7.7% |
| 2028 | 3,435,939 | 12.6% | 11.6% | 10.0% |
| 2029 | 4,530,451 | 15.4% | 14.0% | 12.0% |
| 2030 | 5,297,853 | 17.3% | 15.7% | 13.4% |
| 2036 | 6,054,736 | 19.2% | 17.4% | 14.7% |

Under the **moderate reabsorption scenario**, UK unemployment reaches **11.6% by 2028** — exceeding the post-2008 financial crisis peak of 8.5% [C12]. Even under the **high reabsorption scenario** (aggressive reskilling, rapid new job creation), unemployment reaches **10.0% by 2028** and plateaus near **14.7% by 2036**.

## Sector Disruption Highlights [C13]

**Immediate crisis (2026):** Retail sales workers (10.8% displaced), cashiers (10.8%), waiters (12.1%), nursing assistants (11.5%), and HGV drivers (10.5%) are already past tipping. These are the UK's largest low-wage occupation groups.

**Delayed but steep (2028–2030):** Management (0.7% → 38.0%), business & financial (1.6% → 38.7%), legal services (1.1% → 38.5%), and software developers (1.2% → 37.3%) show low 2026 rates but steep acceleration as AI capability crosses the 8–40 hour task threshold in 2027–2028.

**NHS exposure:** With 2.8 million healthcare workers and a 34.9% displacement rate by 2036, the NHS faces ~977,000 FTE displaced in cognitive roles — documentation, scheduling, diagnostic support, and administrative functions [C14].

## Productivity Dividend (DERIVED) [C15]

The same AI systems that displace workers generate a **£129.7 billion annual productivity gain** by 2036 (5.6% of 2024 UK GDP), with cumulative gains of **£1.12 trillion** over 2026–2036. This accrues primarily to capital owners and high-skill augmented workers — it does not automatically offset the unemployment impact for displaced workers.
