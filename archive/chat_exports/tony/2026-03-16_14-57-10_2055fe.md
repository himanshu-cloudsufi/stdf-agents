## Ras Laffan LNG: Drone Threat, Disruption Scale, and Market Impact

*Scope: Qatar as global LNG supplier; regional markets: Middle East, Europe, Asia, Americas; time horizon 2026–2036. Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded unless specifically requested.*

---

### The Framing Problem: The Wrong Question Reveals the Right Risk

The question of how many Shahed drones it takes to incapacitate Ras Laffan is the wrong unit of analysis. The correct question is: **what is the economic consequence of a Ras Laffan disruption in a market that is already in structural decline?** The answer is counterintuitive — a major disruption in 2026 produces an acute price spike but *accelerates* the very demand destruction that makes Ras Laffan a stranded asset by the mid-2030s. The attack scenario is simultaneously catastrophic in the short run and self-defeating for LNG incumbents in the long run.

---

### Part 1: Infrastructure Vulnerability — What the Abqaiq Precedent Tells Us

The canonical benchmark for drone/missile attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure is the September 2019 Abqaiq-Khurais attack. Approximately 18 drones and cruise missiles struck Saudi Aramco's Abqaiq processing facility — the single largest oil processing node on earth, handling ~7% of global oil supply (~5.7 million barrels/day). The attack temporarily removed ~50% of Saudi Arabia's production capacity. Recovery to full capacity took approximately 2–3 weeks for the processing facility, though full stabilization of downstream systems took ~6 weeks [C1].

The Abqaiq precedent establishes three critical parameters for Ras Laffan analysis:

1. **Concentration of critical nodes**: Abqaiq was a single-point-of-failure for Saudi oil processing. Ras Laffan is similarly concentrated — it is the world's largest LNG export complex, housing all of Qatar's ~95 MTPA (2026) export capacity on a single industrial peninsula [C2].
2. **Recovery asymmetry by component**: Processing equipment (compressors, heat exchangers, stabilization columns) recovers in weeks if structural damage is limited. Liquefaction trains — the core of LNG production — are far more complex. A direct strike on a liquefaction train's main cryogenic heat exchanger (MCHE) would require 12–24 months for replacement, as these are custom-fabricated components with no spare inventory [C3].
3. **Drone quantity is not the binding constraint**: The Abqaiq attack used ~18 projectiles to achieve a 50% production disruption at the world's most hardened energy facility. The binding constraint is not drone count but **targeting precision and component vulnerability**. A small number of precision strikes on the right components (MCHE units, gas turbine compressors, or LNG storage tank pressure relief systems) achieves disproportionate disruption relative to the number of munitions.

**Ras Laffan's specific vulnerability profile:**
- **14+ LNG trains** (QG1–QG7 legacy trains + NFE/NFS expansion trains) arranged in parallel — this provides partial redundancy; destroying 3–4 trains removes ~25–30% of capacity [C2]
- **Storage tanks**: ~30 full-containment LNG tanks, each holding ~180,000 m³. A breach of a full-containment tank triggers a boiloff event but does not necessarily cascade — the outer concrete containment wall limits blast propagation [C3]
- **Loading berths**: 14 berths serving ~400 LNG carrier loadings per year. Berth destruction halts exports even if liquefaction capacity is intact — this is the highest-leverage, lowest-hardness target [C3]
- **Utility systems**: Power generation, seawater cooling, and nitrogen supply are shared infrastructure. A strike on the central power station or cooling water intake disables multiple trains simultaneously

**Disruption scale by target type:**

| Target | Drones Required (est.) | Capacity Impact | Recovery Timeline |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|------------------|
| 1–2 loading berths | 4–8 | 15–25% export halt | 3–6 months |
| 2–3 liquefaction trains (MCHE) | 6–12 | 15–20% production loss | 12–24 months |
| Central power/cooling utility | 8–15 | 60–80% production halt | 2–4 months |
| 4–6 trains + utility systems | 20–35 | 40–60% production loss | 18–36 months |
| Full complex (all trains + berths) | 60–100+ | ~95% production halt | 36–60 months |

*Note: Drone count estimates are derived from Abqaiq precedent scaling (18 drones → 50% disruption at a comparable facility) and publicly available technical literature on LNG train architecture. These are analytical estimates, not classified assessments.*

**Conclusion on the user hypothesis**: A "several months" incapacitation of Ras Laffan at meaningful scale (>50% capacity) requires targeting utility systems or multiple liquefaction trains with precision munitions — not a mass Shahed swarm. The Shahed-136 loitering munition carries a ~50 kg warhead, sufficient to damage exposed process equipment but insufficient to breach reinforced LNG storage tanks or destroy hardened compressor buildings without multiple direct hits on the same target. A coordinated attack of 20–40 Shahed-136s targeting utility infrastructure and 2–3 liquefaction train compressor halls could plausibly achieve a 40–60% production disruption lasting 3–6 months. Achieving a "several months" near-total incapacitation requires either a much larger salvo (60–100+ munitions) or the use of heavier ballistic missiles alongside loitering munitions — consistent with the threat profile of the current Iran conflict context [C1][W6].

### Part 2: Qatar's Market Position — Dominant and Structurally Stranded

Qatar's Ras Laffan complex expands to 95 MTPA in 2026 and 110 MTPA by 2027 (North Field Expansion + North Field South), representing 22.6% of global LNG trade in 2026 [C4].

The structural paradox: Qatar's share of global LNG trade *rises* from 22.6% (2026) to 59.5% (2036) — not because Qatar wins the market, but because the market is being destroyed by SWB cost-curve dynamics [C4]. Global LNG trade falls from 420 MTPA (2026) to 185 MTPA (2036) as gas power generation is displaced by SWB S-curve adoption — from 3,556 TWh (2026) to zero by 2036 [C5]. This displacement follows S-curve dynamics: slow initial displacement through the late 2020s, accelerating through the early 2030s as SWB cost advantage becomes overwhelming, reaching full displacement by 2036.

Oil and gas revenues represent ~20% of Qatar's GDP [C6]. The fiscal exposure is not to a drone attack — it is to the structural demand collapse that makes Ras Laffan's 110 MTPA of capacity a stranded asset regardless of whether it is physically damaged.

### Part 4: The SWB Acceleration Effect — Why the Disruption Is Self-Defeating for LNG

A USD 35–38/MMBtu LNG price makes SWB + battery economics overwhelmingly superior for every gas-dependent power buyer in Asia and Europe. The mechanism:

1. **Europe exits permanently**: European gas power generation declines along an S-curve toward zero by 2033 regardless of Qatar disruption [C5]. A price shock accelerates SWB deployment, compressing this timeline by 2–3 years.
2. **Asia accelerates SWB**: Asia's LNG demand peaks at ~290 MTPA in 2026–2028 and declines as SWB S-curve adoption accelerates. A price shock pulls forward the demand destruction curve by 2–4 years [C4].
3. **The long-run price ceiling is SWB LCOE**: Once SWB + battery delivers firm power at USD 40–60/MWh (~USD 8–12/MMBtu gas equivalent), no rational buyer pays above that level for LNG. The price ceiling is set by the disruptor, not by supply dynamics [C5].

Global gas power generation follows S-curve displacement: 3,556 TWh (2026) → 2,874 TWh (2030) → 1,276 TWh (2034) → **zero (2036)** [C5]. The S-curve inflection point occurs in the early 2030s as SWB cost advantage crosses the threshold where gas power cannot compete on new-build economics. This displacement is driven by SWB cost-curve dynamics — the disruption accelerates it but does not cause it.

### Part 5: Qatar's Fiscal and Geopolitical Exposure

Qatar's hydrocarbon revenues represent ~20% of GDP [C6]. The fiscal break-even oil price for Gulf states is structurally exposed to demand collapse — national oil companies may continue making uneconomic investments in anticipation of price recovery [C8], but the cost-curve dynamics of SWB make that recovery structurally impossible beyond the mid-2030s.

The geopolitical risk is therefore two-layered:
- **Short-run**: A drone/missile attack on Ras Laffan in the context of the current Iran conflict [W1][W6][W10] produces an acute price spike and a supply crisis lasting 6–36 months depending on targeting precision and component damage
- **Long-run**: The structural demand destruction from SWB makes Ras Laffan's 110 MTPA capacity a stranded asset regardless of physical security — Qatar holds 59.5% of a 185 MTPA market by 2036 [C4], a market that is itself in terminal decline toward zero in the 2040s
