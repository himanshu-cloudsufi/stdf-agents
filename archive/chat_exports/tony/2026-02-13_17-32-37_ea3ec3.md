USA unemployment will NOT reach 7% based on job displacement alone. The question conflates two distinct metrics: **replacement rate** (jobs displaced by AI) and **unemployment rate** (percentage of labor force without work).

## SKILL OUTPUT (DETERMINISTIC)

- **AI capability**: 7-month doubling (METR empirical)

- **Tipping threshold**: AI cost < 50% of human labor cost
- **Adoption schedule**: S-curve post-tipping (5% → 80% over 4 years)
- **Replacement ceiling**: 80% (human-in-the-loop minimum)
- **Replacement/productivity split**: 50/50

## CRITICAL DISTINCTION: REPLACEMENT ≠ UNEMPLOYMENT

The skill outputs **replacement rate** (percentage of jobs displaced). This is NOT unemployment rate. Converting displacement to unemployment requires three assumptions the user has not confirmed:

### 1. Displacement-to-Unemployment Conversion Factor

What percentage of displaced workers become unemployed vs re-employed? Historical precedent varies:

- **Industrial Revolution mechanization**: Hand-spinners experienced severe localized displacement, but aggregate unemployment did not spike catastrophically [W1][W5]. Workers transitioned to factory employment, though adjustment was neither instantaneous nor painless [W2][W4].
- **Adjustment speed**: Some workers transitioned within years; older, less mobile workers faced permanent income losses and prolonged unemployment [W4][W6].
- **Asymmetric impacts**: Technology-driven displacement creates transitional costs even when long-term employment stabilizes [W2][W4].

**Suggested range**: 30–80% (varies by sector, speed, demographic factors).

### 2. Labor Force Denominator

The model covers 93.08M jobs; the US labor force is ~168M (Jan 2026 baseline) [W1]. Displacement in modeled sectors affects the broader economy through cascading effects.

### 3. Baseline Unemployment

Current unemployment: **4.3%** (Jan 2026) [W1][W2][W4]. The US economy added 130,000 jobs in January 2026 [W2][W4]. New displacement adds to this baseline.
