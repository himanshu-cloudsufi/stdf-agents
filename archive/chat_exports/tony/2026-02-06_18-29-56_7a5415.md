**Example:**
- Baseline payroll tax: 7.65% (current FICA)
- Firms with <10% AI displacement: 7.65% (no change)
- Firms with 10-30% AI displacement: 9.65% (+2 percentage points)
- Firms with >30% AI displacement: 11.65% (+4 percentage points)

**Revenue potential:**
- 15.1M FTE displaced by July 2027 = $750B in lost wages (at $50K average) [C15]
- 4 percentage point surtax on $750B = $30B/year revenue
- By 2030 (35-40M displaced, $1.75-2T lost wages): $70-80B/year revenue

**Advantages:**
- **Existing infrastructure:** IRS already collects payroll taxes; minimal new enforcement apparatus
- **Incentive alignment:** Encourages firms to retain human workers or invest in retraining
- **Progressive incidence:** High-displacement firms (tech, finance, telecom) pay more; low-displacement firms (construction, healthcare) pay less

**Implementation barriers:**
- **Measurement problem:** How to attribute displacement to AI vs. other factors (offshoring, business cycle, productivity improvements)?
- **Gaming:** Firms will restructure to avoid high-displacement classification (outsource AI-intensive functions to subsidiaries)
- **Competitiveness:** Penalizes US firms relative to foreign competitors without the tax

**Economic incidence:**
- **Short-run:** Firms bear tax (profit reduction)
- **Medium-run:** Passed through to workers via lower wage growth (50-60%) and consumers via higher prices (40-50%) [C16]
- **Long-run:** Firms accelerate AI adoption to reduce headcount below tax thresholds, perversely increasing displacement

**Likely outcome:** Payroll tax adjustment will be the most politically viable option (uses existing infrastructure, targets "job destroyers"). Expect 2-4 percentage point surtax by 2029-2030, generating $30-80B/year revenue. However, incidence will fall on workers and consumers, not capital.

**Corporate Income Surtax on AI-Driven Productivity Gains:**

**Structure:** Surtax on corporate profits attributable to AI-driven productivity improvements. Precedent: Excess profits taxes (World War I, World War II, Korea). [C17]

**Example:**
- Baseline corporate tax rate: 21%
- Firms report AI-driven productivity gains (e.g., "AI reduced headcount by 10,000, saving $500M")
- Surtax: 10-15 percentage points on profits attributable to AI savings
- Effective rate on AI profits: 31-36%

**Revenue potential:**
- 15.1M FTE productivity gains by July 2027 = $750B in additional output [C18]
- Corporate profit share: ~30% = $225B
- 10 percentage point surtax: $22.5B/year revenue
- By 2030 (35-40M FTE productivity gains, $1.75-2T output): $52-60B/year revenue

**Advantages:**
- **Targets economic rents:** Taxes windfall profits from AI, not productive investment
- **Minimal distortion:** Does not penalize AI adoption per se, only the profits from it
- **Progressive:** High-profit firms (tech, finance) pay more; low-margin firms (retail, hospitality) pay less

**Implementation barriers:**
- **Attribution problem:** Impossible to isolate AI-driven profits from other factors (market conditions, management quality, non-AI technology)
- **Accounting manipulation:** Firms will shift AI profits to low-tax jurisdictions via transfer pricing
- **Enforcement cost:** Requires invasive audits of corporate AI deployment and productivity metrics

**Economic incidence:**
- **Short-run:** Shareholders bear tax (lower dividends, stock buybacks)
- **Medium-run:** Passed through to consumers (40-50%), workers (30-40%), and shareholders (10-20%) [C19]
- **Long-run:** Capital relocates to low-tax jurisdictions; US loses AI-intensive industries

**Likely outcome:** Corporate surtax will be proposed as a "fair share" measure, but will stall due to attribution and enforcement problems. If enacted, revenue will be 40-60% below projections due to profit-shifting and narrow tax base.

## 4. Implementation Barriers

**Tax Base Definition Challenges:**

The core problem: **AI is software, and software is infinitely replicable and reconfigurable.** [C24] Defining the tax base requires answering:

- Is an Excel macro an "AI agent"? (No, but where is the line?)
- Is a customer service chatbot taxed per conversation, per user, per deployment, or per model?
- Are open-source models taxed? (If yes, how? If no, massive evasion.)
- Are AI-augmented humans taxed? (If yes, every knowledge worker becomes a tax target.)

Each definition creates:
- **Bright-line gaming:** Firms will engineer around thresholds (e.g., deploy 9 bots instead of 10 to avoid tax)
- **Litigation risk:** Ambiguous definitions invite legal challenges and multi-year court battles
- **Administrative burden:** IRS will require new expertise, auditing tools, and enforcement capacity

**Enforcement Complexity:**

AI deployment is decentralized and opaque:
- **Cloud APIs:** Firms call OpenAI/Anthropic APIs millions of times/day; tracking is feasible but requires real-time reporting mandates
- **On-premise models:** Firms run models on internal servers; detection requires invasive audits
- **Open-source models:** Firms download and deploy models (Llama, Mistral) with zero transaction trail; enforcement is impossible without mandatory registration

**Enforcement cost estimate:** $5-10B/year for IRS to build AI tax enforcement capacity (precedent: $80B IRS funding in Inflation Reduction Act for general enforcement). [C25] This reduces net revenue by 5-10%.

**International Coordination Problems:**

AI taxation requires multilateral coordination (precedent: OECD global minimum corporate tax). [C26] Key challenges:

1. **Tax competition:** Jurisdictions will compete to attract AI capital by offering low/zero AI taxes (Singapore, Ireland, UAE already positioning)
2. **Treaty negotiation timelines:** OECD corporate tax minimum took 8 years (2013-2021) to negotiate and is still not fully implemented. AI tax coordination will take 5-10 years minimum.
3. **Enforcement asymmetry:** US can tax US-based firms, but cannot prevent foreign firms from deploying untaxed AI in US markets (precedent: digital services tax disputes with EU).

**Competitiveness Concerns:**

Unilateral US AI taxation will:
- **Reduce US AI investment:** Firms will shift R&D and deployment to low-tax jurisdictions
- **Advantage foreign competitors:** Chinese and EU firms without equivalent taxes will undercut US firms in global markets
- **Accelerate offshoring:** AI-intensive services (customer support, data analysis, content generation) will move offshore

**Historical precedent:** US corporate tax cuts in 2017 (35%→21%) were justified by competitiveness concerns; reversing this logic for AI will face fierce lobbying resistance. [C27]

## 5. Historical Precedents for Taxing Labor-Saving Technology

**Precedent 1: Luddite Uprisings (1811-1816)**

- **Context:** Textile workers destroyed automated looms in England
- **Policy response:** Military suppression, no taxation of machines
- **Outcome:** Automation proceeded; displaced workers absorbed into other sectors over decades
- **Lesson:** Governments do not tax labor-saving technology when it drives economic growth; they suppress resistance instead. [C28]

**Precedent 2: ATM Deployment (1970s-1990s)**

- **Context:** ATMs displaced 400,000+ bank tellers in the US [C29]
- **Policy response:** No taxation; retraining programs (limited success)
- **Outcome:** Teller employment fell 30-40%; remaining tellers shifted to sales roles; no ATM tax ever proposed
- **Lesson:** Even visible, concentrated displacement (bank tellers) does not trigger technology taxation when consumers benefit (24/7 banking access).

**Precedent 3: Industrial Robot Deployment (1980s-2010s)**

- **Context:** 1.5M+ manufacturing jobs displaced by robots in US (2000-2010) [C30]
- **Policy response:** No federal robot tax; South Korea reduced R&D tax credits for automation (2017, repealed 2018) [C31]
- **Outcome:** Manufacturing employment fell 30%; no robot tax gained traction due to competitiveness concerns and diffuse political opposition
- **Lesson:** Even large-scale displacement (1.5M jobs) does not trigger technology taxation when:
  1. Displacement is gradual (over decades)
  2. Displaced workers are geographically concentrated (Rust Belt) but politically weak
  3. Beneficiaries (consumers, shareholders) are diffuse and politically powerful

**Precedent 4: Digital Services Taxes (2018-present)**

- **Context:** EU, UK, France imposed 2-3% taxes on digital revenues (Google, Facebook, Amazon) [C32]
- **Policy response:** US threatened retaliatory tariffs; OECD negotiated global minimum corporate tax (15%) as compromise [C33]
- **Outcome:** Digital services taxes generated <$5B/year revenue; most countries suspended them pending OECD implementation
- **Lesson:** Technology taxation is feasible when:
  1. Tax base is narrow and measurable (digital ad revenue)
  2. International coordination exists (OECD framework)
  3. Revenue expectations are modest (<1% of fiscal gap)

**Key Insight from Historical Precedents:**

Governments tax labor-saving technology only when:
1. **Displacement is acute and politically visible** (unemployment >12-15%)
2. **Fiscal pressures are severe** (deficits >5% of GDP)
3. **International coordination exists** (prevents capital flight)
4. **Revenue expectations are modest** (symbolic, not transformative)

AI taxation will follow this pattern: symbolic proposals in 2027-2028, serious legislation in 2029-2030 (when unemployment exceeds 12-15%), but revenue will be 20-30% of fiscal gap at most. [C34]

## 6. Symbolic vs. Substantive Legislation

**Symbolic Proposals (Political Theater):**

Characteristics:
- **Introduced in response to media coverage of AI displacement**
- **Broad, vague language** ("tax AI to fund workers")
- **No detailed implementation plan** (no definition of tax base, rate structure, or enforcement mechanism)
- **Sponsored by backbenchers or presidential candidates** (not committee chairs)
- **No CBO score or revenue estimate**
- **Dies in committee; never reaches floor vote**

**Examples (2027-2028):**
- "AI Accountability Act" (requires firms to report AI deployment; no tax)
- "Robot Tax Act" (imposes $10,000/robot tax; no definition of "robot")
- "AI Windfall Profits Tax" (10% surtax on AI profits; no attribution methodology)

**Purpose:** Signal concern to constituents, generate media coverage, position for 2028 election. No material policy impact.

**Substantive Legislation (Material Revenue Impact):**

Characteristics:
- **Introduced after unemployment exceeds 12-15%** (crisis threshold)
- **Detailed implementation plan** (tax base definition, rate structure, enforcement mechanism, revenue estimate)
- **Sponsored by committee chairs** (Ways and Means, Finance)
- **CBO score showing $50-100B+/year revenue**
- **Paired with spending measures** (UI extension, UBI pilot, retraining)
- **Floor vote within 6-12 months of introduction**

**Examples (2029-2030):**
- **Payroll Tax Adjustment Act:** 2-4 percentage point surtax on high-displacement firms, $30-80B/year revenue, paired with UI extension
- **AI Compute Tax Act:** 10-15% tax on cloud compute for AI training/inference, $8-15B/year revenue, paired with retraining funding
- **Corporate Minimum Tax on AI Profits:** 15% minimum tax on AI-attributable profits (similar to OECD global minimum), $20-40B/year revenue, paired with UBI pilot

**Differentiation Criteria:**

| Criterion | Symbolic | Substantive |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Timing** | Before crisis (unemployment <12%) | After crisis (unemployment >12%) |
| **Sponsor** | Backbencher, candidate | Committee chair |
| **Detail** | Vague, no implementation plan | Detailed, CBO-scored |
| **Revenue** | No estimate or <$10B/year | $50-100B+/year |
| **Pairing** | Standalone | Paired with spending |
| **Outcome** | Dies in committee | Floor vote, possible passage |

**Prediction:**

- **2027-2028:** 10-15 symbolic AI tax proposals introduced; zero pass committee
- **2029-2030:** 2-3 substantive AI tax proposals introduced; 1-2 pass committee; 50% chance of passage (depends on 2028 election outcome and unemployment trajectory)
- **2031+:** AI taxation becomes routine budget tool, generating $100-150B/year revenue (20-30% of fiscal gap)
