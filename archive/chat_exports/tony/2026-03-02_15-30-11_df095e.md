## Economic Drivers

The deployment capacity is driven by three converging cost-curve dynamics:

1. **AI Capability Growth:** AI can now reliably handle tasks up to 2.1 hours in duration (7,560 seconds), up from 0.43 hours in January 2025 [C1]. Capability doubles every 7 months following METR empirical data [C1].

2. **AI Cost Collapse:** Inference costs have declined to $1.35 per million tokens in March 2026, a 73% reduction from the $5.00 baseline in January 2025 [C1]. Costs decline 70% annually [C1].

3. **Tipping Point Acceleration:** When AI cost drops below 50% of human labor cost for a given task, adoption accelerates from 5% (pre-tipping floor) to the full S-curve schedule [C1]. By March 2026, 7.34% of cognitive tasks have crossed this threshold [C1].

## Trajectory

Deployment capacity is accelerating exponentially:

The doubling reflects the compounding effect of capability growth (7-month doubling) and cost decline (70% annual) converging to unlock new tipping points each month. As more tasks cross the 50% cost threshold, adoption jumps from 5% to 10-20% within the first year post-tipping [C1].

## Methodology

The calculation applies a seven-step bottom-up model [C1]:

1. **Capability Gating:** Only cognitive subtasks within AI capability threshold (2.1 hours as of March 2026) are automatable [C1]
2. **Cost-Parity Tipping:** Adoption accelerates when AI cost < 50% of human cost per task iteration [C1]
3. **S-Curve Adoption:** Post-tipping adoption follows empirical schedule (Year 0: 5%, Year 1: 10%, Year 2: 20%, Year 3: 40%, Year 4+: 80%) [C1]
4. **Hours Freed:** Automation frees worker hours = task time × adoption rate × task weight [C1]
5. **Replacement Split:** 50% of freed hours → replacement, 50% → productivity [C1]
6. **FTE Conversion:** Hours freed ÷ standard work hours = FTE equivalent [C1]
7. **Ceiling Applied:** Maximum 80% replacement per occupation (human-in-the-loop minimum); overflow redirected to productivity [C1]

**Scope:** 341 occupations, 143,066,500 workers, USA geography, cognitive tasks only (physical tasks excluded) [C1].

## Assumptions & Limitations

This analysis outputs FTE displaced and productivity gains—NOT unemployment rate [C1]. Unemployment requires additional factors not modeled here: new job creation, labor force reabsorption, and non-cognitive job dynamics [C1]. The 10.5 million figure represents deployment capacity based on current AI economics and capability, not actual labor market outcomes.

Automation is constrained to cognitive subtasks within the 341-occupation dataset [C1]. Physical tasks, creative work requiring human judgment beyond the capability threshold, and occupations outside the dataset are excluded [C1]. The 80% replacement ceiling enforces a human-in-the-loop minimum; no occupation can be fully automated [C1].

The cost-curve dynamics driving this deployment capacity show no signs of slowing. AI capability continues doubling every 7 months, and inference costs continue declining 70% annually [C1]. By December 2026, deployment capacity will reach 17.4 million digital workers—a 65% increase from March 2026 levels [C1].
