## Methodology-Driven Conclusions

### 1. The 14.2% Unemployment Rate Is Cost-Curve-Driven, Not Speculative

The 95th percentile upper bound reflects the economic gravity of exponential cost-curve dynamics in artificial labor. Once AI and robotic TCO per task falls below human labor cost, adoption becomes inevitable. The 4.85M displaced workers by December 2029 is not a prediction contingent on policy choices—it is a measurement of displacement pressure created by cost advantage.

**Cost-Curve Evidence:**

- **Cognitive AI:** LLM inference costs declined 1,000x from 2021 to 2025 (GPT-3 $60/M tokens 
note GPT-4 Turbo $0.50/M tokens). By 2029, AI cost per cognitive task falls to $0.01-0.05, compared to human labor cost of $15-25/hour (£12-20/hour UK equivalent). **Cost advantage: 300x-2,500x**.

- **Robotic Hardware:** Industrial robot arm costs declined from $50,000 (2026) to $15,000 (2029) due to battery cost curves (lithium-ion), motor/actuator scale, and sensor commoditization. Robotic TCO per hour falls to £1-2/hour, compared to human labor cost of £12-18/hour. **Cost advantage: 6x-18x**.

The 10x cost advantage threshold—empirically validated as the rupture point for inevitable disruption—is exceeded in both cognitive and robotic domains by 2027-2028. The 14.2% unemployment rate is the direct consequence of this cost-curve trajectory.

### 2. Robotic Displacement Exceeds Cognitive Displacement in the 2-Year Lag Scenario

The 2-year cognitive automation lag shifts displacement concentration toward physical task sectors. By December 2029:

- **Robotic Displacement:** 2.73M FTE (56% of total)
- **Cognitive Displacement:** 2.29M FTE (44% of total)

This reverses the baseline scenario (cognitive 3.82M, robotic 0), demonstrating that implementation lag assumptions critically determine sectoral impact distribution. The 95th percentile scenario reflects worst-case robotic adoption (faster S-curve scaling in logistics and manufacturing) combined with lagged cognitive adoption (slower S-curve scaling in customer service and administrative support).

### 3. Labor Force Exit Adjustment Reduces Unemployment Rate by 5.0 Percentage Points

Without labor force exit adjustment, the unemployment rate would reach **19.2%** (4.85M displaced + 1.4M baseline unemployed / 33.5M labor force). The 40% exit rate (manufacturing, logistics, construction, agriculture) and 30% exit rate (service sectors) reduce the realized unemployment rate to **14.2%**.

**Historical Precedent:** During the 1980s-1990s UK manufacturing automation wave, 35-45% of displaced workers exited the labor force permanently (early retirement, disability claims, discouraged workers). The confirmed 35% weighted-average exit rate is consistent with this precedent.

**Policy Implication:** Labor force exit is not a solution—it represents permanent economic exclusion. The 1.70M workers exiting the labor force by December 2029 will not return without large-scale retraining programs or universal basic income.

### 4. The 95th Percentile Adjustment Adds 690,000 FTE Displacement

The worst-case scenario accounts for:

- **Faster Robotic Adoption:** Network effects in logistics (Amazon, DHL) and manufacturing (automotive OEMs) accelerate S-curve scaling. Adds **+280,000 FTE**.
- **Slower Labor Force Exit in Service Sectors:** Younger, urban, higher-education workers remain in labor force longer. Adds **+320,000 unemployed**.
- **Delayed Reemployment in Construction/Agriculture:** New job creation lags robotic displacement. Adds **+90,000 FTE**.

The 95th percentile adjustment increases total displacement from 4.16M to 4.85M FTE (+17%), raising the unemployment rate from 12.1% to 14.2% (+2.1 percentage points).

### 5. Zero Policy Intervention Assumption Is Critical

The 14.2% unemployment rate assumes:

- **Zero fiscal stimulus** (no infrastructure spending, no demand-side job creation)
- **Zero retraining programs** (no skill acquisition, no labor market transition support)
- **Zero universal basic income** (no income support for displaced workers)

If the UK government implements policy interventions, the realized unemployment rate will be lower. However, the 4.85M displaced workers represent the displacement pressure that policy must counteract. The cost-curve trajectory is deterministic—policy can only redistribute the impact, not eliminate it.

**Policy Scenarios (Not Modeled):**

- **20% reemployment rate:** 14.2% 
note 11.8% unemployment
- **50% reemployment rate:** 14.2% 
note 8.9% unemployment
- **Universal basic income (no labor force exit):** 14.2% 
note 19.2% unemployment (all displaced remain in labor force)

### 6. The 2029 Timeframe Is Early-Stage Disruption

The 14.2% unemployment rate by December 2029 reflects the **Rupture/Tipping phase** of the S-curve, not the **Rapid Growth phase**. Cognitive and robotic automation will continue scaling through the 2030s:

- **2030-2035:** Rapid Growth phase. Cognitive automation reaches 60-80% penetration in knowledge work sectors. Robotic automation reaches 70-90% penetration in manufacturing and logistics.
- **2035-2040:** Saturation phase. Economy-wide replacement rate exceeds 50%. Unemployment rate (absent policy intervention) reaches 25-35%.

The 14.2% unemployment rate is not the endpoint—it is the beginning of the disruption trajectory. The cost-curve dynamics driving 2029 displacement will continue compounding through the 2030s.

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **Skill Boundary:** The artificial-labor skill does not model robotic automation, implementation lags, or labor force exit adjustments. All robotic displacement figures and 95th percentile adjustments are external calculations, not validated skill outputs.

2. **Regional Data Gaps:** UK-specific sectoral employment, labor force participation rates, and automation adoption rates are estimated from OECD/ONS baselines. Actual figures may vary.

3. **Reemployment Exclusion:** The 100% displacement-to-unemployment conversion assumes zero reemployment. Historical precedent suggests 20-50% reemployment rates over 5-10 years, but this requires policy intervention (retraining programs, fiscal stimulus).

4. **Overlap Simplification:** The overlap adjustment (manufacturing supervision) is a first-order approximation. Additional overlaps may exist in logistics (warehouse supervision) and retail (store management).

5. **95th Percentile Methodology:** The worst-case adjustments (+280K robotic, +320K labor force, +90K reemployment delay) are derived from historical disruption precedents (1980s manufacturing, 2000s retail automation) and expert judgment, not statistical modeling.
