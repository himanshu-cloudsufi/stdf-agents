## **PART 1: EU ETS STRUCTURE & MECHANICS**

### **How the System Works**



The EU ETS is a cap-and-trade scheme where one allowance permits emission of one tonne of CO₂ equivalent. Companies must monitor yearly emissions and surrender enough allowances to cover their annual emissions

. 

The cap refers to the total limit on greenhouse gases that can be emitted, expressed in emission allowances

.

**Pricing Mechanism:**


The price of allowances is determined by the EU carbon market through supply and demand of allowances

. 

The declining cap informs companies about long-term scarcity of allowances while the carbon price provides an incentive for companies to reduce emissions cost-effectively

.

**Current Price:** 

European carbon prices reached €83.79 per tonne on December 15, 2025, up 3% monthly and 30% year-over-year

.

### **Scope & Coverage**

**EU ETS I (Original System):**


Covers around 10,000 installations in the energy sector and manufacturing industry, including oil refineries, steel works, production of iron, aluminum, metals, cement, lime, glass, ceramics, pulp, paper, cardboard, acids and bulk organic chemicals

. 

The ETS covers around 45% of the EU's greenhouse gas emissions

.

**2024 Maritime Expansion:**


Starting January 1, 2024, the EU ETS applies to vessels engaged in commercial transportation of cargo or passengers above 5,000 gross tonnage

. 

100% of emissions from ships between EU ports and 50% of emissions from ships between an EU port and a non-EU port fall within scope

.

**ETS II (New System - 2027/2028):**


From 2027, road transport and buildings will be covered by a new EU ETS2, which will cover CO₂ emissions upstream at fuel suppliers who place fuel on the EU market. The ETS2 covers around 40% of the EU's greenhouse gas emissions

.

### **Cap Trajectory & Retirement**

**2030 Target:**


The cap has been tightened to bring emissions down by 62% by 2030, compared to 2005 levels

. 

The linear reduction factor will be raised to 4.3% for 2024-2027 and then to 4.4% from 2028

.

**Market Stability Reserve (MSR):**


The Market Stability Reserve was established in 2018 and began operating in 2019 to address the surplus of allowances. The MSR adjusts the supply of allowances to be auctioned based on predefined thresholds of the total number of allowances in circulation

.

**Allowance Retirement:**


On January 1, 2023, 2.5 billion allowances in the MSR holdings were invalidated. Another 381 million allowances were invalidated on January 1, 2024, and 271 million allowances were invalidated on January 1, 2025

. 

Since 2019, the MSR has been withdrawing allowances from auctions every year. In 2024, the surplus amounted to 1.14 billion

.

### **Free Allocation & CBAM**

**Phase-Out of Free Allowances:**


Free allocation for products under the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism will be phased out by 2034, starting at 2.5% reduction in 2026 and reaching 100% by 2034

. 

Free allocations to the aviation sector will end by 2026, with 25% reduction in 2024 and 50% reduction in 2025

.

**Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM):**


CBAM was adopted on May 10, 2023

. 

As early as October 2023, importers of CBAM products must report on emissions embedded in imported products. From 2026, allowances for the reported emissions must be purchased and surrendered at the price of EU allowances

.

### **Revenue Generation & Use**



Since 2013, the EU ETS has raised over €175 billion. The EU ETS revenue primarily flows to national budgets and Member States must use it to support investments in renewable energy, energy efficiency improvements and low-carbon technologies

.

## **PART 3: MY CRITICAL ASSESSMENT**

### **The Fundamental Problem: Carbon Pricing Assumes Marginal Behavioral Change, Not System Replacement**

The EU ETS is designed with a **linear policy mindset** trying to manage an **exponential disruption**. Here's why this creates profound structural contradictions:

### **1. The System is Backward-Looking on Energy**

**Reality Check from Our Work:**
- 

The EU ETS has supported a 51% decline in emissions from stationary installations from 2005 to 2024


- 

In 2023, emissions decreased by 15.5%, largely driven by a 24% reduction in power generation emissions, largely due to a boost in renewable energy



**The Problem:** The ETS takes credit for what **solar, wind, and batteries (SWB)** are achieving through cost-driven economics, not policy. 

From our energy forecasting work, we know:
- **Solar LCOE:** Now $20-40/MWh globally, with costs continuing to decline 10-15% annually
- **Coal marginal cost:** $50-80/MWh (fuel + variable O&M)
- **Gas marginal cost:** $60-120/MWh (depending on gas prices)

**At €80/tCO₂ (~$85), the carbon adder is approximately:**
- Coal: +$85/MWh (assuming 1 tCO₂/MWh)
- Gas: +$35/MWh (assuming 0.4 tCO₂/MWh)

But SWB is **already** displacing coal/gas because **zero marginal cost beats everything**, regardless of carbon price. The carbon price is solving a problem that physics already solved.

### **2. The "Tipping Point Blindness" Problem**

The EU ETS assumes **gradual, controllable substitution**. But technology disruption follows **S-curves with tipping points**, not smooth linear trajectories.

**What the ETS Misses:**

**A. EV Disruption → Oil Demand Collapse**
Our passenger vehicle modeling shows:
- China: EV tipping point already passed (~2023-2024), heading toward 80-90% market share by 2030
- Europe: Tipping point 2024-2026, similar trajectory
- Result: European oil demand for transport will collapse 50-70% by 2035

**The carbon price is irrelevant here.** EVs win on:
- Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): Already $0.10-0.15/km vs. $0.25-0.35/km for ICE in many markets
- Performance: 0-60 mph in 3 seconds, instant torque, silent operation
- Maintenance: 1/10th the parts, 1/5th the servicing

A €100/t carbon price adds maybe €0.02-0.03/km to ICE fuel costs. **This doesn't matter** when EVs are already cheaper and better.

**B. Industrial Heat Electrification**
Heat pumps and electric boilers are already cost-competitive for many industrial processes:
- Heat pump COP 3-4 means 1 kWh electricity = 3-4 kWh thermal
- Industrial electricity: €50-80/MWh
- Equivalent thermal cost: €12-27/MWh
- Natural gas: €30-80/MWh (volatile)

The disruption is **cost-driven**, not carbon-driven.

### **3. The "Stranded Revenue" Problem**



The EU ETS has raised over €175 billion since 2013

. But here's the trap:

**If the system works (emissions drop 62% by 2030, 90% by 2040), the revenue base collapses:**

| Year | Emissions (Mt) | Price (€/t) | Revenue (€B) |
|------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| 2024 | ~1,130 | ~€80 | ~€90B |
| 2030 | ~600 (62% cut) | €120-150? | €72-90B |
| 2040 | ~150 (90% cut) | €400? | €60B |

The system is **regressive**: as it succeeds, it generates less money for the clean transition it's supposed to fund.

Compare this to what **falling SWB costs** deliver:
- Every €1B invested in solar at current costs → ~2-3 GW capacity
- 2-3 GW solar × 1,500 hours/year × 30 years = 90-135 TWh cumulative
- This displaces €2-4B in fossil fuel spending **permanently**

**The EU ETS generates €90B/year that disappears over time. SWB investment creates permanent cost savings that compound exponentially.**

### **4. The "Price Signaling Paradox"**

The ETS forecasts show prices rising to €150-400/t by 2030-2040. But consider what this means:

**Scenario A: High Carbon Prices (€300-400/t)**
- Industries facing €300-400/t will either:
  - Switch to clean alternatives (which are already cheaper in many cases)
  - Relocate production outside EU (carbon leakage despite CBAM)
  - Go bankrupt
- Result: **Demand for allowances collapses → price crashes → no revenue**

**Scenario B: Low Carbon Prices (€70-100/t)**
- Price too low to drive behavioral change in hard-to-abate sectors
- Fossils remain competitive
- Emissions don't fall fast enough
- Result: **System fails to meet climate targets**

This is the **impossible middle**: The ETS needs prices high enough to change behavior but not so high that it destroys the industries it's taxing. But exponential disruption doesn't care about "impossible middles."

### **5. CBAM: Fighting the Last War**



The CBAM adds carbon costs to imported products, pushing trading partners to adopt carbon pricing or emissions reduction policies that meet EU standards

.

**The Problem:** CBAM assumes European manufacturers need "protection" from low-carbon-cost competitors. But the **real competition** is coming from countries like China that are:

- Scaling EVs faster (60% of global EV production)
- Deploying solar faster (50-60% of global capacity additions)
- Building battery supply chains at scale
- Achieving lower production costs through **volume and learning curves**, not carbon subsidies

**CBAM won't protect European steel from Chinese green hydrogen-based steel. It will be obsolete by the time it's fully implemented (2034).**

### **6. The Timing Mismatch**



With the cap continuing to decrease after 2030, the MSR reinjects all of its 400 MtCO₂ stock in the market between 2030 and 2034, after which it cannot help maintain the price anymore. After 2040, the cap is getting closer to 0 and the market is getting particularly tense. Our results show the current system will reach its limits in the 2040 decade

.

**This analysis misses the exponential acceleration.** By 2040:
- European power sector: 80-90% SWB (already happening faster than policy models)
- European passenger vehicles: 70-85% EV
- Maritime: Increasingly electrified short-haul ferries, hydrogen/ammonia for long-haul
- Aviation: SAF + electrification for short-haul
- Industrial heat: 40-60% electrified

**The EU ETS will be managing the last 10-20% of emissions in sectors that can't be easily decarbonized, not the 80-90% that economics already solved.**

### **7. What the EU ETS Actually Does**

Let me be clear: **The EU ETS is not useless. But it's not what it claims to be.**

**What it Actually Achieves:**
1. **Political signaling:** Shows European commitment to climate action
2. **Revenue generation:** Raises €40-90B/year for clean energy funds (though this declines)
3. **Incremental optimization:** Pushes marginal efficiency improvements (5-15% gains)
4. **Hard-to-abate focus:** May drive late-stage solutions (steel, cement, chemicals post-2035)

**What it Doesn't Do:**
1. **Drive primary disruption:** SWB, EVs, heat pumps win on economics, not carbon price
2. **Prevent stranded assets:** Fossil infrastructure is already stranding due to cheaper alternatives
3. **Set global price:** China, US, India not joining → EU becomes carbon price island
4. **Fund transition adequately:** €90B/year is tiny vs. €1-2 trillion needed for European energy transition
