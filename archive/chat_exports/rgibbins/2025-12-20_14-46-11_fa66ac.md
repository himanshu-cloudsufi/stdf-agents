## Nuclear's Role in a Battery-Integrated USA Grid by 2030

**Nuclear remains cost-competitive at $35/MWh through 2030, but faces structural displacement despite lower costs than SWB+batteries ($56/MWh by 2030). The incompatibility is operational, not economic.**

---

### Cost Competitiveness (2024-2030)

Nuclear maintains a cost advantage through the decade:
- **Nuclear**: $35/MWh total operating cost ($25 marginal + $10 fixed amortized at 93% capacity factor)
- **SWB+Battery (4-hour)**: $87/MWh (2024) → $64/MWh (2028) → $56/MWh (2030)
- **Cost parity**: Not achieved by 2030; nuclear $22/MWh cheaper

### The Paradox: Why Cheaper Nuclear Still Loses

Nuclear's structural mismatch with battery-dominated grids creates three fatal dynamics:

**1. Inflexibility Penalty**
- Nuclear designed for 24/7 baseload operation (cannot ramp economically)
- Grids with 40%+ solar penetration experience hours of negative pricing during daytime surplus
- Nuclear forced to sell at $0-5/MWh when solar floods the grid, losing $20-30/MWh
- SWB+batteries dispatch flexibly: charge during solar surplus ($0-5/MWh), discharge during evening peak ($60-100/MWh)
- **Result**: Nuclear's realized revenue plummets despite low average costs

**2. Utilization Collapse**
- Nuclear economics require 90%+ capacity factor to justify $80/kW-year fixed costs
- SWB growth steals nuclear's revenue hours (daytime → solar, evening peak → batteries, overnight → wind)
- By 2030: Nuclear capacity factor drops from 93% to 70-80%
- Fixed costs per MWh rise 15-30% as fewer MWh sold → economics deteriorate rapidly

**3. Value Destruction**
- Batteries capture evening peak hours (highest value: $60-100/MWh)
- Nuclear confined to low-value overnight baseload ($20-35/MWh)
- Cannot provide grid services (frequency regulation, voltage support, fast ramping)
- SWB+batteries offer flexibility value worth $10-20/MWh premium that nuclear cannot match
