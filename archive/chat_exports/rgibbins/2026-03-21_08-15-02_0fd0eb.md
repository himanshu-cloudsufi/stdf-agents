## Central Bank Rate Forecast: BoE and Fed Policy Path Through March 2027

**Thesis:** The Hormuz closure is the largest single-point oil supply disruption in market history — 14.7 Mb/d removed, 14.3% of global demand [C1]. It creates a textbook stagflationary impulse: energy-driven inflation surging into economies already running above target, while the supply shock simultaneously drags growth. Both central banks will hold rates through mid-2026, then diverge — the BoE hikes once; the Fed holds and eventually cuts. Here is the transmission mechanism and the most likely policy path.

---

### 1. The Supply Shock: Scale and Transmission

The Strait of Hormuz carried 21 Mb/d of crude and petroleum products before the closure [C1]. With tanker traffic down ~70% and war-risk insurance cancelled from March 5, the effective disruption is 14.7 Mb/d — three times larger than the 1973 Arab embargo (~5 Mb/d) [C1]. Brent crude has surged from a pre-war ~$75/barrel to $108–110, peaking at $126 [C2][C3].

Simultaneously, Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility — the world's largest — sustained Iranian missile strikes that knocked out 17% of export capacity [C4]. Qatar had already suspended LNG production on March 2 [C5]. European TTF gas surged 11% to ~€61/MWh on March 19 [C5].

Critically, both Middle East maritime corridors are blocked simultaneously for the first time in modern history — Hormuz is closed while the Red Sea/Suez route operates at just 49% of pre-crisis capacity due to resumed Houthi attacks [C6]. VLCC tanker day-rates hit an all-time record of $423,736/day [C7]. Asia–Europe container spot rates surged 10–19% in the first two weeks of March, with emergency fuel surcharges of $150–265/TEU layered on top [C8][C9].

### 2. Inflation Transmission: Four Channels

**Channel 1 — Petrol/transport fuel (immediate, 0–4 weeks):**
Already visible at the pump. Governor Bailey confirmed: "You can already see that at the petrol pump" [C10]. This is the fastest channel — a 47% oil price increase passes through at ~70% efficiency to pump prices [C1].

- US CPI contribution: **+0.98 percentage points** [C1]
- UK CPI contribution: **+1.14 percentage points** [C1]

**Channel 2 — Household gas/heating (lagged 1–2 quarters via Ofgem cap):**
This is the dominant UK-specific channel. The Ofgem price cap resets quarterly. The Q2 2026 cap was set at £1,641/year before the war [C11]. Cornwall Insight now estimates the Q3 cap (July–September) will jump 20% to £1,972 — a £331 increase [C12]. Crucially, the cap methodology means "even if wholesale prices quickly return to pre-conflict levels, some of this recent volatility will be baked into the July 2026 cap" [C12].

The UK's gas heating CPI weight (3.0%) is double the US equivalent (1.5%), and the UK's LNG import dependency amplifies the TTF shock [C1].

- US CPI contribution: **+0.45 percentage points** [C1]
- UK CPI contribution: **+1.95 percentage points** [C1]

**Channel 3 — Goods inflation via shipping costs (lagged 2–12 months):**
The CEPR academic framework confirms that 90% of a shipping cost shock transmits to import prices within two months, with peak consumer price inflation impact arriving approximately 12 months after the shock [C13]. Applied to the March 2 shock date, this places the goods inflation peak in **Q1 2027**.

Cape of Good Hope rerouting adds 14 days and ~$300,000 in fuel per voyage [C14]. This channel hits the UK harder than the US — Asia–Europe lanes are surging 10–19%, while Asia–US East Coast rates actually fell 3% in the same period [C8].

**Channel 4 — Electricity generation costs (lagged 1–3 quarters):**
US gas-fired generation (1,906 TWh, 44.5% of mix) sees marginal cost rise from $35/MWh to ~$56/MWh (+60%) [C15]. European/UK gas generation (539 TWh, 16% of mix) faces a more severe shock: marginal cost rises from $60/MWh to ~$138/MWh (+130%) [C15]. The merit order inverts in the US — gas marginal cost ($56) now exceeds coal ($40), pulling coal back into dispatch [C15].

## ECB Policy Path: Hormuz Shock Through End-2026 and Q1–Q2 2027

**Thesis:** The ECB entered the Hormuz crisis from a structurally different position than the Fed or BoE — it had already cut rates eight times to 2.00%, HICP was running *below* target at 1.7–1.9%, and the Eurozone economy was fragile. The energy shock now reverses the disinflation trajectory, but the ECB's response will be materially more cautious than the BoE's: it holds through 2026, does not hike, and resumes cutting only in Q2 2027 once the goods inflation peak passes.

---

### 1. Starting Position (Confirmed Actuals)

The ECB Governing Council held the deposit facility rate at **2.00%** on March 19, 2026 — the sixth consecutive hold since June 2025, following eight cumulative cuts from 4.00% [C1]. The main refinancing operations rate sits at 2.15% and the marginal lending facility rate at 2.40% [C1].

Eurozone HICP before the shock:
- December 2025: 2.0% [C2]
- January 2026: 1.7% [C3]
- February 2026: 1.9% [C4]
- Core (ex-energy/food): 2.2% in February [C5]

The ECB was on track to undershoot its 2% target. The Hormuz closure on March 2 reversed that trajectory entirely.

---

### 2. Eurozone Structural Exposure: Four Transmission Channels

The Eurozone is structurally more exposed to this shock than the US, and comparably exposed to the UK — but the transmission mechanism differs in important ways.

**Channel 1 — Petrol/transport fuel (immediate):**
Brent crude surged from ~€75/barrel pre-war to above €86/barrel and climbing [C6]. Eurozone petrol CPI weight is approximately 3.2% — slightly higher than the US (3.0%) but comparable to the UK (3.5%). Pass-through efficiency at ~70%.
- **Eurozone CPI contribution: +1.05 percentage points**

**Channel 2 — Gas heating (lagged 1–2 quarters, structurally severe):**
This is the dominant Eurozone-specific channel. European TTF gas surged from €30/MWh the week before the war to above €60/MWh on March 3, before settling near €48–61/MWh [C7]. European gas prices are up 60% since the conflict began [C8]. Critically, Europe entered 2026 with gas storage at only **46 bcm** — versus 60 bcm in 2025 and 77 bcm in 2024 [C9]. This depleted storage baseline means the Eurozone has less buffer against a sustained supply disruption than in any recent year.

Qatar's Ras Laffan facility — which supplies 12–14% of Europe's LNG — was damaged by Iranian strikes, with 17% of export capacity knocked out [C10]. Europe competes directly with Asian buyers for flexible spot LNG cargoes, a dynamic that drove TTF to record levels during 2021–2023 [C9].

Eurozone gas heating CPI weight: ~2.5%. At 60% wholesale price increase with ~65% retail pass-through:
- **Eurozone CPI contribution: +0.98 percentage points**

**Channel 3 — Goods inflation via shipping (lagged 2–12 months):**
Asia–Europe container spot rates surged 10–19% in the first two weeks of March [C11]. The CEPR framework places peak consumer price inflation from shipping costs approximately 12 months after the shock — meaning the goods inflation peak arrives in **Q1 2027** [C12]. The Eurozone is the primary destination for the Asia–Europe lanes experiencing the sharpest rate increases, making this channel proportionally larger for the Eurozone than for the US.
- **Eurozone CPI contribution: +0.22 percentage points** (lagged, arriving Q4 2026–Q1 2027)

**Channel 4 — Electricity generation costs (lagged 1–3 quarters):**
European gas-fired generation (539 TWh, 16% of the mix) faces marginal cost rising from ~€60/MWh to ~€138/MWh (+130%) [C13]. Unlike the US, where coal re-enters dispatch as gas becomes more expensive, European coal capacity is more limited — the merit order shock is absorbed more directly into wholesale electricity prices. European SWB generation (1,133 TWh, 33.5% of the mix) is completely insulated at zero marginal fuel cost [C13], acting as a structural firebreak — but 66.5% of the mix remains exposed.
- **Eurozone CPI contribution: +0.18 percentage points**

### 3. The ECB's Structural Dilemma

The ECB faces a different version of the stagflation trap than the Fed or BoE, for three reasons:

**First, it was already cutting.** The ECB had reduced rates by 200bp from peak to 2.00% — it was in active easing mode when the shock hit. The question is not whether to hike but whether to pause the cutting cycle and for how long.

**Second, the Eurozone economy is weaker.** The Eurozone was growing at approximately 1.0–1.2% pre-shock, with Germany in particular structurally challenged by energy costs and export weakness. A supply-side energy shock hitting a weak economy argues strongly against hiking — the growth drag from $110+ Brent is a more immediate threat to Eurozone GDP than the inflation overshoot is to credibility.

**Third, the inflation overshoot is supply-side and temporary in structure.** HICP was at 1.9% before the shock — the ECB was *below* target. The shock pushes it to 3.8–4.3% at peak, but the ECB's own projections show it returning to 2.0% in 2027 [C1]. This is the classic case for "looking through" a supply shock: the medium-term inflation anchor is not threatened if second-round wage effects do not materialise.

The critical variable is services inflation and wage growth. Core HICP (ex-energy/food) was 2.2% in February [C5] — elevated but not accelerating. If services inflation re-accelerates above 3.5% or wage growth re-accelerates above 4%, the ECB's calculus changes. If not, the hold-and-wait approach is dominant.
