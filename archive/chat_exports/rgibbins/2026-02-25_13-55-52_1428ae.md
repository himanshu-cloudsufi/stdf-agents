**Rollout Lag and Diffusion Lag: Definitions and Framework Application**

Rollout lag and diffusion lag are distinct temporal dynamics in disruption analysis, each describing different phases of the adoption timeline.

**Rollout Lag (Capability-to-Deployment Delay)**

Rollout lag is the time interval between when a technology achieves technical capability and when it becomes commercially deployed at scale. This lag reflects the time required to:

• Build production infrastructure and supply chains
• Establish distribution networks and sales channels
• Train deployment workforces
• Achieve regulatory approval and certification
• Overcome initial capital constraints

In the artificial labor model, rollout lag represents the delay between when an AI system demonstrates task capability (e.g., passing benchmark thresholds) and when enterprises deploy that capability in production environments. For cognitive task automation, this lag is measured in months, not years, because AI systems are software-based and can be deployed via API or cloud infrastructure without physical manufacturing constraints.

**Diffusion Lag (Deployment-to-Saturation Delay)**

Diffusion lag is the time interval between initial commercial deployment and market saturation (typically 80-95% adoption). This lag reflects the S-curve adoption dynamics:

• Early adopters (2-10% market share): 2-5 years
• Rapid growth phase (10-80% market share): 10-20 years
• Saturation phase (80-95% market share): 5-10 years

Diffusion lag is NOT the delay between cost-parity and adoption. Once cost-parity is achieved, adoption accelerates immediately. Diffusion lag describes the time required to traverse the S-curve from tipping point (10% adoption) to saturation (80%+), which is governed by positive feedback loops (cost reduction → adoption → scale → further cost reduction) and negative feedback loops (infrastructure constraints, regulatory barriers, workforce retraining).

**Application to Artificial Labor Disruption**

The artificial labor model incorporates both lags explicitly:

**Rollout Lag Structure**

• **Capability Achievement**: AI system crosses task-specific capability threshold (e.g., METR benchmark score ≥ 0.85)
• **Cost-Parity Achievement**: AI cost per task-hour falls below human wage floor (e.g., $15/hour)
• **Deployment Delay**: 6-18 month rollout lag between cost-parity and initial enterprise deployment
• **Mechanism**: Enterprises require time to integrate AI systems, retrain workflows, and overcome organizational inertia

**Diffusion Lag Structure**

• **Tipping Point (10% Adoption)**: Occurs 12-24 months after initial deployment
• **Rapid Growth Phase (10-80% Adoption)**: 5-10 years for cognitive task categories

• **Mechanism**: Governed by S-curve dynamics with exponential acceleration during the 10-50% adoption phase

**Adoption and Diffusion Dynamics: The Analytical Framework**

Adoption and diffusion describe the temporal trajectory by which a disruptive technology displaces an incumbent system. The framework distinguishes between adoption inevitability (whether disruption occurs) and adoption velocity (how fast disruption occurs). Cost-parity achievement determines inevitability; feedback loop dynamics determine velocity.

**Core Mathematical Structure: The Logistic S-Curve**

Adoption follows a logistic function:

**A(t) = L / (1 + e^(-k(t - t₀)))**

Where:
• **L** = Saturation ceiling (typically 80-95%)
• **k** = Growth rate parameter (0.2-0.5 per year for most disruptions)
• **t₀** = Inflection point (time when adoption reaches 50% of L)
• **t** = Time since cost-parity achievement

This produces the characteristic S-shape: slow early growth, exponential acceleration, then saturation.
