PJM Interconnection has implemented comprehensive regulatory reforms from 2023-2026 to accelerate grid connection timelines and expand transmission capacity, driven by the economic imperative of rapidly growing electricity demand from data centers, electrification, and clean energy deployment [W1][W2][W3].

**Interconnection Queue Reforms (2023-2024)**

PJM replaced its first-come, first-served interconnection process with a cluster-based study approach in 2023 [W2][W4]. Transition Cycle 1 studies were completed in 2024, processing over 200 projects representing more than 60 GW of generation capacity—the largest single interconnection study cycle in PJM history [W1][W7]. The reforms introduced enhanced readiness requirements, including site control demonstrations, commercial readiness milestones, and increased financial commitments from developers to reduce speculative queue positions [W2][W8]. FERC approved streamlined connection processes for distribution-level resources in 2024, simplifying requirements for smaller-scale projects and reducing administrative barriers [W6].

**Transmission Planning Expansion (2024-2025)**

PJM's 2024 Regional Transmission Expansion Plan (RTEP) identified significant transmission capacity expansion needs to accommodate burgeoning electricity demand growth [W3]. The planning process has shifted from reactive to proactive, incorporating multi-year demand forecasts for data centers, EV charging infrastructure, and industrial electrification [W3]. This represents a systemic recognition that transmission infrastructure must be built ahead of demand, not in response to it—a departure from decades of incremental planning.

**Persistent Bottlenecks**

Despite reforms, interconnection timelines remain 3-5 years on average, with network upgrade costs creating substantial barriers to grid access [W5][W9]. The pace of reform implementation is insufficient relative to demand growth projections and the scale of the interconnection queue backlog, which exceeds 270 GW of proposed generation [W5][W9]. The fundamental constraint is not regulatory process but physical infrastructure: transmission capacity expansion requires multi-year construction timelines and billions in capital investment.

**Economic Context**

These reforms reflect the economic gravity of the SWB (solar-wind-battery) cost collapse. Solar and wind generation costs have fallen below $30/MWh in many regions, creating overwhelming economic pressure to connect new capacity [W5]. The regulatory changes are not driving the transformation—they are responding to it. The bottleneck has shifted from regulatory approval to physical infrastructure deployment, which cannot be accelerated beyond construction and manufacturing constraints.

The PJM reforms represent necessary but insufficient steps. The real constraint is no longer queue management but transmission build-out speed, which is governed by steel, copper, and labor availability—not policy.
