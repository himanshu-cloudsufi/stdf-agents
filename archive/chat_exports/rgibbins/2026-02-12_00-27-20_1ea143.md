**Optimal Technology Mix (Cost-Curve Driven)**

Solar PV dominates because it has the fastest deployment cycle (6-12 months vs 24-36 months for wind), the steepest cost curve (LCOE $29-40/MWh in the US by 2025), and the highest manufacturing scalability. Utility-scale solar installed costs in the US are $1,100-1,300/kW (2025), falling to $900-1,000/kW by 2029 via Wright's Law dynamics. Onshore wind complements solar with capacity factors of 35-45% and LCOE $30-50/MWh, but permitting and siting constraints limit annual deployment to 15-20 GW/year. Battery storage (4-hour duration) is sized at 10 GW (40 GWh) to manage evening ramps and frequency regulation, not to replace thermal capacity.

The 67 GW/year target is 1.9x the 2023 US solar+wind deployment rate of ~35 GW (24 GW solar, 11 GW wind). The US has never exceeded 40 GW/year of combined solar+wind deployment; the proposed rate requires systemic reform, not incremental optimization.

2. **Transmission Capacity**: The US transmission system was built for centralized fossil generation, not distributed renewables. High-quality wind and solar resources are in MISO, SPP, and ERCOT, but load centers are in PJM and CAISO. Transmission build rates are 1,500-2,000 miles/year; 67 GW/year of renewables requires 3,000-4,000 miles/year of new high-voltage lines. This requires FERC Order 1920 (regional transmission planning) enforcement, federal transmission siting authority (overriding state vetoes), and cost allocation reforms (socializing benefits rather than assigning costs to first-mover projects).
