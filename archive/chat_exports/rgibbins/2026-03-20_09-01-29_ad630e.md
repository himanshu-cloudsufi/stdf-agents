## AI Perspectives on Central Bank Policy Rates: A Structural Assessment

**Geographic scope assumed: US Federal Reserve and ECB as primary reference systems; conclusions apply globally with regional calibration.**

Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded.

---

### Thesis

Applying AI-informed analytical frameworks to central bank policy rates is not merely useful — it is **structurally necessary**. The reason is not that AI improves pattern recognition at the margin. It is that the deflationary forces AI and zero marginal cost energy (SWB) are generating will systematically break the transmission mechanisms that Taylor Rule and DSGE models depend on. A central bank operating without this framework will set rates too high, misread labor market signals, and persistently undershoot its inflation target — not from insufficient demand, but from a phase change in the economy's cost structure.

---

### Dimension 1: AI as a Pattern-Recognition Tool for Rate Cycle Analysis

AI adds genuine value here, but it is the least transformative of the four dimensions. Historical rate cycle analysis — identifying macro regimes (expansion, stagflation, disinflation, deflation) from multi-variate time series — is a domain where AI's pattern-recognition capability exceeds human analysts. Regime detection across 50+ simultaneous indicators (yield curve shape, credit spread dynamics, employment diffusion indices, PMI sequences) is computationally intractable for human analysts but tractable for trained models.

The analytical utility is real but bounded: AI pattern recognition on historical data is only as useful as the assumption that future cycles resemble past ones. The value degrades precisely as the structural disruption deepens. By 2028–2030, the macro regime the AI is being asked to classify will have no historical analog in its training data.

**Conclusion:** High utility for 2026–2027; declining utility as structural break deepens post-2028.

---

### Dimension 2: AI as a Forecasting Layer for Rate Path Prediction

AI-augmented forecasting of rate paths using macro indicators (inflation, employment, GDP, credit spreads) improves on linear regression models in non-stationary environments. The improvement comes from two sources: (1) non-linear feature interactions that DSGE models linearize away, and (2) real-time data ingestion that reduces the publication lag problem.

However, the forecasting layer faces the same structural limitation as pattern recognition: the training distribution is pre-disruption. An AI model trained on 1990–2025 macro data will embed the assumption that labor cost dynamics are mean-reverting around a stable wage Phillips Curve. This assumption is false from 2027 onward [C1].

The correct application is not AI as a drop-in replacement for DSGE forecasting — it is AI as a **structural break detector**, flagging when incoming data diverges from the historical distribution by more than model uncertainty bounds. This is a monitoring function, not a forecasting function.

**Conclusion:** Useful as a regime-change early warning system; unreliable as a rate path predictor beyond 2027 without explicit AI-deflation inputs.

A central bank operating a Taylor Rule calibrated on pre-AI labor market dynamics will set policy rates structurally too high relative to the inflation-neutral rate. The error is not noise — it is a systematic bias that compounds annually through 2030 [C2].

The neutral rate r\* itself requires downward revision of **–1.5 to –1.7 pp by 2036** in both the US and EU from energy deflation alone [C3], with additional downward pressure from labor cost deflation beginning 2028–2029.

**DSGE Model Failure Modes:**

DSGE models embed three calibration assumptions that the SWB + artificial labor convergence breaks simultaneously [C3]:

- *Energy price process:* Modeled as AR(1), mean-reverting. SWB cost curves are a deterministic downward trend with no mean reversion.
- *Technology shocks:* Modeled as transitory. The cost-curve collapse is permanent.
- *Labor market:* Modeled as sticky wages around a stable equilibrium. Artificial labor collapses the marginal cost of cognitive labor for automatable tasks — not a sticky-wage phenomenon, a structural cost floor collapse.

The failure mode is specific: DSGE models will interpret persistent inflation undershoots as demand weakness and recommend stimulus. The stimulus will not close the gap because the gap is supply-side structural. The models will oscillate between overtightening (misreading energy deflation as a positive supply shock requiring normalization) and over-stimulating (misreading persistent undershoots as output gaps). Neither response addresses the actual cause [C3].

**The asymmetric risk is deflationary undershoot combined with policy overtightening** — a central bank applying the brakes while the economy's cost structure is undergoing a phase change toward superabundance [C3].

## Fed Policy Rates in a US-Iran War: The Structural Collision

**Scope: US Federal Reserve as primary institution. Oil price spike treated as a temporary supply shock, not a permanent structural shift. Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded.**

### The Core Tension in Three Sentences

That world no longer exists, and applying 1979 logic to a 2026–2028 structural deflation environment is the most dangerous monetary policy error available.

---

### Part 1: What the Fed Would Actually Do (The Conventional Response)

The Fed's institutional reflex, shaped by four decades of inflation-targeting credibility, is to **hold rates or deliver a modest hawkish signal** in response to an oil supply shock — not to raise aggressively, and not to cut. Here is why:

**The post-2008 Fed doctrine on supply shocks:** The Fed learned from 2008 and 2011 that mechanically tightening into commodity-driven inflation destroys demand without addressing the supply-side cause. The standard post-Volcker playbook is:

1. **Hold rates** if the shock is clearly temporary (< 90 days, SPR release underway, diplomatic resolution visible)
2. **Deliver hawkish forward guidance** to anchor inflation expectations without actually tightening
3. **Cut rates** if the shock triggers demand destruction severe enough to threaten recession (GDP impact > –0.8%)

In a full Hormuz closure scenario (Scenario B/C: $109–$194/bbl, GDP impact –0.4% to –1.2%) [C1], the Fed faces a genuine stagflation signal — rising headline CPI from gasoline (+$0.89 to +$2.93/gallon) [C1] combined with weakening demand. The 1970s response was to tighten into this. The post-Volcker response is to hold and wait for the supply shock to resolve.

**The most likely Fed action in 2026–2028:** Hold rates with hawkish communication. Cut by 25–50 bps if GDP impact crosses –0.8% and demand destruction becomes the dominant signal. Do not raise rates unless the shock proves non-transitory (> 6 months) and inflation expectations become unanchored.

---

### Part 2: The Historical Precedents — and Why They Break Down

**1973 Arab Oil Embargo:** ~5% global supply loss → ~300% oil price spike. Fed (Burns) tightened into the shock, then reversed. Result: stagflation embedded for a decade. The lesson: tightening into a supply shock amplifies demand destruction without resolving the supply constraint.

**1979 Iranian Revolution:** ~4% global supply loss → ~100% price spike. Fed (Volcker) raised the federal funds rate to 20%. Result: inflation broken, but at the cost of a severe recession. The lesson: this worked because inflation expectations were already unanchored from years of prior accommodation. Volcker was not responding to the oil shock — he was breaking a wage-price spiral that predated it.

**1990 Gulf War:** ~5% supply loss → ~100% spike, 3-month duration. Fed (Greenspan) held rates, then cut as demand destruction materialized. Result: mild recession, inflation contained. The lesson: a temporary, geopolitically-driven shock resolves itself; the correct response is patience.

**2003 Iraq War:** Oil spiked briefly but the shock was modest and short-lived. Fed held rates near zero. No inflation transmission.

**Why all four precedents break down in 2026–2028:**

The critical difference is the structural deflationary backdrop. In 1973, 1979, 1990, and 2003, the US economy had no structural deflationary force operating against the oil shock. The oil price spike transmitted directly into CPI with no countervailing pressure. In 2026–2028, the combined SWB + artificial labor deflation is running at –0.46 to –1.08 pp/year [C2], and accelerating. The oil shock is a temporary spike layered on top of a permanent downward cost trajectory.

| Precedent | Structural Deflation Backdrop | Oil Shock Duration | Correct Fed Response |
|-----------|------------------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 1973 | None | 6 months | Hold/cut (Burns tightened — error) |
| 1979 | None | 12 months | Tighten (Volcker — correct for unanchored expectations) |

The 1979 precedent is the most dangerous template to apply. Volcker's rate hikes worked because he was breaking an entrenched wage-price spiral in an economy with no structural deflation. In 2026–2028, artificial labor is already displacing 17–28 million FTE workers [C3] and suppressing wage growth structurally. There is no wage-price spiral to break. Applying Volcker-style tightening into a structural deflation environment would be tightening against gravity.

This is the analytically decisive dimension. The oil shock is temporary; the structural deflation is permanent. The Fed's reaction function cannot correctly process both simultaneously because its models treat them as the same type of signal.

A Taylor Rule operating in 2027 observes:

The problem arrives when the oil shock resolves (30–90 days): headline CPI drops sharply, and the structural deflation pressure of –2.43 pp/year [C3] is suddenly unmasked. A Fed that resolves the oil shock correctly but then fails to cut rates aggressively enough into the structural deflation will produce a recession not from the war, but from the monetary policy response to the war's aftermath.

## UK Gas Price Shock in a US-Iran War: Acute Phase Analysis (0–90 Days)

**Scope: UK and broader European gas markets. Conflict scenario: US-Iran war with Trump attempting to control or blockade Kharg Island. Acute shock phase: 0–90 days. Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded.**

### The User Hypothesis: Kharg Island

Kharg Island is Iran's primary crude oil export terminal, handling approximately **90% of Iran's ~1.5 mb/d of crude exports** [C1]. A US ground operation or naval blockade targeting Kharg would be a direct attack on Iran's export infrastructure — not merely a Hormuz interdiction. The strategic logic is clear: Kharg is a fixed, identifiable chokepoint. Controlling it eliminates Iranian export revenue without requiring full Hormuz closure.

However, the gas market implications of a Kharg operation are **indirect but severe**, operating through three distinct channels:

1. **Iranian LNG and gas export disruption** — Iran holds the world's second-largest natural gas reserves (~34 trillion cubic metres) but exports relatively little LNG due to sanctions. A Kharg blockade signals escalation that would immediately trigger panic pricing in global LNG spot markets.
2. **Hormuz risk premium** — Any US military operation in the Persian Gulf raises the probability of Iranian retaliation via Hormuz mining or closure. The market prices this probability, not just the current reality.
3. **Qatar contagion** — Qatar exports ~77 million tonnes per annum (mtpa) of LNG, the majority of which transits or originates in the Persian Gulf. Qatar's North Field is the world's largest single gas reservoir. A conflict that threatens Persian Gulf transit routes threatens **~20–25% of global LNG supply** [C1].

The gas shock from a Kharg blockade is therefore **not primarily about Iranian gas** — it is about the Qatari LNG risk premium and the Hormuz closure probability that a Kharg operation implies.

### UK Gas Market Exposure: Structural Vulnerability

The UK's gas exposure is structurally severe for four compounding reasons:

**1. Declining North Sea production.** UK domestic gas production has fallen from ~108 bcm/year (peak, 2000) to approximately **35–40 bcm/year** in 2025 [C1]. The UK is now a net gas importer, dependent on Norwegian pipeline gas (the Langeled and FLAGS systems) and LNG imports for supply balance.

**2. LNG import dependency.** The UK imports approximately **15–20 bcm/year of LNG**, primarily from Qatar (via South Hook terminal, Milford Haven) and the US. Qatar supplies roughly **10–12 bcm/year** of UK LNG imports [C1]. A Persian Gulf conflict that threatens Qatari LNG loading operations hits the UK's largest single LNG supplier directly.

**3. European interconnector exposure.** UK gas prices are set at the National Balancing Point (NBP), which is tightly coupled to the Dutch TTF hub through the Interconnector UK (IUK) and BBL pipelines. When TTF spikes — as it did to **€340/MWh in August 2022** during the Russia-Ukraine supply crisis [C1] — NBP follows within hours. The UK cannot insulate its price from European market dynamics regardless of its own supply position.

**4. Gas-dominated electricity generation.** Unlike France (nuclear-heavy) or Germany (coal + wind), the UK relies on gas for approximately **35–40% of electricity generation** [C1]. A gas price spike transmits directly into electricity prices through the merit order mechanism — gas sets the marginal price of electricity for the majority of hours. This creates a **double exposure**: UK households and businesses face both direct gas bill increases and electricity bill increases simultaneously.

**Scenario B — Kharg Blockade + Hormuz Partial Closure (Iranian retaliation)**

This is the more likely scenario. Iran's asymmetric response to a Kharg operation is Hormuz mining or naval harassment — not a symmetric military engagement. **Scenario C — Full Hormuz Closure (Iranian escalation to maximum response)**

The edge was the gap between the institutional reflex (hike aggressively into supply-side CPI) and the correct response (hold or cut). That gap was wide.

At 40 bps, the market has moved approximately halfway toward the analytically correct response. The pricing now implies one to two 25 bps hikes — a credibility signal rather than a full tightening cycle. This is closer to what the BoE *should* do (one token hike as an expectations anchor, then hold) than the 85 bps path was.

**The edge has compressed, but it has not disappeared.** The reason is directional asymmetry: the analytically correct response in Scenario B/C is hold-to-cut, not hold-to-hike. Even one 25 bps hike is the wrong instrument applied to a supply-side shock. The structural deflationary backdrop — SWB + AI labour deflation running at –1.12 pp/year on average over 2026–2036 [C2] — means the inflation-neutral rate is structurally lower than 4.15%. Receiving fixed at 4.15% still captures the gap between where the market prices the terminal rate and where the structural forces are pulling it.

The key distinction: at 85 bps, the market was pricing the *wrong policy* at *full magnitude*. At 40 bps, the market is pricing the *wrong policy* at *half magnitude*. The direction of the error is unchanged; the size of the opportunity is halved.

### 2. Breakeven and Carry Dynamics at the Tighter Entry Level

**The breakeven is now much tighter, which changes the risk/reward calculus materially.**

At 85 bps (prior case):
- Fixed received: ~4.60%
- Floating paid: 3.75% (starting rate)
- Gross carry if BoE holds: +85 bps over the horizon
- Breakeven: BoE must hike fewer than 85 bps for the position to profit
- The receiver profits in every scenario where the BoE delivers fewer than 4 hikes

At 40 bps (current case):
- Fixed received: ~4.15%
- Floating paid: 3.75% (starting rate)
- Gross carry if BoE holds: +40 bps over the horizon
- Breakeven: BoE must hike fewer than 40 bps for the position to profit on a carry basis
- The receiver profits only if the BoE delivers zero or one partial hike

**The breakeven has shifted from "fewer than 4 hikes" to "fewer than 2 hikes."** This is a significant compression. The position now requires the BoE to essentially hold rates (or cut) to generate positive carry — a one-move hike of 25 bps leaves the receiver roughly flat on carry (25 bps received premium minus 25 bps of floating rate increase = approximately zero net carry, before transaction costs).

**Carry table at 40 bps entry:**

| BoE Outcome by Dec 2026 | Floating Rate Paid (avg) | Fixed Received | Net Carry | Receiver P&L |
|------------------------|--------------------------|----------------|-----------|--------------|
| Hold (0 hikes) | ~3.75% | 4.15% | +40 bps | Profit |
| 1 hike (25 bps) | ~3.85% (blended) | 4.15% | +30 bps | Small profit |
| 40 bps (2 partial hikes) | ~3.95% (blended) | 4.15% | +20 bps | Marginal profit |

| 4 hikes (100 bps) | ~4.25% (blended) | 4.15% | –10 bps | Loss |

The position remains profitable across a wide range of outcomes — but the margin of safety has compressed from 85 bps to approximately 40 bps. The receiver now needs the BoE to deliver fewer than 75 bps of hikes to generate meaningful positive carry, versus fewer than 85 bps in the prior case. That is a narrower but still favourable band.

**Mark-to-market dynamics:** If the gas shock resolves within 90 days and the curve reprices lower (BoE cuts 25–50 bps post-shock as structural deflation unmasks [C1]), the receiver captures both carry and mark-to-market gains. The MTM gain on a 50 bps cut from 3.75% to 3.25% on a December 2026 instrument is approximately +50 bps in price terms — adding to the +40 bps carry for a total return of ~+90 bps. This remains an attractive risk/reward even at the tighter entry.

### 3. Does the Structural Deflationary Backdrop Still Argue for a Terminal Rate Below 4.15%?

**Yes. Unambiguously and with greater force than the market's 40 bps pricing implies.**

The structural case for a terminal rate below 4.15% by December 2026 rests on three independent forces, each of which is executing on its cost curve regardless of the gas shock scenario:

**Force 1 — SWB cost-curve deflation (UK-specific):**
UK SWB penetration reaches approximately 31.8% of electricity generation in 2026 [C3], with offshore wind the dominant driver. Every percentage point of SWB penetration that displaces gas from the merit order reduces the structural inflation floor in the UK economy. The cost-curve trajectory of offshore wind (UK installed capacity growing from ~15 GW toward 50 GW by 2030 [C1]) is a permanent, one-way ratchet in the marginal cost of UK electricity. This is not priced into the BoE's DSGE models and is not reflected in the 4.15% terminal rate.

**Force 2 — AI labour displacement (structural wage suppression):**
AI labour displacement is running at 6–12% of the cognitive workforce in 2026–2027 [C4], generating structural downward pressure on wage growth. The wage-price spiral — the primary justification for any BoE hike in a supply-side shock environment — requires sustained worker bargaining power. That bargaining power is being systematically eroded by the cost-curve dynamics of artificial labour. A terminal rate of 4.15% embeds no weight on this structural force.

**Force 3 — The post-shock deflationary undershoot:**
When the gas shock resolves (assumed 0–90 days, Scenario B), headline CPI drops sharply as energy prices revert. The structural deflation of –1.0 to –1.2 pp/year [C2] is then unmasked. The BoE, facing a CPI undershoot below 2%, will need to cut — not hold at 4.15%. The December 2026 terminal rate implied by the post-shock structural environment is closer to 3.25%–3.50% than 4.15%.

**The structural verdict:** 4.15% is above the analytically correct terminal rate for December 2026 by approximately 65–90 bps. The receiver at 4.15% is still positioned on the right side of the structural argument — but with less cushion than at 4.60%.

### 4. At What Priced-In Level Does the Receiver Position Become Neutral or Unattractive?

**The receiver becomes neutral at approximately 15–20 bps of priced hikes. It becomes unattractive below that.**

The logic is as follows:

**Neutral zone (15–20 bps priced):** At this level, the fixed rate received is approximately 3.90%–3.95%. The analytically correct BoE response (hold at 3.75% or cut 25 bps) generates only 15–20 bps of carry — insufficient to compensate for the tail risk that the BoE delivers one 25 bps hike as a credibility signal (which would leave the receiver flat or marginally negative on carry). The structural deflation argument still favours the receiver directionally, but the carry cushion is too thin to justify the position on a risk-adjusted basis.

**Unattractive zone (< 15 bps priced):** At this level, the fixed rate received is below 3.90%. The receiver is essentially paying for the optionality of the BoE cutting — a legitimate structural argument, but one that requires the BoE to cut within the December 2026 horizon. Given the BoE's demonstrated institutional bias toward holding or hiking into supply-side CPI spikes [C1], the probability of a cut within the horizon is lower than the probability of a hold. The carry is insufficient to compensate for this institutional risk.

**Attractiveness gradient:**

### Synthesis: Does the Asymmetry Still Favour the Receiver at 40 bps?

**Yes — but the conviction level drops from high to moderate.**

The three structural arguments that made the receiver compelling at 85 bps remain intact at 40 bps:

1. The gas shock is supply-side; Bank Rate cannot address it; the BoE should hold or cut [C1]
2. The structural deflationary backdrop (SWB + AI labour) argues for a terminal rate below 4.15% [C2][C3][C4]
3. The post-shock deflationary undershoot will force cuts in the 6–18 month window following resolution [C1]

What has changed is the margin of safety. At 85 bps, the receiver was correct in every scenario except a sustained Scenario C with full wage indexation — a low-probability tail. At 40 bps, the receiver is correct in every scenario where the BoE delivers fewer than approximately 75 bps of hikes — which still covers the base case (hold) and the most likely institutional response (one credibility hike of 25 bps), but leaves less room for error if the shock proves more persistent than assumed.

**The position is still worth taking. The sizing should be reduced by approximately 40–50% relative to the 85 bps case** to reflect the compressed carry cushion and the reduced gap between market pricing and the analytically correct BoE response. The structural deflationary argument is unchanged; the entry level is simply less generous.

The trade remains: receive fixed at ~4.15% December 2026, pay floating from 3.75%. It pays if the BoE delivers fewer than approximately 75 bps of hikes — which is the analytically correct and still higher-probability outcome. The edge exists. It is smaller. It is still real.
