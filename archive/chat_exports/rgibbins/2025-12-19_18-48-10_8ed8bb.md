Based on the current US Department of Energy's "energy dominance" framework, here's how to think about it through a disruption lens:

## The Core Thesis



The administration's plan aims to "unleash American energy at home and abroad" by expanding energy production and reducing costs for American families and businesses

. The framework rests on four pillars:

1. **Maximize fossil fuel extraction** (oil, gas, coal from federal lands/offshore)
2. **Remove regulatory barriers** to energy infrastructure permitting
3. **Refill Strategic Petroleum Reserve** (currently at historic lows)
4. **Nuclear renaissance** for baseload power (especially for AI/data centers)

## The Structural Logic: Abundance Through Supply Expansion



The policy explicitly rejects "net-zero policies" as cost-raising threats to energy security, asserting "we need more energy, not less"

. The assumption: Low energy costs drive US economic competitiveness → Therefore maximize domestic production of ALL energy forms.

**Financial vehicles**: 

The Energy Dominance Financing Program guarantees loans for projects that "add energy to the grid or enhance reliability," including clean coal and oil/gas power generation

.

## How to Think About This: Three Critical Questions

### 1. **Is "Abundance" Being Measured Correctly?**

The framework conflates **energy supply volume** (barrels, BTUs, cubic feet) with **energy service cost** ($/mile driven, $/kWh delivered, $/ton-mile shipped).

- **Their metric**: Barrels produced, cubic feet extracted
- **Disruption metric**: Cost per unit of service delivered to end-user

**The gap**: If solar costs $0.02/kWh with zero fuel cost versus gas at $0.06/kWh with volatile fuel, adding more gas supply doesn't create "dominance" in the service that matters—it creates stranded asset risk.

### 2. **What Is the Marginal Cost Curve Position?**

US shale oil operates at $40-65/barrel breakeven (varies by basin). US LNG has liquefaction + shipping costs versus pipeline gas.

**Critical insight**: 

"Over the last four years, it is estimated that our Nation produced over two billion fewer barrels of oil than anticipated"

—but this assumes constant demand. If EV adoption accelerates (China BEVs hit 45% market share in 2024), that "lost production" becomes avoided stranded capital.

**The dominance question**: Are you dominant in a growing market (electricity, batteries, electric miles) or a declining one (liquid fuels for transport)?

### 3. **What Are the Demand-Side Dynamics?**

The plan focuses entirely on supply expansion. It ignores cost-driven demand destruction:

- **Oil for transport**: EVs reach cost parity at $150/bbl oil-equivalent when accounting for total cost of ownership. Every 10% EV adoption = ~2-3% global oil demand reduction.
- **Gas for power**: Solar+wind+battery systems reach cost parity with gas peaker plants at $0.04-0.06/kWh in most US regions. Once built, solar has ~$0.00 marginal cost versus gas turbine fuel costs.
- **Coal for baseload**: Already uneconomic versus combined-cycle gas; increasingly uneconomic versus SWB overbuild.

## The Disruption Lens: Incumbent Optimization vs. System Transformation

| Dimension | Energy Dominance Plan | Disruption Reality |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Primary focus** | Maximize hydrocarbon extraction volume | Minimize cost per service unit |
| **Demand assumption** | Static/growing fossil demand | Exponential decline in ICE, gas peakers |
| **Capital allocation** | Offshore leases, pipelines, refineries | Battery factories, charging networks, grid storage |
| **Time horizon** | 2026-2031 leasing programs (decades of production) | Cost parity already hit (China EVs), tipping point 2024-2027 (US/EU) |
| **Stranded asset risk** | Not mentioned | $10-20 trillion in fossil infrastructure at risk |
| **Technology curve** | Assumes flat fossil competitiveness | Wright's Law: Solar -40% cost/decade, batteries -18%/year learning rate |

## The Investment Logic Test

**If the plan succeeds** (maximized fossil production):
- Does this lower **delivered service costs** faster than solar/batteries/EVs?
- Or does it increase **sunk capital** in declining-demand infrastructure?

**The math**: 
- Adding 2 million barrels/day of US production at $65/bbl breakeven doesn't help if oil demand falls 5 million bbl/day globally due to EV adoption.
- Building new LNG export terminals with 30-year payback assumes Asian buyers prefer $12/MMBtu LNG over $0.02/kWh domestic solar—increasingly false as battery storage scales.

## Three Scenarios to Consider

### **Scenario A: Policy Extends Incumbent Dominance (Low Probability)**
- Regulatory barriers slow EV/solar deployment in US
- US captures larger share of shrinking global fossil market
- Outcome: Short-term cost relief, long-term competitiveness loss (capital misallocation into declining sectors)

### **Scenario B: Disruption Proceeds Despite Policy (High Probability)**
- Cost curves for solar/batteries/EVs continue exponential decline
- China, EU accelerate electrification while US subsidizes incumbents
- Outcome: US fossil production peaks 2025-2030, then stranded asset wave as export markets collapse

### **Scenario C: Hybrid Path (Medium Probability)**
- US excels in cheap natural gas for AI data centers (near-term)
- But EV adoption + renewables proceed in transport/power sectors
- Outcome: US becomes "gas dominant" but loses leadership in electric transport, battery manufacturing, distributed energy

## The Strategic Vulnerability



The framework describes "energy scarcity" and "artificial shortages" despite "massive reserves"

. This reveals a fundamental misdiagnosis:

**The actual constraint isn't resource scarcity—it's cost competitiveness of the service delivered.**

- Having the world's largest coal reserves doesn't create dominance if solar delivers cheaper electricity.
- Having shale oil abundance doesn't create dominance if EVs deliver cheaper miles.

## How to Evaluate This Strategically

**For policymakers**: Ask whether you're optimizing for:
- Maximum barrels/BTUs extracted (supply-side dominance)
- Minimum $/kWh or $/mile for end-users (service-cost dominance)

**For investors**: The plan creates short-term tailwinds for:
- Oil services, drilling (2025-2028)
- LNG infrastructure, pipelines
- Natural gas power for data centers

But accelerates long-term headwinds:
- Stranded asset risk in refineries, gas stations, ICE supply chain
- Capital misallocation if EV/solar adoption faster than plan assumes
- Loss of manufacturing competitiveness in batteries, EVs (ceding to China/EU)

**For companies**: If you're building 30-year infrastructure (pipelines, refineries, LNG terminals), the plan reduces regulatory risk but doesn't change the demand trajectory. The cost-curve physics still apply.

## The Bottom Line

The "energy dominance" plan optimizes US position within the **incumbent energy system** (oil, gas, coal). It does not address—or explicitly rejects—the **disruptor energy system** (solar, wind, batteries, EVs).

**The critical question**: Is it dominance to maximize production in a market entering structural decline? Or is it late-cycle capital misallocation?

By 2030-2035, "dominance" will be measured by:
- Cost of delivered electricity ($/kWh)
- Cost of mobility ($/passenger-mile)
- Cost of industrial heat/power ($/MMBtu equivalent)

The plan assumes fossil fuels will remain cost-competitive in these services. The disruption framework predicts they won't—not due to policy, but due to exponential cost declines in solar, batteries, and electric drivetrains.

**You should think of this as an incumbent optimization strategy during a phase change—maximizing position in System A while System B scales exponentially.**
