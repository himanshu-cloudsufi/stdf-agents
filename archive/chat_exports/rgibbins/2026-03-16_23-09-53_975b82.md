The 60-point reabsorption differential between London (73.6%) and Wales/Northern Ireland/North England (13.1–16.9%) is **not primarily driven by differences in displacement rates**. ### 1. Theoretical and Empirical Foundation for Differential Reabsorption

The reabsorption rate answers one question: *of every 100 workers displaced by AI, how many find new employment within the modeling window?* The framework draws on five bodies of labor economics literature:

**Task-Based Framework [C1]:** Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003) established that automation targets routine cognitive tasks while expanding non-routine cognitive tasks. This is the theoretical basis for why high-cognitive-density regions generate more new roles. The displacement model operationalizes this at the subtask level (89,040 subtasks across 341 occupations).

**Agglomeration Economics [C2]:** Rosenthal & Strange (2004) and Combes et al. (2012) establish that doubling employment density raises productivity 3–8% and accelerates labor market matching by 2–5% per doubling of density. London's 8,500 workers/km² versus Wales's 180 workers/km² creates a 47× density differential. The 28% matching efficiency premium for London is derived from these elasticities applied to that differential.

**Local Multiplier Effects [C3]:** Moretti (2010) found that each high-tech job creates 5 local service jobs in thick labor markets. This multiplier does not operate in thin markets — the Wales and Northern Ireland estimates explicitly exclude it. This single mechanism accounts for 28 percentage points of London's reabsorption advantage.

**Robots and Jobs [C4]:** Acemoglu & Restrepo (2018) found that each robot per 1,000 workers reduces employment by 0.18–0.34% in manufacturing-heavy regions. AI disruption is broader, but the geographic concentration of impact follows the same pattern — peripheral regions bear disproportionate net unemployment despite lower gross displacement.

**IPPR Automation and Regions (2023) [C5]:** UK-specific finding that automation exposure is London 28%, Wales 42%, NI 44%, NE 46% — peripheral regions face *higher* automation exposure despite lower cognitive-sector concentration, because routine cognitive tasks in healthcare, retail, and admin support are highly automatable.

# Agglomeration Economics and Moretti Multiplier: Validity Under AI Displacement

**Scope:** United Kingdom regional labor markets, 2026–2032. All displacement figures are SKILL OUTPUT (DETERMINISTIC) from the artificial-labor simulation (341 occupations, 143M workers, 7-month METR capability doubling model). All reabsorption rates, mechanism assessments, and revised ranges are DERIVED.

## 1. Agglomeration Economics and AI Displacement

### What the Framework Claims and Its Empirical Basis

Agglomeration economics (Ciccone & Hall 1996; Rosenthal & Strange 2004; Combes et al. 2012) establishes three operative mechanisms:

- **Labor market pooling:** Dense labor markets reduce search frictions — firms find workers faster, workers find jobs faster. Empirically, doubling employment density raises productivity 3–8% and accelerates matching speed 2–5% per doubling.
- **Knowledge spillovers:** Physical proximity enables tacit knowledge transfer between workers and firms. Empirically identified through wage premiums in dense cities that persist after controlling for worker characteristics.
- **Matching efficiency:** Thick markets produce better job-worker matches, reducing mismatch unemployment. Empirically, urban areas have lower structural unemployment rates than rural areas after controlling for sector mix.

**Empirical status:** These three mechanisms are robustly established for the period 1970–2020 in manufacturing and services labor markets. The evidence base is pre-AI, calibrated on human-capital-intensive economies where geographic proximity was the primary channel for knowledge transfer and labor market thickness.

### Critical Assessment: Do These Mechanisms Survive AI Displacement?

**Labor market pooling — WEAKENED, not eliminated [C1]**

Pooling requires a thick market of workers and vacancies. SKILL OUTPUT shows London's displacement rate reaching 36.4% by 2032 — higher than peripheral regions (33.2–33.5%) because London's sector concentration (Business-Financial 22%, Computer-IT 12%, Management 14%) sits precisely in the highest-displacement occupational bands (37.3%, 36.1%, 36.0% respectively). A thick market of displaced workers does not generate pooling benefits — it generates competition for a shrinking vacancy pool. The mechanism is not reversed, but its net contribution is sharply reduced when the vacancy side of the market is contracting.

**Knowledge spillovers — STRUCTURALLY INVERTED [C2]**

This is the most critical failure. The core mechanism of agglomeration — knowledge spillovers from physical proximity — is precisely what large language models replicate at zero marginal cost. A financial analyst in Cardiff accessing the same AI system as one in Canary Wharf receives an equivalent knowledge spillover. The geographic density premium for knowledge transfer collapses when AI distributes that knowledge to any location with internet connectivity. SKILL OUTPUT's capability model shows AI handling tasks up to 15.7 hours in duration by 2028, covering the overwhelming majority of cognitive work in London's financial and legal sectors. By 2029 (52-hour task duration), the knowledge spillover channel is effectively digitized. **EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED:** The pre-AI record is solid. **EXTRAPOLATION:** Applying it forward through 2032 is indefensible — the mechanism's physical-proximity requirement is eliminated by the same technology driving displacement.

**Matching efficiency — DEGRADED by 2028 [C3]**

Matching efficiency requires that displaced workers can be matched to new vacancies in the same dense market. This holds when displacement is cyclical (demand recovers, same roles refill) or sequential (old sector shrinks, new sector grows in same location). AI displacement is simultaneous across cognitive sectors — Business-Financial, Legal, Computer-IT, and Management all displace at 36–37% by 2032. There is no sequential lag for matching to operate within. The vacancy pool in London's dominant sectors contracts faster than new sectors emerge.

**DERIVED — Agglomeration mechanism degradation timeline:**

|------|-----------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| 2026 | 1.43 hrs | Intact | Intact |
| 2027 | 4.75 hrs | Degrading | Degrading |
| 2028 | 15.7 hrs | Substantially degraded | Substantially degraded |
| 2029 | 52 hrs | Largely digitized | Near-zero |
| 2030+ | 172+ hrs | Effectively zero | Effectively zero |

**Summary — Agglomeration:** The pre-AI empirical record is solid. Forward application through 2032 is an extrapolation that fails on the knowledge spillover channel (digitized by AI) and the matching efficiency channel (simultaneous displacement eliminates sequential reabsorption). Labor market pooling partially survives but contributes negatively when the vacancy pool contracts. **EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED: 2026 only. EXTRAPOLATION: 2027–2032.**

## 2. Local Multiplier Effects and AI Displacement

### What Moretti (2010) Actually Found

Moretti's empirical identification used variation in tradeable-sector employment across US cities 1980–2000 to estimate that each net new tradeable-sector job creates approximately 2.5 non-tradeable service jobs through local spending effects (the figure cited as "5:1" in some applications refers to the full multiplier chain including indirect effects; the direct empirical estimate is closer to 2.5 non-tradeable jobs per tradeable job).

**Conditions under which the multiplier holds:**
1. Net positive tradeable-sector employment growth (the input variable is positive)
2. Wage income flowing into local consumption (workers spend locally)
3. Sequential dynamics — tradeable job creation precedes service demand
4. Thick non-tradeable service markets capable of absorbing demand
5. Calibration timescale: 10–20 year decadal variation

### Critical Assessment: Are These Conditions Met Under AI Displacement?

**Condition 1 — Net positive tradeable employment: BROKEN from 2027 [C4]**

SKILL OUTPUT shows Business-and-Financial sector displacement at 14.9% by 2028 and 37.3% by 2032. London's tradeable sector IS the displacement target. The multiplier's input variable goes negative:

**DERIVED — London tradeable sector net employment trajectory:**

| Year | Displacement | Organic Growth | Net Employment | Moretti Status |
|------|-------------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| 2026 | 3.7% | ~3.5% | ~0% net | Barely operative |
| 2027 | 9.2% | ~3.5% | −5.7% net | **Broken** |
| 2028 | 17.1% | ~3.5% | −13.6% net | **Inverted** |
| 2029 | 24.5% | ~3.5% | −21.0% net | Strongly inverted |
| 2030–2032 | 30–36% | ~3.5% | −27% to −33% | Fully inverted |

From 2028 onward, the Moretti mechanism runs in reverse: tradeable sector job destruction propagates non-tradeable service job destruction through the same spending channel.

**Condition 2 — Wage income flowing to local consumption: WEAKENED [C5]**

AI productivity gains — the 50% of hours freed that flow to productivity in the skill model — accrue to firm owners and shareholders, not to displaced workers. Capital income has a lower local spending multiplier than wage income and a higher propensity to flow to financial assets. The spending channel that drives non-tradeable demand is attenuated.

**Condition 3 — Sequential dynamics: ABSENT [C6]**

Moretti was calibrated on US manufacturing and tech job creation (1980–2000), where tradeable employment grew and non-tradeable demand followed. AI displacement is simultaneous across tradeable AND non-tradeable sectors. SKILL OUTPUT shows customer service (39.4%), food preparation (34.3%), and personal care (34.1%) displacing at rates comparable to finance (37.3%). Both sides of the multiplier mechanism displace simultaneously.

**Condition 4 — Does the multiplier apply when AI-driven job creation is small relative to displaced volume?**

This is the user's specific challenge, and it is correct. The Moretti multiplier requires net new tradeable employment at meaningful scale. AI-adjacent role creation (prompt engineers, model auditors, AI governance specialists, automation consultants) is small in absolute number relative to displaced volume. SKILL OUTPUT displaces 1,892,800 London FTE by 2032. AI-adjacent roles in the UK are estimated at 50,000–150,000 total by 2030 (DERIVED from ONS tech sector employment growth rates). Even applying the full 2.5 multiplier to 150,000 new AI roles generates 375,000 non-tradeable jobs — against 1,892,800 displaced. The multiplier cannot close a gap of that magnitude. **EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED: The mechanism exists. SPECULATIVE: That it operates at sufficient scale to matter for reabsorption.**

**Condition 5 — Calibration timescale: MISMATCHED**

Moretti's identification uses decadal variation. The 2026–2032 analysis window is 6 years — shorter than the mechanism's adjustment timescale. Even if operative in principle, it would not manifest within the forecast horizon.

**Summary — Moretti multiplier:** The empirical finding is real and robust for the conditions under which it was estimated. Those conditions — net positive tradeable employment, sequential dynamics, wage-income spending channel, sufficient scale — are all absent or inverted under AI displacement in the 2026–2032 window. **EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED: The mechanism exists in pre-AI labor markets. SPECULATIVE: That it applies to AI-driven displacement at the scale and speed modeled here.**

**Mechanism 4 — Geographic mobility as a two-way channel [C10]**

London absorbs displaced workers from peripheral regions through in-migration. This improves London's reabsorption rate at the cost of worsening peripheral region rates. The net effect on the national structural unemployment rate is zero — it is a distributional mechanism, not a reabsorption mechanism.

**DERIVED — London reabsorption without Moretti/agglomeration, with alternative mechanisms:**

```
Base mechanisms:           33.6pp
Wage premium/reskilling:   +5.0pp
VC/startup ecosystem:      +6.0pp  
Sector diversity:          +4.0pp
Geographic mobility (net): +0.0pp (distributional only)
─────────────────────────────────
Revised London central:    48.6%
```

This 48.6% figure represents the analytically defensible London reabsorption estimate when Moretti and agglomeration are removed but alternative structural mechanisms are credited. It produces a London structural unemployment rate of approximately 19.5% by 2032 — still better than peripheral regions (23–26%) but the differential narrows from 60pp to approximately 5–6pp.

**Is the differential sufficient to sustain a meaningful London reabsorption advantage?**

The honest answer is: marginally. The 5–6pp differential (London ~19.5% vs. periphery ~25%) is real and driven by durable structural factors (wage premium, VC concentration, sector diversity). But it is a fraction of the 60pp differential in the prior analysis. The prior analysis's London advantage was almost entirely an artefact of applying mechanisms that do not survive AI displacement conditions. The residual advantage — driven by wage premium, startup ecosystem, and sector diversity — is meaningful but does not support the characterization of London as a high-reabsorption region. At 19.5–24.2% structural unemployment, London is a moderately-better-than-peripheral-regions outcome, not the resilient urban labor market the prior 73.6% figure implied.
