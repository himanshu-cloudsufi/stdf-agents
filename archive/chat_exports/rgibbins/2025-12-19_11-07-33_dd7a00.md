## Step 1: AI Capability Growth Model

AI task-handling capability follows exponential growth with 7-month doubling time (empirically measured from METR benchmarks):

$$
C(t) = C_0 \cdot 2^{(t - t_0)/T_d}
$$

Where:
- \( C(t) \) = AI capability in seconds at time \( t \)
- \( C_0 = 1,560 \) seconds (26 minutes baseline at 2025)
- \( T_d = 0.583 \) years (7-month doubling time)
- \( t \) = year
- \( t_0 = 2025 \) (baseline year)

| Year | Doubling Periods | Capability (seconds) | Human Equivalent |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------|
| 2025 | 0.0 | 1,560 | 26 minutes |
| 2026 | 1.7 | 5,190 | 1.4 hours |
| 2027 | 3.4 | 17,270 | 4.8 hours |
| 2028 | 5.1 | 57,450 | 16 hours |
| 2029 | 6.9 | 191,100 | 2.2 days |
| 2030 | 8.6 | 635,700 | 7.4 days |

## Step 2: Task-Level Automation Calculation

For each job category \( j \), automation percentage is calculated using weighted task analysis:

$$
A_j(t) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot \mathbb{1}(C(t) \geq D_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i} \times 100\%
$$

Where:
- \( w_i = \frac{R_i \cdot I_i}{10,000} \) (task weight = relevance × importance)
- \( R_i \) = task relevance score (0-100)
- \( I_i \) = task importance score (0-100)
- \( D_i \) = task duration in seconds
- \( \mathbb{1}(C(t) \geq D_i) \) = indicator function (1 if automatable, 0 otherwise)

**Example: Customer Service Representatives (2027)**

Selected tasks with weights:
| Task | Duration | Relevance | Importance | Weight | Automatable in 2027? |
|------|----------|-----------|------------|--------|---------------------|
| Answer customer queries | 600s (10 min) | 95 | 90 | 0.855 | Yes (17,270s > 600s) |
| Process complaints | 1,800s (30 min) | 90 | 85 | 0.765 | Yes |
| Update records | 300s (5 min) | 80 | 75 | 0.600 | Yes |
| Escalate complex issues | 25,200s (7 hrs) | 70 | 95 | 0.665 | No (17,270s < 25,200s) |

$$
A_{CSR}(2027) = \frac{0.855 \cdot 1 + 0.765 \cdot 1 + 0.600 \cdot 1 + 0.665 \cdot 0}{0.855 + 0.765 + 0.600 + 0.665} \times 100\% = 76.6\%
$$

By 2028 (capability = 57,450s = 16 hours), escalation tasks become automatable → 100% automation.

## Step 3: Implementation Lag Model (S-Curve Adoption)

Corporate adoption follows logistic curve with 1-year lag between technical capability and mass deployment:

$$
D_{actual}(t) = D_{max} \cdot \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(t - t_0 - \Delta t)}}
$$

Where:
- \( D_{actual}(t) \) = actual jobs displaced in year \( t \)
- \( D_{max} \) = technical displacement potential in year \( t \)
- \( k = 1.2 \) (adoption curve steepness)
- \( t_0 = 2025 \) (capability baseline)
- \( \Delta t = 1.0 \) year (implementation lag)
- \( e \) = Euler's number

**Displacement Gap Analysis:**

| Year | \( D_{technical} \) | \( D_{actual} \) | Gap | Gap Closure Rate |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|-----|-----------------|
| 2025 | 32.4M | 1.6M | 30.8M | 5% |
| 2026 | 58.3M | 1.6M | 56.6M | 3% |
| 2027 | 84.4M | 26.6M | 57.9M | 31% |
| 2028 | 92.5M | 62.7M | 29.7M | 68% |
| 2029 | 93.1M | 84.8M | 8.2M | 91% |
| 2030 | 93.1M | 91.5M | 1.6M | 98% |

The S-curve inflection occurs in 2027 when adoption accelerates from 3% to 31% closure rate.

## Step 4: Cost Parity Analysis

AI labor cost follows exponential decline (approximately 68% annual reduction 2024-2028):

$$
Cost_{AI}(t) = Cost_{AI,0} \cdot e^{-\lambda (t - t_0)}
$$

Where:
- \( Cost_{AI,0} = 10.00 \) $/hour (2024 baseline)
- \( \lambda = 1.13 \) (annual decline rate)
- Human cost assumed constant at $35.00/hour

**Cost Advantage Ratio:**

$$
R_{cost}(t) = \frac{Cost_{AI}(t)}{Cost_{human}} = \frac{10 \cdot e^{-1.13(t-2024)}}{35}
$$

| Year | AI Cost | Human Cost | Ratio | Advantage |
|------|---------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| 2024 | $10.00 | $35.00 | 28.6% | 3.5× cheaper |
| 2025 | $3.16 | $35.00 | 9.0% | 11× cheaper |
| 2026 | $1.00 | $35.00 | 2.9% | 35× cheaper |
| 2027 | $0.32 | $35.00 | 0.9% | 109× cheaper |
| 2028 | $0.10 | $35.00 | 0.3% | 350× cheaper |
| 2030 | $0.01 | $35.00 | 0.03% | 3,500× cheaper |

**Economic Tipping Point**: Cost parity crossed in 2024-2025 for most knowledge work. By 2026, AI is 35× cheaper, creating irresistible economic pressure for corporate adoption despite 1-year lag.

- \( H_{annual} = 2,080 \) hours/year (full-time equivalent)
- \( Cost_{human} = 35.00 \) $/hour
- \( Cost_{AI}(t) \) = AI cost in year \( t \)

- **Disruption role**: Enables automation of visual inspection, monitoring, quality control

- **Capability trajectory**: Dexterity improving, learning from demonstration, warehouse-ready (2024), general-purpose (2027+)
- **Disruption role**: Extends AI disruption from cognitive → physical tasks

### Convergence Pathways

**Pathway 1: Knowledge Work Automation**
\( LLMs + Agentic AI + APIs \) → Autonomous business processes

- **Timeline**: 2025-2027 tipping point
- **Affected roles**: Customer service (2026), accounting (2027), legal research (2027), software development (2028)
- **Capability unlock**: Multi-hour task chains with 80% reliability → most white-collar jobs automatable

**Pathway 2: Physical Work Automation**
\( Computer Vision + Robotics + LLM Control \) → General-purpose physical labor

- **Timeline**: 2027-2029 tipping point
- **Affected roles**: Warehouse work (2027), delivery (2028), manufacturing assembly (2029), construction (2030+)
- **Capability unlock**: Visual understanding + manipulation + adaptive learning → factory-floor to field deployment

\( Multimodal AI + Robotics + Real-time Planning \) → Complex service jobs

- **Timeline**: 2028-2030 tipping point
- **Affected roles**: Healthcare (nursing tasks 2028), food service (2029), retail (2029), home services (2030)
- **Capability unlock**: Interpret environment + physical interaction + social communication → service sector automation

**Phase 1 (2025-2026): Early Knowledge Work**
- Convergence driver: LLMs reach 26-min → 1.4-hour capability
- Jobs affected: Customer service, data entry, basic coding
- Corporate adoption: 5% (early adopters)

**Phase 2 (2027-2028): Mass Knowledge Work Collapse**
- Convergence driver: Agentic AI + 4.8-hour → 16-hour capability

- Jobs affected: Warehouse, logistics, manufacturing, healthcare support

- **Timing impact**: Shortens 2026-2028 adoption window by 6-12 months vs. pure hardware scaling

- **Timing impact**: Physical automation catches up to cognitive faster than expected (2028 vs. 2030+)

- **Effect**: Self-reinforcing economic pressure once adoption begins
- **Timing impact**: Creates S-curve inflection in 2027 (lag releases pent-up demand)

- **Implication**: Shift from "labor-intensive" to "capital-intensive with human augmentation"

**Critical Caveats:**

1. **Coverage**: Analysis covers 93.1M US jobs across 186 occupations (~60% of US workforce). Remaining 40% may have different automation profiles.

2. **Adoption Lag**: Model applies 1-year lag between technical capability and mass corporate adoption. Actual lag varies by sector, company size, and regulatory environment.

3. **Job Creation Not Modeled**: This forecast estimates displacement only. Does not account for new jobs created by AI deployment, economic adaptation, or emerging industries.

4. **Binary Automation Model**: Tasks modeled as fully automatable or not automatable based on capability threshold. In practice, partial automation and human-AI collaboration are common transitional states.

5. **Physical Automation Dependency**: Physical jobs (9.6% of analyzed workforce) require robotics deployment. Cost trajectories and regulatory barriers differ from pure software AI, potentially delaying displacement 1-2 years beyond cognitive work.

6. **Policy Uncertainty**: Analysis assumes baseline policy environment. Aggressive regulation (automation taxes, deployment restrictions) could slow adoption. Conversely, government incentives could accelerate displacement.

---

This analysis presents a **capability-driven disruption scenario** where exponential AI growth and 1-year implementation lag create rapid labor market transformation 2027-2030. The 62.7M jobs displaced by 2028 (67% of analyzed workforce) represents technical and economic viability, not a prediction of actual outcomes. Policy responses, corporate adoption speed, and job creation dynamics will determine whether the result is economic disruption or smooth transition to an AI-augmented economy.
