## The Interconnection Queue: Regulatory Architecture and Delays

The interconnection queue is a **regulatory approval process** that governs how new generation assets (solar, wind, batteries, gas plants) connect to the transmission grid. It exists because the grid is a shared network with physical constraints: adding new generation affects voltage stability, fault currents, and power flows across the entire system. The queue ensures that each new project undergoes engineering studies to verify it will not destabilize the grid and identifies required transmission upgrades.

**What the Queue Governs:**

1. **Feasibility Study**: Initial assessment of whether the grid can physically accommodate the project at the proposed location. Timeline: 6-12 months.
2. **System Impact Study**: Detailed analysis of how the project affects grid stability, voltage, and fault currents. Identifies required transmission upgrades (new lines, transformers, substations). Timeline: 12-18 months.
3. **Facilities Study**: Engineering design and cost estimate for required upgrades. Timeline: 6-12 months.
4. **Interconnection Agreement**: Legal contract specifying upgrade responsibilities, cost allocation, and connection timeline. Timeline: 6-12 months.

Total timeline: **2-4 years** from queue entry to grid connection approval. The queue backlog as of 2026 is **2,400 GW** of solar and wind projects—five times the total installed capacity in 2020.

**Why Delays Occur:**

- **Serial Processing**: Projects are studied sequentially, not in parallel. Each study waits for prior projects to complete.
- **Transmission Upgrade Bottlenecks**: If a project requires a new 500 kV transmission line (5-year permitting + 3-year construction), the project cannot connect until that line is built—regardless of when the study completes.
- **Utility Incentive Misalignment**: Transmission owners (utilities) have no economic incentive to accelerate interconnection. Faster approvals do not increase their regulated rate-of-return; slower approvals protect their existing generation assets from competition.
- **FERC Jurisdiction Limits**: FERC (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) governs interstate transmission but not intrastate projects. State-level interconnection procedures vary widely and are often slower than FERC-jurisdictional processes.

FERC Order 2023 (issued April 2023) reforms interconnection procedures by introducing cluster studies (batching projects) and requiring developers to post financial security earlier to reduce speculative applications. Implementation is ongoing through 2026-2028, but the order does **not** eliminate the queue—it streamlines procedures. The 2,400 GW backlog persists.
