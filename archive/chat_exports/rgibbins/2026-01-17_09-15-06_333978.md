---

## **1. Technical Capability Timeline** ✅ **HIGH VALUE**
**When AI CAN do the job** — this is **cost + capability parity**, the foundation of all disruption analysis.

What I CAN model robustly:
- **Capability trajectories** (e.g., "AI passes 80% reliability threshold for task X by year Y")
- **Cost curves** (e.g., "Cost per inference drops below human wage-equivalent by 2027")
- **Tipping points** (e.g., "When does AI + robotics reach $5/hour equivalent for warehouse picking?")

This is **pure physics/economics** — no policy assumptions needed. It answers: *"When does the technology BECOME capable of displacing labor at lower cost?"*

**Example output:**
- "AI capability for legal contract review reaches 80% reliability by Q2 2026"
- "Cost per inference drops to $0.002 by 2028, making it 100× cheaper than human paralegal hour"
- "Tipping point: 2027 ±6 months (when cost + capability both favor AI)"

---

## **2. Observed Displacement Velocity** ✅ **MEDIUM-HIGH VALUE**
**What's actually happening now** — empirical data on early adoption.

This grounds analysis in **reality** vs. speculation. Tracks:
- Job postings trends (declining demand for specific roles)
- Enterprise AI adoption rates (% of companies deploying automation)
- Productivity metrics (output per worker rising without headcount growth)

**Limitation:** Early-stage data is noisy. Many sectors haven't hit tipping yet, so current velocity ≠ post-tipping velocity.

**Example output:**
- "Legal assistant job postings down 12% YoY in US (2024-2025)"
- "Enterprise adoption of AI contract tools: 8% of law firms (still pre-tipping)"
- "Post-tipping analog: When EVs hit cost parity in China (2022), adoption accelerated from 15% → 35% in 2 years"

---

## **3. Scenario Frameworks** ⚠️ **USE SPARINGLY**
**If X adoption speed + Y policy, then Z** — this is where I need discipline.

The Seba Framework uses scenarios for **constraints/modifiers**, not primary drivers. Scenarios should be:
- **Anchored to capability timelines** (not floating policy narratives)
- **Bounded by physics** (can't assume AI stops improving or costs rise)
- **Focused on adoption speed variance**, not whether disruption happens

**Valid use:**
- "Strong/Early scenario: AI capability threshold + rapid enterprise adoption (no regulatory friction) → 60% of paralegal tasks automated by 2030"
- "Delayed scenario: AI capability threshold + slow enterprise adoption (union resistance, licensing rules) → same 60% but by 2035"

**Invalid use:**
- "Policy X prevents all displacement" (ignores cost/capability inevitability)
- "Education solves everything" (doesn't explain where new demand comes from)

---

## **4. Leading Indicators to Watch** ✅ **HIGH VALUE**
**Empirical signals** that validate or invalidate the capability timeline.

This is **disciplined forecasting hygiene**. Watch for:
- **Cost curve deviations** (is inference cost declining as expected?)
- **Capability benchmark surprises** (does AI hit 80% reliability faster/slower?)
- **Early adopter data** (what's happening in sectors already past tipping?)
- **Policy wildcards** (regulatory bans, subsidy shifts)

**Example output:**
- "Key signal: If legal AI achieves 85% accuracy on bar exam by mid-2026, tipping point confirms for 2027"
- "Red flag: If enterprise adoption stalls below 10% despite cost parity, reassess friction assumptions"
- "Analog: China EV adoption accelerated 2× faster than Europe post-tipping due to lower regulatory friction"

---

## **My Recommendation: Hybrid Approach**

For labor disruption queries, I should structure responses as:

### **A. Capability Timeline (Core Forecast)**
- When AI reaches cost + capability parity for specific job categories
- Tipping point windows (not point estimates)
- Derived from technical benchmarks + cost curves

### **B. Observed Velocity (Current State)**
- What's happening in early-adopter sectors
- Job posting trends, enterprise adoption data
- "We are here" markers on the disruption curve

### **C. Adoption Range (Scenario Bounds)**
- Fast scenario: Low friction (China-like adoption speed)
- Slow scenario: High friction (Europe-like adoption speed)
- Both hit same endpoint, differ on timing

### **D. Leading Indicators (Validation Checkpoints)**
- What data to watch to confirm/invalidate forecast
- Which benchmarks matter most
- Policy wildcards that could shift timing (not direction)
