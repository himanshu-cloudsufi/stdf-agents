# Iran/Strait of Hormuz Crisis: Impact on India's Power Sector, Macroeconomy, and Renewables IPPs

*Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded unless specifically requested.*

---

## Thesis

The 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis does not create India's clean disruption trajectory — it **compresses it by two years**. Solar LCOE in India crossed below new coal in 2022 and below gas in 2023. The shock raises gas-fired power LCOE to ~$90/MWh against a solar LCOE of $28/MWh [C1] — a 3.2x cost disadvantage for gas that widens to 6.1x by 2030 [C2]. The economic gravity was already overwhelming. The shock makes it politically and financially impossible to ignore simultaneously.

## 1. India's Power Sector

### Current Generation Mix (2025 Baseline)

|---|---|---|
| Coal (domestic) | 1,365 | 70.0% |
| Solar PV | 137 | 7.0% |
| Wind | 111 | 5.7% |

India's installed base at end-2025: **132 GW solar, 47 GW wind** [C3]. The SWB stack contributes 13% of generation — but all cost tipping points have already been crossed. No new coal or gas plant commissioned after 2022 is economically rational [C4].

### Shock Impact on Merit Order

The Hormuz disruption raises India's effective gas-fired LCOE from $65/MWh (base) to **$90/MWh** (shock) [C5] — a 38% increase driven by LNG spot premium. The merit order consequence is precise:

- **Short term (2026–2028):** Coal ($38/MWh marginal cost) displaces gas in dispatch. India's domestic coal insulation from the shock means coal temporarily absorbs baseload previously served by gas. Net emissions impact in 2026–2028 is approximately neutral — coal substitutes for gas.
- **Structural (2028–2032):** Solar at $14.8/MWh by 2030 [C6] displaces both gas and coal. The shock does not change the structural outcome; it accelerates the timeline.

The 10x cost improvement threshold (solar vs. gas) is crossed in **2028 under shock conditions**, vs. 2031 under base case [C7]. This is the empirical rupture trigger — the point at which economic gravity becomes irreversible.

By 2036, the SWB stack approaches full demand coverage, enabling Superpower conditions: surplus zero-marginal-cost electricity available for industrial electrification and desalination.

### Gas Stranding: Begins 2026

India's ~25 GW of gas-fired capacity [C9] becomes economically non-viable at $90/MWh fuel cost. At capacity factors collapsing below 10%, these plants cannot cover fixed costs. **Economic stranding begins in 2026** under shock conditions — 8 years ahead of the natural cost-curve timeline. Stranded asset value at risk: ~$8–12 billion [C10].

### Grid Stability

The short-term grid stability implication is coal-supportive: domestic coal ($38/MWh) fills the gas dispatch gap. India's grid does not face a stability crisis from the Hormuz shock — it faces an **accelerated investment decision** on SWB + storage to replace gas peaking capacity. The primary grid constraint is transmission infrastructure, not generation economics.

## 2. India's Macroeconomy

### Current Account and Fiscal Pressure

India imports ~4.5 mb/d of crude oil [C11]. A sustained $30/barrel shock above pre-crisis levels generates an **additional ~$49 billion/year** in import costs [C12] — equivalent to ~1.3% of GDP. This widens India's current account deficit from ~1.7% to approximately **3.0% of GDP** [C13], historically the threshold where RBI FX intervention intensifies.

The rupee faces ~7% depreciation pressure [C14]. This creates a secondary feedback: solar module import costs rise in INR terms, partially offsetting the SWB cost advantage — but not reversing it. At 3.2x cost advantage, a 7% currency move is noise, not a structural brake.

### Inflation Pass-Through

The shock delivers an estimated **+2.2 percentage point direct CPI impact**, rising to **+3.8 pp total** with second-round effects [C15]. India's fuel price sensitivity is structurally high — transport fuel costs represent a larger share of household budgets than in OECD economies. The RBI faces a classic supply-side inflation dilemma: rate hikes are the wrong tool for a cost-push shock. The RBI's most impactful lever is FX intervention and macroprudential guidance on fossil fuel loan exposure, not rate policy [C16].

### Fiscal Pressure from Energy Subsidies

India's energy subsidy system (LPG, kerosene, fuel oil) faces maximum stress. The $49B/year import bill increase represents ~30% of India's fiscal deficit budget [C17]. The government faces a binary choice: absorb the fiscal cost of subsidies or accelerate the domestic SWB deployment that eliminates import dependency. The cost-curve dynamics make the second option not just preferable but **economically self-reinforcing** — SWB investment at 3.2x cost advantage does not require subsidies to be competitive.

The Germany-Russia gas crisis (2022) is the direct structural analog [C18]. Germany spent $41 billion annually on fossil fuel subsidies [C19] — comparable to India's shock exposure. RethinkX documented how that crisis created an "unprecedented opportunity" to accelerate SWB deployment [C20]. India's shock is larger in absolute fiscal terms and arrives when India's SWB cost advantage is even more pronounced.

### GDP Growth Trajectory

The shock imposes a **short-term GDP headwind of ~0.8–1.2% in 2026** via import cost inflation, current account deterioration, and consumer purchasing power compression. However, the structural GDP trajectory is positive: every dollar redirected from LNG imports to domestic SWB manufacturing and deployment stays within the Indian economy. India's electricity demand CAGR of 7.4% (2025–2036) [C21] — driven by industrial electrification, EV adoption, and datacenter expansion — is the demand engine that justifies ~1,032 GW of new SWB capacity. At SWB cost curves, 100% of incremental demand is served by solar and wind.

## 3. India's Renewables Sector and Listed IPPs

### Does the Shock Accelerate SWB Deployment? Yes — Structurally.

Three reinforcing feedback loops activate simultaneously:

**R1 — Cost-Curve Feedback (Strengthened):** The shock raises the fossil cost floor from $65/MWh to $90/MWh while solar continues declining from $28/MWh toward $14.8/MWh by 2030. The cost gap widens from $47/MWh (base) to $75/MWh (shock) in 2025 — a 60% larger cost advantage. Every rupee of LNG import cost is a rupee of incentive to accelerate domestic solar manufacturing [C22].

**R2 — Fiscal Pressure → Policy Acceleration:** The $49B/year import bill increase forces the government to choose between subsidizing fossil imports indefinitely or fast-tracking domestic SWB. Emergency permitting reform, PLI scheme expansion for solar manufacturing, and accelerated grid investment become fiscally rational responses [C23].

**R5 — Stranded Asset Death Spiral:** Markets reprice gas-fired IPP assets downward → cost of capital rises → capacity factors fall as SWB displaces dispatch → fixed costs spread over fewer kWh → gas LCOE rises further → more displacement. The 25 GW of stranded gas capacity ($10B) enters this death spiral in 2026 [C24].

### IPP Project Economics

The shock **improves** solar and wind IPP project economics and **destroys** gas IPP economics simultaneously:

- Solar IPPs: LCOE $28/MWh (2025) → $14.8/MWh (2030), against a wholesale market increasingly priced at gas-shock rates. Corporate PPA demand from industrial users (steel, cement, chemicals, data centers) accelerates as buyers lock in $28/MWh solar rates before further demand-driven price increases.
- Gas IPPs: Capacity factors collapse below 10% under merit order displacement. Plants cannot cover fixed costs. Balance sheet deterioration forces portfolio reallocation toward SWB.
- Wind IPPs: LCOE $32/MWh (2025) → $21/MWh (2030) [C25]. Wind revival constrained by grid connectivity and land acquisition — the primary structural brake on deployment acceleration.

## India Gas Capacity Factor Analysis: Testing the Peaking Hypothesis

*Analysis reflects cost-curve dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded unless specifically requested.*

---

### Thesis

The user hypothesis is **partially correct but structurally incomplete**. India's gas fleet does operate at a low system-wide capacity factor (~26%), and OCGT peaking plants run at 10–15% CF. However, low CF does not mute the Hormuz shock or the stranded asset conclusion — it changes the *magnitude* of losses, not the *sign*. Gas is already the last fuel in India's merit order at $65–80/MWh. A Hormuz shock to $90/MWh widens the gap but does not alter dispatch sequencing. Stranding is structural, not cyclical.

---

### 1. Capacity Factor Profile by Plant Type

**[SKILL OUTPUT — India disaggregated from RoW framework]**

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CCGT (baseload) | 10.0 | 6.0 | 38–55% | 38% | 33.3 TWh |

| Stranded (idle) | 14.5 | 0 | 0% | 0% | 0 TWh |
| **System Total** | **25.0** | **10.5** | — | **26%** | **~57 TWh** |

The 26% system CF is 14 percentage points below the Rest of World average of 40.1% [C1]. The gap is explained by ~14.5 GW of CCGT capacity — built in anticipation of domestic gas supply that never materialized — operating at 0% CF. These are not peaking plants; they are fully stranded assets generating zero revenue.

The user hypothesis of 10–20% CF applies specifically to the OCGT peaking cohort (15 GW installed, 4.5 GW operational). This is accurate for that sub-fleet. But the CCGT baseload cohort (10 GW installed, 6 GW operational) runs at 38–55% CF when gas is available — a fundamentally different utilization profile [C2].

### 3. Stranded Asset Calculations: CF Sensitivity

**[DERIVED from skill outputs — stranded asset P&L by CF scenario]**

| CF Scenario | Annual Generation | Energy Margin Loss | Fixed Cost | Net P&L | Stranded? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Low CF (10%) | 21.9 TWh | −$526M | −$500M | **−$851M/yr** | YES |
| Low CF (15%) | 32.9 TWh | −$788M | −$500M | **−$1,113M/yr** | YES |
| User hypothesis (20%) | 43.8 TWh | −$1,051M | −$500M | **−$1,376M/yr** | YES |
| Current system (26%) | 56.9 TWh | −$1,367M | −$500M | **−$1,692M/yr** | YES |

*Energy margin = (market price $47/MWh − variable cost $65–80/MWh) × generation; Fixed cost = $20/kW/year × 25 GW; Capacity payments = +$175M/yr offset applied [C4]*

The counterintuitive result: **lower CF produces smaller annual losses**. At 10% CF, the fleet burns less gas at a loss ($526M energy margin loss) and fixed cost dominates. The stranding mechanism shifts from "fixed cost exceeds revenue" at low CF to "energy margin loss dominates" at high CF — but the sign never flips. Stranding is structural because India's average pool price (~$47/MWh) is permanently below gas LCOE ($65/MWh base, $90/MWh shock) [C5].

Book value of stranded assets: 25 GW at $650M/GW = $16.2B replacement value. At average plant age 15 years and 25-year depreciation life, book value = $6.5B. Of this, 14.5 GW already stranded = **$3.8B already impaired**. The remaining 10.5 GW operational fleet ($2.7B book value) is on trajectory to full impairment by 2033 [C6].

Low CF (10–20%) is not a defense against displacement — it is the signature of a fleet already in the early stages of stranding. The X-Curve dynamic is operating now: as SWB capacity grows, gas dispatch hours fall, fixed costs spread over fewer MWh, and economics deteriorate further. The Hormuz shock's power-sector impact is partially muted by the peaking structure — the user hypothesis holds for this narrow dimension. But the shock's macroeconomic impact ($49B/year, ~1.3% of GDP) is structurally dominated by non-power gas demand, making the peaking/baseload distinction irrelevant to the current account and fiscal conclusions [C12].
