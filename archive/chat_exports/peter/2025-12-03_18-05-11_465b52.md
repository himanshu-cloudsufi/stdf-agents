# Europe Gas Import Disruption Analysis Plan

## Analysis Approach

This analysis applies the **cost-driven energy disruption framework** to Europe's natural gas imports. The core thesis: Solar-Wind-Battery (SWB) systems reach cost parity with gas generation, triggering exponential capacity growth that displaces gas demand in power generation (Europe's largest gas use case at ~25-30% of total gas consumption). As SWB capacity scales, gas import volumes decline, reshaping Europe's energy security and supplier dependencies.

**Key Disruption Dynamics**:
- **SWB cost advantage**: LCOE of solar+wind+battery falls below gas generation costs (including fuel + carbon costs)
- **Exponential capacity growth**: Post-tipping, SWB capacity grows 15-25% annually, displacing gas generation
- **Sequential displacement**: Coal displaced first (already underway), then gas baseload, then gas peaking
- **Import composition shifts**: As total gas demand falls, pipeline vs LNG shares adjust based on contract flexibility, infrastructure, and geopolitical constraints

## Methodology Overview

**Phase 1: SWB Disruption Modeling** (using energy-forecasting skill)
- Calculate SWB stack cost parity vs gas generation in Europe
- Model SWB capacity growth using YoY averaging methodology (exponential trends, not linear)
- Forecast SWB generation displacement of gas-fired power (TWh/year)

**Phase 2: Gas Demand Translation**
- Convert displaced gas generation → reduced gas consumption (using thermal efficiency ~45-55%)
- Apply to Europe's total gas demand breakdown: Power generation (~27%), Industry (~28%), Residential/Commercial (~35%), Other (~10%)
- Model demand decline trajectory accounting for non-power uses (slower displacement, industrial electrification lags)

**Phase 3: Import Sourcing Analysis**
- Baseline import structure (2023-2024): Norway pipeline (~25%), Russia pipeline (~8% post-2022 drop), LNG USA (~15%), LNG Qatar (~10%), Algeria pipeline (~8%), Azerbaijan pipeline (~7%), Other (~27%)
- Project sourcing evolution under declining demand: Long-term pipeline contracts (Norway, Azerbaijan) maintain share, flexible LNG (USA) adjusts to demand, Russia remains constrained
- Calculate annual import volumes by source (bcm/year) and type (pipeline vs LNG)

**Phase 4: Strategic Implications**
- Energy security: Reduced import dependency, diversified sources vs concentrated suppliers
- Cost impacts: SWB zero marginal cost vs gas fuel costs + carbon pricing
- Infrastructure: Stranded LNG terminals, pipeline underutilization, hydrogen conversion potential
- Geopolitical: Shift from gas leverage (Russia) to technology/manufacturing power (China solar/batteries, EU wind)

## Data Sources

- SWB capacity and cost trajectories (solar, wind, battery storage) for Europe
- European gas generation baseline and thermal efficiency
- Historical gas import volumes by source country/region (2020-2024)
- Pipeline vs LNG infrastructure capacity and contract structures
- European power demand and generation mix

## Expected Outputs

**Quantitative Deliverables**:
- SWB cost parity timing and LCOE trajectories (€/MWh)
- Gas generation displacement timeline (TWh/year displaced, 2025-2035)
- Total gas import volume forecasts (bcm/year, 2025-2035)

**Strategic Insights**:
- Tipping point identification: When SWB displaces >50% of gas generation
- Energy security transformation: Import dependency reduction trajectory
- Supplier power shifts: Which sources maintain/lose market share
- Infrastructure implications: Underutilized capacity, stranded assets
- Decarbonization enablement: Emissions reduction from gas displacement
- Cost savings: Zero marginal cost SWB vs fuel/carbon costs

**Scenario Framework**:
- **Fast/Tech-First**: Aggressive SWB buildout, rapid gas displacement (20-25% annual SWB growth)
- **Base/Central**: Moderate SWB growth, steady gas decline (15-18% annual SWB growth)
- **Slow/Friction-Heavy**: Policy/infrastructure delays, slower displacement (10-12% annual SWB growth)
