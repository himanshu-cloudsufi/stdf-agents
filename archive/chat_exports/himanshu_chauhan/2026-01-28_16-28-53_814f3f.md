**Methodology**

Unemployment forecasting requires separating cyclical labor market dynamics from structural technology-driven displacement. The artificial-labor skill provides bottom-up AI displacement trajectories by occupation and age cohort. However, **December 2026 sits at the leading edge of measurable AI labor impact** [C3]. Displacement accelerates post-2027 as AI capability crosses task-level cost parity and adoption S-curves steepen.

For December 2026 specifically:
- **Cyclical component dominates**: Historical USA unemployment volatility (3.5–10% range 2000–2023) driven by business cycles, Fed policy, and demand shocks.
- **Structural AI displacement remains <0.3 percentage points** [C4]: Early adopters in customer service and back-office roles begin layoffs, but economy-wide penetration lags capability by 12–24 months.
- **Labor force participation and demographic trends**: Aging workforce and participation rate shifts (~63% baseline) modulate headline unemployment independently of AI.

**Assumptions & Limitations**

- **No major exogenous shocks**: Excludes geopolitical crises, financial system failures, or pandemic-scale disruptions.
- **AI adoption follows cost-parity logic**: Enterprises adopt when AI task cost < human wage + benefits. Regulatory or cultural resistance could delay by 6–12 months.
- **Displacement precedes productivity**: Early AI adoption replaces workers faster than it creates new roles. Net job creation from AI-enabled industries materializes post-2028.
