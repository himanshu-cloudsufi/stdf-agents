This forecast reflects AI-driven job displacement beginning to materialize in customer service, production supervision, and health aide roles, partially offset by continued economic expansion and labor force churn. ## Methodology

The forecast integrates three components:

1. **Baseline unemployment dynamics**: Historical USA unemployment volatility and current labor market tightness (January 2025 ~4.0%) [C2].
2. **AI-driven displacement**: Bottom-up task-level modeling of job loss in exposed occupations using capability thresholds, cost-parity tipping points, and adoption S-curves [C3].
3. **Offsetting factors**: GDP growth, labor force exits (retirements, reskilling), and new job creation in AI-adjacent sectors [C4].

The artificial-labor skill was applied to quantify displacement in customer service, production supervisors, and health aides—three occupations with high AI exposure and available headcount data. Displacement estimates were aggregated and divided by the USA labor force (~165 million) to compute the unemployment rate delta.

## Limitations

- **Data gaps**: No real-time USA unemployment, labor force participation, or GDP data available in the dataset catalog. Baseline and offsetting factors rely on typical historical ranges.
- **Scope**: Analysis covers three high-exposure occupations (~8 million workers total). Economy-wide displacement may be higher if AI penetrates additional white-collar roles (legal, finance, software) faster than modeled.
- **Reemployment assumptions**: The 50–70% reemployment rate within 12 months is a historical average; AI-displaced workers may face longer transitions if skill mismatches are severe.
- **Macroeconomic shocks**: A recession, financial crisis, or policy shock could push unemployment significantly higher independent of AI effects.

## Upside Optionality

- **Faster GDP growth** (3.0%+) from AI productivity gains could absorb displaced workers more quickly, keeping unemployment closer to 4.5%.
- **Slower AI adoption** due to regulatory friction or integration challenges could delay displacement, resulting in unemployment closer to baseline (~4.0–4.5%).
