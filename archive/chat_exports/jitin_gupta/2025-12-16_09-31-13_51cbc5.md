## Germany Natural Gas Demand Peak Analysis

**Germany's natural gas demand peaked in 2019 at 95.7 BCM**. By 2023, demand had declined 14% to 82.4 BCM. Coal will be displaced FIRST (95% by 2032-2034) despite both having high marginal costs, due to Germany's coal phase-out policy and SWB economics. Total gas demand will continue declining to ~61 BCM by 2035 (-36% from peak), driven by multi-sector displacement.

### Displacement Sequence: Coal Before Gas

Marginal cost hierarchy determines displacement priority:

| Technology | Marginal Cost | Displacement Priority |
|------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Coal | €140/MWh | **Displaced First** (2025-2034) |
| Gas | €164/MWh (2023) | Displaced Second (post-2030) |
| SWB | <€50/MWh | Disruptor (near-zero marginal cost) |

**Coal displaced first** despite both fossil fuels having high costs because:
- Coal phase-out policy target: 2030-2038
- Coal marginal cost consistently above gas
- Gas retains grid flexibility role (peaking plants)
- Nuclear closure (April 2023) temporarily delayed coal displacement

### Why Gas Demand Peaked BEFORE Coal Displacement

**Critical insight**: Power sector represents only 20-25% of Germany's total gas consumption. The 2019 peak occurred due to **non-power sector drivers**:

**Demand Composition:**
- Industrial (40-45%): Chemical feedstock, process heat, manufacturing
- Residential heating (20-25%): Gas boilers for space/water heating
- Power generation (20-25%): Electricity production
- Other (10-15%): Commercial, district heating

**2019-2023 Decline Drivers:**
- High gas prices (2022-23 energy crisis) drove demand destruction
- Industrial efficiency improvements and production cuts
- Early heat pump adoption in residential sector
- Economic slowdown (COVID 2020, energy crisis 2022-23)
- **NOT primarily SWB displacement** (power sector still minimal in 2023)

### LNG Import Infrastructure: Stranded Asset Risk

Germany built 45-50 BCM/yr of emergency LNG import capacity (€6-10 billion investment) in 2023-2024, but faces severe underutilization:

| Year | Total NG Demand | LNG Need | Capacity | Utilization |
|------|----------------|----------|----------|-------------|
| 2023 | 82.4 BCM | 42.4 BCM | 48 BCM/yr | 88% |
| 2024 | 80.3 BCM | 40.6 BCM | 48 BCM/yr | 85% |
| 2027 | 74.4 BCM | 35.5 BCM | 48 BCM/yr | 74% |
| 2030 | 69.0 BCM | 30.9 BCM | 48 BCM/yr | **64%** |
| 2035 | 60.8 BCM | 23.9 BCM | 48 BCM/yr | **50%** |

**Terminal economics require >60% utilization** for cost recovery. Germany crosses below this threshold by 2028-2030, creating €6-10B stranded asset risk. Floating terminals (FSRU) can be relocated; fixed terminals cannot.

### LNG Displacement Priority by Cost

When gas demand declines, highest-cost sources displaced first:

**1. US LNG (Displaced First)**
- Delivered cost: €88-123/MWh (€25-35/MMBtu)
- Cost structure: Liquefaction + transatlantic shipping + regasification
- Germany impact: Spot contracts most vulnerable

**2. Qatar LNG (Displaced Second)**
- Delivered cost: €63-98/MWh (€18-28/MMBtu)
- Cost structure: Lower extraction + long shipping + regasification
- Long-term contracts provide stability

**3. Norwegian Pipeline Gas (Displaced Last)**
- Delivered cost: €42-70/MWh (€12-20/MMBtu)
- Cost structure: Production + short pipeline transport
- **Cheapest source, most resilient to demand decline**

**LNG Exporter Vulnerability:**

**High Risk (Spot-Exposed):**
- Venture Global (Plaquemines LNG)
- NextDecade (Rio Grande LNG)
- New Fortress Energy (FLNG units)

**Lower Risk (Long-Term Contracts):**
- Cheniere Energy (Sabine Pass/Corpus Christi): Take-or-pay through 2040s, but contract renewal risk post-2035
