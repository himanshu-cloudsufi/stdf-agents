Now I see the methodology. The script:

1. **Uses historical data** for years where it's available (2005-2024)
2. **Forecasts future years** using a displacement model that:
   - Calculates "residual" = Total Demand - SWB - Nuclear/Hydro
   - For "coal_first" regions (like Germany), allocates gas first, then coal gets the rest
   - Both decline at 5%/year from their last historical values
   - BUT respects "reserve floors" (10% coal, 15% gas of peak capacity)

The problem is in the **residual calculation**. Look at the output again:

- Total_Demand column shows values like **27,970,814 GWh** (27.9 TWh) for 2025
- But this is clearly wrong - Germany's total electricity demand is ~500-600 TWh, not 27,000 TWh

The script is using the **Non_SWB_Generation** column incorrectly. The "Non_SWB_Generation" value of **26,577,910 GWh (26.6 TWh)** is being treated as a baseline, and then fossil generation is being ADDED to it, when it should be PART of total demand.

This explains why coal "grows" - the script thinks there's massive residual demand (27 TWh total - 0 SWB - 26.6 non-SWB = huge residual), so it allocates this huge residual to coal/gas.

**The actual bug:** The demand forecast is inflated by ~50×, likely a unit conversion error (GWh vs TWh) or the data center/EV demand being added incorrectly.

So to answer your question:
- **Coal forecast methodology**: Coal-first displacement with 5%/year decline to 10% reserve floor
- **The bug**: Total demand is inflated ~50×, creating artificial "residual" that gets allocated to fossil fuels
- **Result**: Coal appears to grow massively when it should be declining to zero by 2038

The methodology itself is sound, but the input data (total demand) is corrupted.
