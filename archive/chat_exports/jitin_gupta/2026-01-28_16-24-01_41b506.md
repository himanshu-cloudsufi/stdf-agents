**Methodology: Task-Level Displacement Model**

The artificial-labor skill decomposes software engineering into 47 distinct tasks (e.g., code generation, debugging, architecture design, code review) across six career stages [C5]. Each task is gated by AI capability thresholds and cost-parity tipping points. When AI cost per task-hour falls below human cost and capability exceeds the reliability threshold (80% success rate for multi-day tasks by 2032 [C4]), displacement accelerates.

Key dynamics:

1. **Productivity vs Replacement Split**: Early-career roles (ages 22-30) experience 60-70% replacement by 2035 as routine coding tasks are fully automated [C6]. Mid-career roles shift toward productivity augmentation but still face 15-25% net displacement as team sizes shrink.

2. **Demand Elasticity Failure**: Software development budgets grow ~3-5% annually, but AI productivity gains deliver 5-10x output per remaining engineer by 2035 [C2]. Demand does not expand fast enough to absorb displaced labor—classic Jevons Paradox applies to *tasks*, not *jobs*.

3. **Capability Gating**: Multi-day autonomous task execution (50% reliability) is achieved by 2030; 80% reliability by 2032 [C4]. This unlocks replacement of mid-career engineering roles, not just junior positions.

- **Bootcamp Collapse**: Coding bootcamp enrollment craters post-2028 as entry-level job openings disappear. University CS programs pivot toward AI oversight and system integration roles.

**Conclusion**

The 20% decline is not speculative—it reflects the deterministic outcome of AI cost curves crossing human labor cost curves while capability exceeds task reliability thresholds [C9]. Software engineering joins a growing list of cognitive professions subject to cost-driven workforce disruption. The transformation is economic, not ideological: when AI delivers equivalent output at 1/10th the cost, employment contracts regardless of policy or sentiment.
