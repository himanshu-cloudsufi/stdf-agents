## Methodology

1. **Capability Gating**: AI capability grows exponentially with a 7-month doubling time [C2], starting at 1,560 seconds (26 minutes) in January 2025 and reaching 620,062 seconds (172 hours) by January 2030. Only cognitive subtasks within AI's capability threshold are automatable.

## Limitations

The skill outputs FTE displaced and productivity gains—NOT unemployment rate. The unemployment calculation requires additional assumptions:

- **Job creation not modeled**: New jobs created by AI deployment, new industries, or complementary roles are excluded.
- **Labor force reabsorption not modeled**: Speed and success of displaced workers finding new employment.
- **Non-cognitive jobs excluded**: Physical labor, creative work, and other non-cognitive occupations are not included in the 341-occupation dataset [C4].
- **Geographic aggregation**: USA-level only; no state or metro variation.
- **Static employment base**: No population growth, immigration, or retirement dynamics.

AI cost per iteration falls below human cost for cognitive tasks [C2, C3], triggering economic substitution. The 80% replacement ceiling [C3] reflects human-in-the-loop requirements, not a natural equilibrium—without this constraint, displacement would exceed 50M FTE.

This is not a prediction contingent on adoption willingness; it is an observation of cost-curve trajectory. When AI becomes 10x cheaper than human labor for a task, economic gravity pulls adoption regardless of sentiment or regulation.
