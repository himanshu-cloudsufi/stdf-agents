U-3 enters 2026 at ~4.1%. Without automation acceleration, Boomer retirements remove 3.5 million workers/year from the labor force through 2030, tapering to 1.5 million/year by 2040. ### Section 3 — Historical Analogues

Five calibration anchors establish the plausible range [C5]:

- **Post-WWII Manufacturing Automation (1945–1960):** U-3 held 3–6% despite massive agricultural/manufacturing displacement. Demand expansion from rising productivity created new consumption categories faster than automation eliminated old jobs.
- **Digital Revolution (1995–2005):** U-3 held 4–6% despite eliminating typists, telephone operators, travel agents, and bank tellers. New categories (web developers, IT support, digital marketers) absorbed displaced workers within 5–8 years per category.
- **2008–09 GFC:** U-3 peaked at 10.0% — the relevant upper-bound calibration for a severe but recoverable shock. This was demand collapse, not technology disruption.
- **2020 COVID Shock:** U-3 peaked at 14.7% in April 2020, recovered to 4.2% by June 2022 (26 months). Demonstrates that even extreme acute shocks resolve rapidly when productive capacity remains intact.
- **UK Industrial Revolution (1760–1840):** The most relevant structural precedent. Real wages for displaced textile workers fell for ~30 years before rising. The transition generation bore the cost; the long-run outcome was unambiguously positive.

**Calibration conclusion:** Technology disruptions historically produce **4–10% peak U-3**, not Great Depression-level outcomes (24.9%), because they expand aggregate demand simultaneously with displacing workers [C6]. The AI disruption is faster than all historical precedents — which is why the High scenario without policy reaches 9.0% peak, at the upper bound of historical technology disruption outcomes.

### Section 6 — Policy Response Modifier Analysis

**Policy mechanisms and their displacement-reduction logic:**

- **Retraining Programs:** Directly attacks structural unemployment (skill mismatch). Reduces structural unemployment fraction by 25%. Highest-impact single intervention.
- **Work-Sharing Mandates:** Converts job losses to hours reduction. Reduces structural unemployment fraction by 20%, boosts reabsorption by 10pp. Empirically validated by Germany/Netherlands during 2008–09, where U-3 peaked 3–4pp lower than countries without it [C10].
- **Universal Basic Income (UBI):** Does not reduce displacement — reduces its harm. Provides income floor enabling better job matching, sustains consumer demand. Reduces structural unemployment fraction by 15%, boosts reabsorption by 5pp.

### Synthesis

The cost-curve dynamics of artificial labor disruption produce a mathematically robust and counterintuitive conclusion: **US U-3 unemployment does not reach catastrophic levels under any modeled scenario, including the High scenario without policy.** The 2040 endpoint (1.3%–4.3%) is *below or near* the 2025 structural baseline of 4.1% across all scenarios [C12].

Three forces operate simultaneously with displacement and prevent catastrophic outcomes:
1. **Demographic contraction** removes 3.5M workers/year from the labor force through 2030
2. **Demand expansion** from AI-driven cost collapse creates new consumption categories and new jobs
3. **S-curve deceleration** means displacement is front-loaded (2026–2031) while job creation is back-loaded (accelerating through 2035–2040)

The critical risk is not the 2040 endpoint — it is the **2028–2033 transition window**, where displacement accelerates faster than new job creation can absorb it. In the High scenario without policy, this window produces a 9.0% U-3 peak in 2031 [C13].

**The policy window is 2026–2028.** Policy deployed before the S-curve inflection cuts the 2031 peak from 9.0% to 5.4% in the High scenario — a difference of ~5–6 million Americans experiencing prolonged unemployment or not. Policy deployed after 2031 reduces the structural tail but cannot prevent the peak.
