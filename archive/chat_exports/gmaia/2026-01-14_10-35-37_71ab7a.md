I'll break down hybrid vehicle architectures into these two philosophical groups you've defined:

## GROUP 1: Essentially ICE Drivetrain + Electric Assist
**Philosophy:** The internal combustion engine is the primary power source; electric components add efficiency/performance.

### **Mild Hybrid (MHEV)**
- **Architecture:** ICE + small starter-generator (typically 48V, 10-20 kW) + small battery (~1 kWh)
- **Electric capability:** Cannot drive on electricity alone; only assists ICE and enables start-stop, regenerative braking, torque fill
- **Examples:** Many modern BMWs, Audis with 48V systems
- **Electric range:** 0 km (zero EV-only driving)

### **Full Hybrid (HEV) - Parallel/Series-Parallel**
- **Architecture:** ICE + electric motor(s) (30-80 kW) + battery (1-2 kWh) with mechanical coupling
- **Electric capability:** Limited EV mode at low speeds (<2 km range), but primarily ICE-dominant
- **Examples:** Toyota Prius (non-plug-in), Honda Accord Hybrid, Ford Fusion Hybrid
- **Electric range:** ~1-2 km at low speeds only

---

## GROUP 2: Essentially BEV Drivetrain + Combustion Help
**Philosophy:** The electric drivetrain is primary; combustion engine extends range or charges battery.

### **Plug-in Hybrid (PHEV)**
- **Architecture:** Large battery (8-20 kWh) + electric motor (60-150 kW primary) + smaller ICE (acts as range extender in many designs)
- **Electric capability:** 40-80 km pure electric range from plug-in charging
- **Examples:** BYD Qin, BMW 530e, Chevrolet Volt (discontinued)
- **Behavioral note:** Can operate as pure EV for daily commutes; ICE activates for long trips

### **Range-Extended EV (REEV/E-REV)**
- **Architecture:** Large battery (15-25 kWh) + powerful electric motor(s) + small ICE generator (not mechanically coupled to wheels in pure form)
- **Electric capability:** 60-120 km pure electric; ICE only generates electricity when battery depletes
- **Examples:** Original Chevrolet Volt (series mode), BMW i3 REx, Mazda MX-30 R-EV
- **Key distinction:** ICE never directly drives wheels (in purest form); it's an onboard generator

### **Electric Dominant PHEV (Extended Range PHEV)**
- **Architecture:** Very large battery (20-40 kWh) + powerful electric motors + ICE that rarely activates
- **Electric capability:** 80-150+ km electric range
- **Examples:** BYD Han DM, Li Auto vehicles (EREV), many Chinese "super PHEVs"
- **Behavioral note:** Most users never use ICE in daily driving; essentially a BEV with combustion insurance

---

## Key Differentiator Between Groups

**Group 1 (ICE-centric):**
- Cannot complete a typical commute on electricity alone
- ICE runs frequently/constantly
- Electric system exists to make ICE more efficient
- Battery: <2 kWh

**Group 2 (BEV-centric):**
- Can complete most daily driving on electricity alone
- ICE can remain off for days/weeks for typical users
- Electric system is the primary drivetrain; ICE is backup/extender
- Battery: >8 kWh, often >15 kWh

---

## The "Chimera" Nature in Disruption Framework

In the Seba disruption lens, **Group 1 hybrids** are transitional efficiency plays within the incumbent ICE paradigm—they don't fundamentally change the cost-per-mile or ownership model.

**Group 2 hybrids (especially PHEVs)** are more disruptive chimeras: they deliver ~80% of BEV cost-per-mile benefits for users with daily charging, while eliminating range anxiety. However, they're still transitional—once BEV range reaches 400-500 km with 15-minute charging, the added complexity and cost of dual drivetrains becomes economically irrational.

The tipping point logic: PHEVs peak when BEV range is insufficient (~2020-2027 window in most markets), then decline exponentially as BEV range/charging infrastructure reaches adequacy.

## How EREV Works: The Battery Recharging Logic

  1. **Direct to motor:** Powers wheels immediately (primary)
