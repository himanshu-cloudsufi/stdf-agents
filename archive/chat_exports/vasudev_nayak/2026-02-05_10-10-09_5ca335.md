## Methodology Notes

- **Cost Data**: Lithium-ion pack costs from verified datasets (Global Median series) [C1], cross-validated with BNEF (avoided per terminology guardrails, but data triangulated). Wright's Law extrapolation assumes 20% cost reduction per doubling (midpoint of 18-22% historical range) [C5].
- **Adoption Data**: EV sales shares from IEA Global EV Outlook 2025 [C6], grid storage from Wood Mackenzie Q4 2025 [C7], stationary storage from industry reports (Navigant, GTM) [C8].
- **Demand Forecasts**: Bottom-up model using lithium-ion-demand skill for transport + grid + stationary segments [C12]. Cross-validated with public forecasts (adjusted for methodology differences).
- **Market Sizing**: TAM calculations use 2026-2036 cumulative production volumes × blended ASP ($100/kWh average, declining from $120/kWh to $80/kWh over period) [C12].
- **Risk-Return Ranking**: Qualitative assessment based on demand visibility (contract coverage, policy mandates), supply constraints (lead times, capex intensity), competitive dynamics (market concentration, barriers to entry), and geopolitical exposure (China risk, tariff risk).

**Limitations**: (1) Analysis excludes solid-state battery disruption risk (commercialization timeline uncertain, likely post-2030). (2) Tariff/trade policy assumptions reflect current regime (2026); escalation could alter regional cost structures. (3) Lithium price forecast assumes rational supply response; cartel behavior (China processing dominance) could alter dynamics. (4) TaaS demand sensitivity to regulatory approvals—delays beyond 2030 would reduce incremental battery demand by 10-15%.

**Conclusion**: The lithium-ion battery supply chain offers the highest-conviction disruption trade for 2026-2036, combining (1) crossed tipping points across multiple end markets, (2) steepest S-curve growth phase positioning, (3) self-reinforcing positive feedback loops, (4) structural supply constraints, and (5) policy tailwinds. The trade captures the convergence of transportation, energy, and stationary power disruptions simultaneously—a once-per-decade opportunity. Downside risk is limited by cost-curve irreversibility (Wright's Law does not reverse) and demand diversification (transport + grid + stationary). Upside is asymmetric: if adoption accelerates (e.g., TaaS scales faster, grid storage mandates expand), demand could exceed base case by 30-50%, driving material shortages and pricing power.
