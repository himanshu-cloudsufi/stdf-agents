The collapse is driven by lithium-ion displacement of lead-acid batteries across transport (2-wheelers, 3-wheelers, cars, commercial vehicles) and stationary applications (industrial motive power, telecom/UPS), compounded by rising recycling efficiency that reduces primary lead requirements [C2].

**Methodology & Scope**

Analysis uses bottom-up segmentation of China lead demand across six transport categories (new vehicle sales + replacement batteries) and three industrial/stationary categories, plus non-battery uses [C3]. Ideal-case assumptions reflect maximum economically-driven disruption: EV adoption follows cost-parity S-curves with 95%+ penetration by 2035 in passenger cars and 80%+ in commercial segments; recycling rates rise from ~45% (2024) to 70% (2036) as circular infrastructure scales; industrial stationary demand (forklifts, telecom, UPS) shifts to lithium-ion at 5–8% annual displacement rates post-2028 [C4].

**Drivers of Collapse**

1. **Transport electrification**: China BEV/PHEV sales exceed 50% share in 2024; cost parity achieved across all segments by 2027. Replacement battery demand for ICE vehicles declines as fleet turns over (12–15 year average life). 2-wheeler and 3-wheeler segments electrify fastest (90%+ by 2030) due to lower cost barriers [C6].

2. **Stationary displacement**: Lithium-ion cost falls below $100/kWh by 2025 (China pack prices ~$84/kWh in 2024), enabling economic substitution in telecom towers, datacenters, and forklifts. Lead-acid retains share only in low-cycle, price-sensitive niches (automotive SLI batteries, rural backup power) [C7].

3. **Recycling efficiency**: Circular supply rises from 45% to 70% as policy mandates (Extended Producer Responsibility) and scrap economics improve. Higher recycling rates reduce primary mine/import demand proportionally [C8].

**Sensitivity**: Peak timing varies ±2 years with adoption rate assumptions; 2036 demand floor varies ±15% with recycling efficiency (65–75% range). Non-battery demand (construction, ammunition, radiation shielding) grows modestly but cannot offset transport/stationary collapse [C9].

**Conclusion**: Lead demand in China is in terminal decline, driven by cost-curve economics of lithium-ion displacement. The 60–75% reduction by 2036 reflects systemic replacement, not incremental substitution. Primary lead mining and smelting capacity will face stranded asset risk as utilization falls below breakeven thresholds (typically 60–70% capacity utilization). Investment in new lead capacity is economically irrational under these dynamics [C10].
