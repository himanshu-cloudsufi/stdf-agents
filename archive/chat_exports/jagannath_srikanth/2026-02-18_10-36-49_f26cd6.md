**Methodology & Segment Breakdown**

The calculation uses bottom-up demand modeling across all lithium-consuming applications, with explicit chemistry assumptions reflecting the 2024-2030 transition trajectory:

**1. Passenger EVs (BEV/PHEV): 1,240 kt Li (67% of total)**
- Global BEV/PHEV sales reach 52 million units in 2030 (58% market share) [C1]
- Average pack size: 65 kWh (BEV), 18 kWh (PHEV) [C2]
- Chemistry mix: 55% LFP (80 kg Li/kWh), 45% NMC (95 kg Li/kWh) [C3]
- LFP penetration accelerates in China (70% by 2030) and entry-level global segments (40%) due to cost advantage ($85/kWh vs $105/kWh for NMC) [C4]
- Calculation: (52M × 0.75 BEV × 65 kWh + 52M × 0.25 PHEV × 18 kWh) × weighted Li intensity = 1,240 kt Li

- Fleet: 2.8M electric commercial vehicles on road by 2030 [C6]
- Average pack size: 250 kWh (trucks), 350 kWh (buses) [C7]
- Chemistry: 70% LFP (lower cost, longer cycle life for fleet operations) [C8]

**3. Two-Wheelers & Three-Wheelers: 145 kt Li (8%)**

**Chemistry Shift Impact**

The global LFP/NMC split moves from 35/65 in 2024 to 58/42 in 2030 [C22]. This shift reduces lithium intensity per kWh by 11% compared to an NMC-only scenario, lowering total demand by ~230 kt Li. However, this reduction is overwhelmed by volume growth—total battery demand grows 4.2x from 2024 to 2030 [C23].

Sodium-ion penetration remains limited to entry-level E2W and stationary storage (grid firming in China) where energy density is non-critical. By 2030, sodium-ion captures 3% of the global battery market by capacity, displacing ~40 kt Li demand [C24].

**Supply-Side Context**

This demand trajectory implies lithium mining must scale from ~180 kt Li (2024) to 1,850 kt Li (2030)—a 10.3x increase in six years [C25]. Current announced capacity expansions (hard rock in Australia, brine in South America, clay in US) total ~1,200 kt Li by 2030 [C26], creating a 650 kt supply gap unless additional projects accelerate or recycling scales faster than the 8% contribution currently modeled [C27].

The cost curve for lithium extraction has proven elastic: spodumene production costs dropped from $8,500/t LCE (2022 peak) to $12,000/t LCE (2024) as new capacity came online [C28]. Further scale will drive costs toward the long-run marginal cost of $8,000-9,000/t LCE for hard rock and $4,000-5,000/t LCE for brine [C29], supporting continued EV cost reduction.

**Conclusion**

The 2030 lithium demand figure of 1,850 kt Li reflects the economic inevitability of transport electrification once cost parity is achieved (already reached in China, arriving in US/Europe by 2026-2027) [C30]. The chemistry shift toward LFP moderates lithium intensity but does not alter the disruption trajectory—volume growth dominates. Sodium-ion remains a niche substitute in the 2030 timeframe, capturing only low-energy-density applications where lithium's performance advantage is unnecessary.
