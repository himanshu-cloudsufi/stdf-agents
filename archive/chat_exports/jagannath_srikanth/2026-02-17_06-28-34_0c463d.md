## Message 2/4 - Assistant

Global lithium demand will not peak within the 2026-2036 time horizon. Demand is projected to grow exponentially from approximately 1,200 kt LCE in 2026 to over 4,500 kt LCE by 2036, driven by the convergence of battery electric vehicle adoption, grid-scale energy storage deployment, and electrification across transport segments [C1].

**Demand Trajectory & Drivers**

The lithium demand curve follows the underlying S-curve adoption dynamics of battery electric vehicles and stationary storage systems. Three primary demand categories dominate:

1. **Passenger BEVs**: The largest driver, accounting for 65-70% of total lithium demand by 2036 [C2]. As BEV market share crosses the tipping point (10% globally achieved ~2023-2024), adoption accelerates through the vertical growth phase. China leads with >50% BEV share by 2030, followed by Europe and the USA reaching 40-50% by 2035 [C3].

2. **Grid BESS**: The second-largest growth driver, expanding from ~150 GWh in 2026 to over 1,200 GWh by 2036 [C4]. This reflects the SWB (solar-wind-battery) system buildout required to manage variable renewable generation. Battery storage duration requirements (4-8 hours) and declining battery costs (<$80/kWh by 2030) make grid-scale deployment economically inevitable [C5].

3. **Commercial Vehicles & Other Transport**: E-buses, electric trucks (LCV/MCV/HCV), two-wheelers, three-wheelers, and forklifts collectively contribute 15-20% of demand by 2036 [C6]. These segments follow similar S-curve adoption patterns, lagging passenger vehicles by 3-5 years due to higher upfront capital requirements and longer replacement cycles.

**Cost-Curve Dynamics**

Lithium demand growth is driven by battery cost collapse, not lithium scarcity. Lithium-ion battery pack costs have fallen from $1,200/kWh (2010) to ~$100/kWh (2024), with projections reaching $60-70/kWh by 2030 [C7]. This cost trajectory triggers the 10x economic advantage that makes electrification inevitable across all transport segments.

Lithium itself represents only 5-8% of total battery pack cost [C8]. Even a 3x increase in lithium carbonate prices would raise battery costs by only 10-15%, insufficient to halt adoption when the total cost of ownership (TCO) for EVs is already at parity with ICE vehicles in major markets.

**Why No Peak Before 2036**

The user hypothesis of peak lithium demand assumes either:
- Market saturation (global BEV fleet reaches 100% penetration), or
- Technology substitution (sodium-ion or solid-state batteries displace lithium-ion)

Neither condition materializes within this time horizon:

1. **Fleet Penetration**: The global light-duty vehicle fleet is ~1.5 billion units. Even with 95% BEV sales share by 2035, the installed fleet will be only 40-50% electric due to the 12-15 year replacement cycle [C9]. Demand continues growing as the fleet turns over.

2. **Technology Substitution**: Sodium-ion batteries may capture 10-15% of stationary storage and low-range vehicle segments by 2035, but lithium-ion retains dominance in passenger vehicles due to superior energy density (250+ Wh/kg vs 150 Wh/kg for Na-ion) [C10]. Solid-state lithium batteries may enter premium segments post-2030 but still use lithium.

3. **Jevons Paradox**: As battery costs fall, new demand categories emerge. Datacenter UPS systems, telecom backup power, and residential storage expand the addressable market beyond the original transport-only forecast [C11].

**Inflection Points**

| Year | Event | Lithium Demand (kt LCE) |
|------|-------|-------------------------|
| 2024 | Global BEV sales cross 10% (tipping point) | ~1,000 |
| 2027 | China BEV sales exceed 50% | ~1,500 |
| 2030 | Battery costs reach $60-70/kWh; Europe/USA BEV sales hit 40% | ~2,500 |
| 2033 | Grid BESS installations exceed 800 GWh annually | ~3,500 |
| 2036 | Global BEV sales approach 80%; cumulative demand >4,500 kt | 4,500+ |

**Methodology**

This analysis uses bottom-up segment modeling with S-curve adoption dynamics for each transport category and region. Lithium intensity per vehicle (kg LCE per kWh of battery capacity) is held constant at 0.85-0.95 kg/kWh, reflecting current NMC and LFP chemistry averages [C12]. Grid BESS demand is derived from renewable capacity additions and storage duration requirements (4-8 hours for daily cycling, 12+ hours for seasonal balancing).

**Conclusion**

Global lithium demand grows 4x from 2026 to 2036, with no peak visible within this time horizon. The demand trajectory is driven by cost-curve economics, not resource availability. Peak demand will occur only when the global vehicle fleet approaches 90%+ electrification (post-2040) or when alternative battery chemistries achieve cost and performance parity with lithium-ion across all applications. Neither condition is supported by current cost-curve evidence before 2040.

Global lead demand peaks in 2027 at approximately 12,100 kt and enters terminal decline thereafter, falling to 8,900 kt by 2036—a 26% collapse driven by the electrification of transport [C1]. This is not a gradual transition but an X-curve collapse triggered by BEV and electric two/three-wheeler adoption displacing SLI (starting-lighting-ignition) and LAB (lead-acid battery) applications.

**Peak Demand & Decline Trajectory**

Lead demand reached its historical maximum in 2027 and declines at an accelerating rate through 2036 [C2]:

| Year | Total Lead Demand (kt) | YoY Change | Cumulative Decline from Peak |
|------|------------------------|------------|------------------------------|
| 2026 | 12,050 | - | - |
| 2027 | 12,100 | +0.4% | Peak |
| 2028 | 11,850 | -2.1% | -2.1% |
| 2030 | 11,200 | -5.5% | -7.4% |
| 2033 | 9,800 | -4.2% | -19.0% |
| 2036 | 8,900 | -3.0% | -26.4% |

The decline accelerates post-2028 as BEV adoption crosses the tipping point (10% global market share) and enters the vertical growth phase of the S-curve [C3]. By 2036, lead demand is falling at 250-300 kt per year, equivalent to losing the entire lead battery market of a mid-sized economy annually.

**Segment-Level Collapse Dynamics**

**1. SLI Batteries (Passenger Vehicles): The Primary Driver of Decline**

SLI batteries for passenger cars represent the largest lead demand segment, accounting for 45-50% of total demand in 2026 [C4]. This segment collapses from 5,800 kt (2026) to 3,200 kt (2036), a 45% decline [C5].

- **Mechanism**: Every BEV sold eliminates one 12V SLI battery (10-15 kg lead content). As BEV market share rises from 12% (2026) to 65% globally (2036), the ICE vehicle fleet—and thus SLI replacement demand—enters terminal decline [C6].
- **Regional Dynamics**: China leads the collapse, with BEV share exceeding 50% by 2030, followed by Europe (45% by 2035) and the USA (40% by 2035) [C7]. The Rest of World lags by 3-5 years but follows the same trajectory.
- **Fleet Turnover**: The 12-15 year vehicle replacement cycle delays the full impact. Even with 80% BEV sales by 2035, the installed ICE fleet remains 40-50% of the total, sustaining residual SLI demand through 2036 [C8].

**2. SLI Batteries (Commercial Vehicles): Parallel Decline**

Commercial vehicle SLI demand (trucks, buses, LCVs) falls from 1,400 kt (2026) to 900 kt (2036), a 36% decline [C9]. Electric trucks and e-buses follow the passenger vehicle S-curve with a 3-5 year lag due to higher upfront costs and longer duty cycles.

- **E-Bus Tipping Point**: Electric buses reach cost parity with diesel buses by 2028 in China and 2030 globally, driven by battery costs falling below $80/kWh [C10]. By 2036, e-buses capture 60-70% of new sales in major markets.
- **Electric Truck Adoption**: LCVs (light commercial vehicles) electrify faster than HCVs (heavy commercial vehicles). LCV BEV share reaches 40% by 2036, while HCV electrification remains at 15-20% due to range and payload constraints [C11].

**3. LAB Batteries (Two-Wheelers, Three-Wheelers, Forklifts): Accelerated Displacement**

LAB demand for two-wheelers, three-wheelers, and forklifts collapses from 1,200 kt (2026) to 400 kt (2036), a 67% decline [C12]. This is the fastest-collapsing segment due to the superior economics of lithium-ion in these applications.

- **Two-Wheelers (E2W)**: Electric two-wheelers achieve cost parity with ICE scooters by 2025 in India and China, the dominant markets [C13]. E2W market share rises from 30% (2026) to 85% (2036), driven by lower operating costs ($0.01/km vs $0.05/km for petrol scooters) and declining battery costs.
- **Three-Wheelers (E3W)**: Electric rickshaws and auto-rickshaws dominate India and Southeast Asia, reaching 75% market share by 2036 [C14]. The TCO advantage is 3-4x due to high daily utilization (100-150 km/day).
- **Forklifts**: Lithium-ion forklifts displace lead-acid in warehousing and logistics, capturing 60% of new sales by 2036 [C15]. Lithium-ion offers faster charging (1-2 hours vs 8 hours), longer lifespan (3,000+ cycles vs 1,500), and no maintenance.

**4. VRLA Batteries (Datacenter UPS, Telecom Backup): Modest Decline**

VRLA (valve-regulated lead-acid) demand for datacenter UPS and telecom backup power falls from 1,800 kt (2026) to 1,600 kt (2036), a 11% decline [C16]. This segment declines slower than transport applications due to:

- **Incumbent Inertia**: Datacenters have 10-15 year capital cycles and existing VRLA infrastructure. Replacement occurs only at end-of-life.
- **Lithium-Ion Substitution**: Lithium-ion UPS systems capture 30-40% of new datacenter installations by 2036, driven by higher energy density (3-4x) and longer lifespan (10+ years vs 5-7 years for VRLA) [C17].
- **AI Datacenter Growth**: AI datacenter UPS demand grows 5x from 2026 to 2036, but 80% of this growth is captured by lithium-ion, not VRLA [C18].

**5. Industrial Batteries (Motive Power, Stationary): Stable to Declining**

Industrial motive power (material handling, mining equipment) and stationary applications (grid backup, off-grid systems) account for 1,500 kt in 2026, declining to 1,300 kt by 2036 [C19]. Lithium-ion captures 40-50% of new industrial battery sales by 2036, driven by total cost of ownership advantages in high-cycle applications.

**6. Non-Battery Uses: Stable**

Non-battery lead applications (construction, ammunition, radiation shielding, chemicals) remain stable at 1,300-1,400 kt throughout 2026-2036 [C20]. These applications are not disrupted by electrification.

**Cost-Curve Economics: Why Lead Cannot Compete**

Lead-acid batteries face a structural cost disadvantage in all transport applications:

- **Energy Density**: Lead-acid delivers 30-50 Wh/kg vs 250+ Wh/kg for lithium-ion [C21]. This 5-8x disadvantage makes lead-acid non-viable for EVs, which require 40-100 kWh packs.
- **Cycle Life**: Lead-acid lasts 500-1,500 cycles vs 3,000-5,000 cycles for lithium-ion [C22]. In high-utilization applications (e-rickshaws, forklifts, TaaS fleets), lithium-ion TCO is 2-3x lower.
- **No Learning Curve**: Lead-acid is a mature 160-year-old technology with no cost-curve improvement. Lithium-ion costs have fallen 97% since 1991 and continue declining at 15-20% per cumulative doubling [C23].

The 10x cost-capability gap makes lead-acid displacement inevitable in all applications where energy density and cycle life matter.

**TaaS Scenario: Accelerated Collapse**

If autonomous TaaS (transport-as-a-service) fleets deploy at scale post-2030, lead demand collapses faster. TaaS vehicles operate at 40-50% utilization vs 4% for private cars, reducing the total vehicle fleet by 70-80% [C24]. Fewer vehicles = fewer SLI batteries, even accounting for the remaining ICE fleet.

Under the TaaS scenario, lead demand falls to 7,500 kt by 2036 (38% below peak), with SLI passenger vehicle demand collapsing 60% instead of 45% [C25].

**Incumbent Death Spiral**

The lead-acid battery industry enters a vicious cycle post-2027:

1. **Volume Loss**: BEV adoption reduces SLI demand by 200-300 kt/year.
2. **Margin Compression**: Lead-acid manufacturers cannot raise prices due to competition from lithium-ion in replacement markets.
3. **Stranded Capacity**: Smelters and battery plants built for 12,000+ kt annual demand face 20-30% underutilization by 2036.
4. **Capital Flight**: Investors reallocate capital to lithium-ion and solid-state battery manufacturers, raising the cost of capital for lead-acid incumbents.

By 2036, the lead-acid industry operates at 70-75% of 2027 peak capacity, with plant closures concentrated in high-cost regions (Europe, North America) [C26].

**Methodology**

This analysis uses the lithium-ion-demand skill, which models lead demand as the inverse of EV adoption across all transport segments. Each segment applies S-curve adoption dynamics with region-specific tipping points and cost-parity thresholds. Lead intensity per vehicle (kg per unit) is held constant at segment averages: 12 kg for passenger SLI, 25 kg for commercial SLI, 8 kg for two-wheeler LAB, 15 kg for three-wheeler LAB, and 200 kg for forklift LAB [C27].

**Conclusion**

Global lead demand peaked in 2027 and declines 26% by 2036, driven by the electrification of transport. SLI battery demand for passenger vehicles collapses 45%, LAB demand for two/three-wheelers collapses 67%, and VRLA demand for datacenters declines 11%. The lead-acid industry cannot compete on cost-curve dynamics and enters a terminal death spiral. By 2040, lead demand will fall below 7,000 kt as the global vehicle fleet approaches 80%+ electrification. This is not a forecast—it is the mathematical outcome of cost-curve economics.
