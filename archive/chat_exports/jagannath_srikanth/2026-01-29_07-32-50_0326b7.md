The USA unemployment rate will reach **7.4% in December 2026** [C1]. This is a structural shift driven by AI-driven job displacement, not a recession.

**AI Displacement Drives the Forecast**

By December 2026, AI will displace 10.8 million full-time equivalent workers—11.6% of the 93.1 million covered employment base [C2]. This displacement reflects cost-curve dynamics: AI capability doubles every 7 months [C5], while AI costs decline 70% annually [C6]. When AI cost drops below 50% of human labor cost for a given task, adoption accelerates rapidly.

The forecast uses bottom-up task-level simulation across 186 occupations and 48,404 subtasks [C7]. Only cognitive tasks are automatable; physical tasks are excluded. Displacement is gated by AI capability (can the AI complete the task?) and cost-parity tipping points (is AI cheaper than humans?).

**Confidence Intervals Reflect Labor Market Absorption Uncertainty**

The 7.4% central estimate assumes 50% of displaced workers are redeployed to new roles within the forecast period, while 50% remain unemployed. The range spans:

- **Lower bound (6.1%)**: 70% of displaced workers redeployed [C3]
- **Central estimate (7.4%)**: 50% redeployed [C1]
- **Upper bound (10.7%)**: 0% redeployed (maximum impact) [C4]

Baseline unemployment is 4.2% [C8], typical for 2024-2025. The labor force is 167.5 million workers [C9].

**Key Assumptions**

- AI capability: 7-month doubling time (METR empirical data) [C5]
- AI cost decline: 70% annually [C6]
- Tipping point: AI cost < 50% of human cost
- Replacement ceiling: 80% (human-in-the-loop minimum)
- Replacement/productivity split: 50% displacement, 50% productivity gain
- Baseline unemployment: 4.2% [C8]
- Labor force: 167.5M workers [C9]
- Coverage: 93.1M workers (~60% of USA workforce) [C7]
- Absorption rate: 50% central estimate (30-70% range)

**Interpretation**

This is **not a recession forecast**. It is a structural labor market shift driven by exponential AI improvement. The 7.4% unemployment rate reflects displaced workers not yet redeployed, even in a growing economy. The range (6.1%-10.7%) captures uncertainty in how quickly displaced workers find new employment or exit the labor force.

Cost-curve dynamics create economic gravity pulling adoption toward rapid displacement. Organizations that adopt AI gain decisive cost advantages; those that delay face margin compression and talent loss. This is disruption, not downturn.
