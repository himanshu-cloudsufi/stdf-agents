Russia faces economic rupture between 2028 and 2032 as global oil demand collapses under the combined weight of transport electrification and SWB energy disruption. The mechanism is straightforward: Russia's fiscal model depends on oil prices above $60-70/bbl to balance its budget, but global oil demand destruction will drive prices toward the marginal cost of production ($25-35/bbl) by 2030-2032, creating a fiscal chasm of $100-150 billion annually.

## Russia's Structural Vulnerability

Russia's economy exhibits extreme dependence on hydrocarbon exports:

- **Export composition**: Oil and gas constitute 60-65% of total export revenues (2023-2025 baseline)
- **Fiscal dependency**: Hydrocarbon revenues fund 40-45% of federal budget expenditures
- **GDP exposure**: Petroleum sector contributes 20-25% of GDP directly, with cascading effects across construction, manufacturing, and services
- **Fiscal breakeven**: Russia requires Brent crude at $60-70/bbl to balance its federal budget under current expenditure levels (defense, social programs, debt service)

## Oil Demand Collapse Trajectory

Global oil demand faces a double disruption from transport electrification and power generation displacement:

**Transport sector (70% of oil demand)**:
- China EV penetration reached 52% in 2024, accelerating toward 80-90% by 2030. Chinese oil consumption peaked in 2023 at 14.5 million barrels/day and will decline to 8-10 million barrels/day by 2030 as the fleet turns over.
- Europe and USA follow 3-5 years behind China's trajectory. Combined OECD oil demand falls from 47 million barrels/day (2023) to 30-35 million barrels/day by 2030.
- Global oil demand: 100 million barrels/day (2023) → 75-80 million barrels/day (2030) → 50-60 million barrels/day (2035).

**Power generation displacement**:
- SWB systems displace oil-fired generation in emerging markets (Middle East, Southeast Asia, Africa). Residual oil generation collapses from 4 million barrels/day (2023) to <1 million barrels/day by 2030 as solar + battery systems achieve $30-40/MWh all-in costs vs $80-120/MWh for oil peaker plants.

## Rupture Point Timing: 2028-2032

The oil market rupture follows a predictable sequence:

**Phase 1 (2026-2028): Demand erosion begins**
- Global demand falls 3-5 million barrels/day annually
- Brent crude oscillates between $50-70/bbl as OPEC+ attempts production cuts
- Russia maintains revenues through volume (8-10 million barrels/day exports) but margins compress

**Phase 2 (2028-2030): Market trauma**
- Demand destruction accelerates to 8-10 million barrels/day annually as EV adoption hits 40-50% globally
- Brent crude crashes toward $35-45/bbl as high-cost producers (US shale, Canadian tar sands, offshore) shut in capacity
- Russia's fiscal deficit explodes: At $40/bbl, Russia loses $80-100 billion in annual revenue vs $70/bbl baseline

**Phase 3 (2030-2032): Rupture and stranded assets**
- Oil prices settle at marginal cost of production: $25-35/bbl (Saudi/UAE/Iraq cost floor)
- Russia cannot compete: Urals crude production costs $20-30/bbl, but transport discounts (sanctions, logistics) push netbacks to $15-25/bbl
- Fiscal crisis: Russia faces $120-150 billion annual deficits (12-15% of GDP) with oil at $30/bbl
- Stranded assets: $1.5-2 trillion in Russian hydrocarbon reserves (Arctic, Eastern Siberia, offshore) become economically unviable

## Economic Vulnerability Indicators

**Fiscal stress markers**:
- Debt-to-GDP ratio: Currently 20%, but will spike to 60-80% by 2032 as deficits accumulate
- Foreign exchange reserves: $600 billion (2025) depletes to $200-300 billion by 2030 defending the ruble and funding deficits
- Currency collapse: Ruble depreciates 40-60% (120-150 rubles/USD by 2030) as capital flight accelerates

**Stranded asset exposure**:
- Proven reserves: 80 billion barrels oil, 48 trillion cubic meters gas
- Stranded value at $30/bbl: $2.4 trillion in oil reserves become uneconomic (Arctic, tight oil, offshore)
- Infrastructure write-offs: $400-600 billion in pipelines, refineries, export terminals built for 10-11 million barrels/day export capacity become excess

**Social and political instability thresholds**:
- Unemployment: Oil/gas sector employs 1.5-2 million directly, 8-10 million indirectly. Sector collapse triggers 15-20% unemployment in resource regions (Siberia, Urals, Far East)
- Pension crisis: Fiscal deficits force cuts to social spending; pension system (40 million recipients) faces 30-40% real-terms cuts
- Regional fragmentation risk: Resource-producing regions (Tatarstan, Sakha, Khanty-Mansi) lose fiscal transfers from Moscow, increasing separatist pressures

## Natural Gas: Parallel Collapse

Russia's natural gas exports face simultaneous disruption:

- **Europe**: Russian gas exports to Europe collapsed from 150 bcm/year (2021) to 40-50 bcm/year (2024) post-sanctions. SWB + heat pumps eliminate residual demand by 2028-2030.
- **China**: Pipeline capacity (Power of Siberia) delivers 38 bcm/year, but China's gas demand growth slows as SWB displaces gas peakers. Chinese gas imports peak 2026-2028, then decline.
- **LNG competition**: Qatar, USA, Australia expand LNG capacity 200+ bcm/year by 2030, undercutting Russian pipeline economics.
- **Price collapse**: European gas prices fall from €30-40/MWh (2024-2025) to €15-20/MWh (2030) as SWB overbuild creates power surplus, destroying gas peaker economics.

Russia's gas export revenues: $50-60 billion/year (2024) → $20-30 billion/year (2030) → $10-15 billion/year (2035).

## Conclusion: Rupture Window 2028-2032

Russia's economic model ruptures between 2028 and 2032 when oil prices fall below $40/bbl and remain there permanently. The mechanism is the death spiral of hydrocarbon-dependent economies:

1. **Demand destruction** (transport electrification + SWB) removes 20-25 million barrels/day from global oil markets by 2030
2. **Price collapse** to marginal cost ($25-35/bbl) as high-cost producers exit
3. **Fiscal crisis** as Russia's $60-70/bbl breakeven becomes untenable; deficits reach $120-150 billion/year
4. **Stranded assets** of $1.5-2 trillion as Arctic and offshore reserves become uneconomic
5. **Currency and debt crisis** as reserves deplete and capital flees
6. **Social instability** as unemployment spikes and pensions collapse

The rupture is not a prediction—it is the mathematical consequence of cost-curve dynamics. Russia's economy is a leveraged bet on $60+ oil. When that bet fails, the system has no alternative revenue base of sufficient scale. The timing is determined by the pace of EV adoption in China (already at 52%) and the SWB cost curve (solar + battery now cheaper than gas peakers in most markets). Both curves are past their tipping points. The boulder is already rolling downhill.

## Methodology Note

Analysis excludes regulatory lag, geopolitical intervention (sanctions relief, OPEC+ coordination), and assumes Russia does not successfully diversify its economy (historical evidence: 25 years of failed diversification attempts). Oil demand trajectories derived from transport electrification S-curves (China 2024 data: 52% EV penetration) and SWB displacement of fossil generation (merit order economics). Fiscal breakeven prices from IMF and Russian Ministry of Finance data (2023-2024). Stranded asset calculations use $30/bbl long-term price assumption (Saudi cost floor) vs Russian all-in costs including transport discounts.
