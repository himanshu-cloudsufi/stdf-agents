# Artificial Labor Disruption Analysis: Economy-Wide Employment Impact

## Analysis Approach

Using Tony Seba's disruption framework applied to labor markets: artificial labor (AI + Robotics + Agentic AI) represents a disruptive technology following exponential cost decline curves (Wright's Law) while human labor costs remain static or rise linearly. This creates classic cost parity → tipping point → S-curve adoption dynamics.

The analysis treats **labor-as-a-service** (cost per task or cost per hour) rather than just technology adoption. Key disruption drivers:
- **AI capabilities** exponentially improving across 68+ benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, etc.)
- **Cost per task** declining 10× every 2-3 years via GPU improvements + algorithmic efficiency
- **Agentic AI systems** orchestrating multi-step workflows, displacing knowledge workers
- **Industrial robots** reaching $10-20k TCO vs $40-80k annual human labor costs
- **Convergence effect**: AI + Robotics + Sensors creating capabilities exceeding human performance

## Methodology Overview

**Data Sources:**
- AI benchmark performance trajectories (68+ benchmarks tracking capability improvement)

- Account for: GPU costs, inference costs, robot TCO, maintenance, human wages + benefits

**Adoption Modeling:**
- Post-tipping: logistic S-curve adoption with asset lifetime adjustments
- Faster adoption for: low-CAPEX software roles (customer service, admin) vs high-CAPEX physical roles (manufacturing)
- Regional friction: USA faster (market-driven, flexible labor) vs Europe slower (policy constraints, labor protections)

**Investment implication:** NVIDIA's dominance doesn't hinge on China access. Even with zero China revenue, US/Europe/rest-of-world AI infrastructure demand through 2027 is sufficient to justify current valuation. China access is "nice to have" not "make or break."
