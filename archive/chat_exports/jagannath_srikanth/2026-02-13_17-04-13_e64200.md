Global lead supply will exceed demand by more than 10% starting in **2029**.

## Economic Dynamics

Lead demand is driven by three segments: automotive starting-lighting-ignition (SLI) batteries (62.5% of 2024 demand), industrial batteries (23.7%), and non-battery uses (13.8%). Total global demand reached 12,259 kt in 2024, growing at a 2.38% CAGR from 2010.

The automotive segment faces structural collapse. Electric vehicles eliminate the need for lead-acid SLI batteries—a BEV requires no 12V starter battery for an internal combustion engine that no longer exists. As EV adoption accelerates through the 2020s, automotive lead demand will decline at approximately -3% per year.

Industrial battery demand has plateaued. Lithium-ion displacement in forklifts, UPS systems, and stationary storage applications has stalled growth. The 2019-2024 trajectory shows only 4.6% total growth over five years—effectively flat.

## Supply Structure

Global lead supply comprises two components: mine production (44% of supply) and recycled lead (56%). In 2024, mine production reached approximately 5,400 kt, with China producing 2.4 million metric tons, Australia 480,000 MT, and the US 250,000 MT. Mine production is projected to grow modestly at +1% per year through 2036.

Recycled lead supply is the critical variable. Lead-acid batteries achieve >99% recycling rates in developed markets. Recycled supply tracks demand with a 4-year lag—the typical automotive battery lifetime. As demand falls, scrap availability falls with it, creating a lagged supply response.

## The Surplus Trajectory

The supply-demand imbalance emerges from asymmetric dynamics:

1. **Demand declines** as EVs displace ICE vehicles (-3%/yr automotive segment)
2. **Mine production grows** modestly (+1%/yr capacity additions)
3. **Recycled supply lags** demand by 4 years, creating temporary oversupply

**2025-2028**: Supply grows faster than demand as recycled lead reflects higher 2021-2024 demand levels. Surplus reaches 9.7% by 2028.

**2029**: The 10% threshold is crossed. Demand falls to 11,173 kt while supply reaches 12,410 kt—a surplus of 1,237 kt (11.1%).

**2030-2036**: The surplus accelerates as automotive demand collapse intensifies. By 2036, surplus reaches 21.3% (2,111 kt excess supply against 9,909 kt demand).

## Sensitivity Analysis

If mine production grows faster than the baseline +1%/yr assumption:

- **+2%/yr mine growth**: 10% surplus reached in 2028 (one year earlier)
- **+3%/yr mine growth**: 10% surplus reached in 2028
- **+4-5%/yr mine growth**: 10% surplus reached in 2027 (two years earlier)

The 2029 baseline assumes conservative mine production growth. Faster capacity additions would accelerate the surplus timeline, but the fundamental driver—automotive demand collapse—remains unchanged.

## Market Implications

The lead market faces a structural oversupply crisis driven by technology disruption in its largest end-use segment. Automotive SLI batteries represent 62.5% of demand, and this segment is being eliminated by the EV transition. Industrial battery growth cannot offset automotive decline.

Mine economics will deteriorate as prices compress under surplus conditions. High-cost producers (US shale-equivalent lead mines) will face margin compression and potential closure. Recycling infrastructure will face volume decline as scrap availability falls with demand.

The 2029 inflection point marks the beginning of a sustained surplus regime lasting through at least 2036. Lead prices will trend toward the marginal cost of the lowest-cost producers, stranding high-cost capacity.

## Methodology

Analysis uses bottom-up demand modeling from verified datasets (2010-2024 historical demand by segment) and web-sourced supply data (mine production, recycling rates, capacity projections). Automotive demand decline assumes -3%/yr based on EV adoption S-curve dynamics. Industrial and non-battery segments held flat reflecting observed 2019-2024 stagnation. Recycled supply modeled with 4-year lag based on industry-standard battery lifetime. Mine production growth assumes +1%/yr reflecting GlobalData and USGS capacity forecasts.
