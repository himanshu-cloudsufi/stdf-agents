## The Collapse Mechanism

Global lead demand stood at 12,259 kt in 2024 [C1], with automotive starting-lighting-ignition (SLI) batteries consuming 62.6% of total demand [C2]. This concentration creates a structural vulnerability: electric vehicles require no lead-acid batteries. As EV adoption accelerates from 20% global market share in 2024 to 63% by 2030 and 89% by 2036 [C5], automotive lead demand collapses proportionally.

The disruption extends beyond passenger vehicles. Industrial batteries (forklifts, stationary storage) face lithium-ion displacement at 12% annual decline rates post-2025 [C6]. Combined with stable-to-declining non-battery uses, total global lead demand falls to 7,269 kt by 2030 (-40.7%) and 4,866 kt by 2036 (-60.3%) [C6].

## Supply Inertia Creates Massive Surplus

Lead supply cannot adjust at the speed of demand collapse. Global mine production reached approximately 4.7-4.8 million tonnes in 2024 [C3], with secondary production from recycling (which exceeds 95% recovery rates [C4]) adding another ~7 million tonnes of refined lead. This creates total supply of ~12 million tonnes, matching 2024 demand.

Supply adjusts with a 4-year lag due to mine closure economics, smelter fixed costs, and recycling infrastructure inertia [C7]. Even with gradual adjustment, supply declines only to 10,916 kt by 2030 and 8,248 kt by 2036 [C7]—far slower than demand collapse.
