## AI Datacenter Optical Interconnect: Value Chain Analysis (2026–2036)

**Scope:** Global (US/China/Taiwan primary) | 2026–2036 | Analysis reflects cost-curve and architecture dynamics; regulatory/adoption lags excluded except where geopolitically material.

---

### Value Chain Architecture: Two Competing Stacks

The AI datacenter optical interconnect market is undergoing a structural bifurcation between two architectures:

- **Pluggable Transceiver Stack (incumbent):** Discrete optical modules connecting switch ASICs to fiber via electrical SerDes interfaces. Dominant 2026–2030.
- **CPO Stack (disruptor):** Optics co-packaged directly with switch ASICs, eliminating the SerDes interface. Scaling 2027–2031, dominant by 2033–2036.

The disruption arc is not a gradual substitution — it is an architectural phase change. When CPO reaches 10% of new AI switch port deployments (estimated 2029–2031 [C1]), the pluggable X-curve becomes irreversible.

---

## Stage 1: Raw Materials & Substrates

### Key Players
| Company | Role | Ticker |
|---|---|---|
| IQE [[COMPANY:CO1]] | Primary InP wafer supplier; specialty compound semiconductor epitaxy | IQE.LSE |
| AXT Inc [[COMPANY:CO2]] | InP and GaAs substrate supplier; US-listed | AXTI.US |
| Sumitomo Electric [[COMPANY:CO3]] | Japanese InP wafer supplier; key non-US/China source | 5802.TSE |
| Shin-Etsu Chemical [[COMPANY:CO4]] | Largest silicon wafer supplier globally | 4063.TSE |

### Value Capture
- **InP wafers:** High-barrier, specialty process. Gross margins ~40–50% for wafer suppliers. No CMOS equivalent — the physics moat is structural.
- **Silicon wafers:** Commodity substrate. Margins ~20–25%, commoditizing further as SiPh volumes scale.

### Disruption Vulnerability
- **Pluggable dominance:** InP wafer demand scales directly with EML/DFB laser production. Constrained but premium pricing. US-China export controls on InP create a bifurcated supply chain — Chinese fabs cannot access US/Japanese InP wafers at scale [C2].
- **CPO dominance:** InP wafer demand partially preserved — CPO optical engines still require InP CW laser sources. Volume shifts from discrete EML chips to CW laser dies, but total InP wafer demand does not collapse.

### Winners / Losers Through 2036
| Condition | Winners | Losers |
|---|---|---|
| Pluggable (EML) | IQE, AXT, Sumitomo — premium pricing, constrained supply | Chinese InP wafer producers (export control exclusion) |
| CPO scaling | IQE, AXT — demand shifts to CW laser dies; volume lower but preserved | Discrete EML wafer demand collapses; net negative for InP wafer suppliers vs pluggable peak |

## Stage 2: Photonic Chip Fabrication

### Key Players

|---|---|---|

### Value Capture
- **InP EML fab (Lumentum, Coherent):** Gross margins ~40–55% at chip level. Vertically integrated players capture both fab and module margin. Revenue growth YoY: Lumentum +21% [C3].
- **SiPh CMOS fab (Tower, TSMC):** Tower Semiconductor gross margin ~14% [C4] — foundry economics, not component economics. TSMC gross margin ~45% [C5] — scale and advanced node premium.

### Disruption Vulnerability
- **Pluggable dominance:** InP EML fabs are structural winners. SiPh fabs scale in mid-tier. Both coexist — InP for performance, SiPh for cost.
- **CPO dominance:** InP EML fab revenue at risk post-2029. SiPh fabs (TSMC, Tower) gain as CPO optical engines are SiPh-based. TSMC is the critical execution node for CPO — CoWoS/SoIC packaging is inseparable from CPO chip fabrication.

| CPO scaling | TSMC — sole credible CPO packaging node; every CPO unit is a TSMC win | Lumentum, Coherent — EML chip revenue collapses; partial offset from CW laser supply |

## Stage 3: Laser & Optical Engine Components

### Key Players
| Company | Role | Ticker |
|---|---|---|
| Lumentum [[COMPANY:CO5]] | Dominant EML/DFB laser supplier; structural pricing power through 2028 | LITE.US |
| Coherent Corp [[COMPANY:CO6]] | Co-dominant EML/DFB; vertically integrated laser-to-module | COHR.US |
| Cisco (Acacia) [[COMPANY:CO11]] | SiPh-based coherent optical engine; leading SiPh coherent platform | CSCO.US |

### Value Capture
- **EML/DFB lasers:** Highest-margin component in the pluggable value chain. InP physics moat — no CMOS equivalent at 200G/400G per lane. Lumentum P/E (forward) 89x [C3] reflects market pricing of this structural position.
- **SiPh coherent engines (Acacia/Cisco):** Lower component margin but platform value — Acacia's SiPh coherent is a system, not a component. Cisco gross margin ~19% [C6] reflects system-level integration.
- **CW laser for SiPh:** SiPh cannot generate light natively — requires external InP CW laser. This hybrid dependency partially preserves InP laser revenue even in SiPh-dominant scenarios.

### Disruption Vulnerability
- **Pluggable dominance:** EML/DFB laser suppliers are the value capture apex of the pluggable chain. Pricing power is structural, not cyclical.
- **CPO dominance:** EML laser revenue collapses as pluggable modules are designed out. CW laser revenue partially offsets — but CPO requires fewer, higher-power CW lasers per port than pluggable EML arrays. Net negative for Lumentum and Coherent vs pluggable peak.

### Winners / Losers Through 2036
| Condition | Winners | Losers |
|---|---|---|
| Pluggable (EML) | Lumentum, Coherent — apex value capture, structural moat | Discrete component suppliers (isolators, lenses) — SiPh integration designs them out |
| Pluggable (SiPh) | Cisco/Acacia — SiPh coherent platform scales | Lumentum, Coherent — mid-tier EML market compressed |
| CPO scaling | Lumentum, Coherent (partial) — CW laser supply preserved | Lumentum, Coherent (net) — EML revenue collapse exceeds CW laser offset |

## Stage 6: Switch ASIC & Co-Design Layer

### Key Players
| Company | Role | Ticker |
|---|---|---|
| Broadcom [[COMPANY:CO14]] | Co-designing CPO switch ASICs (Tomahawk/Jericho); CPO integration is a structural lock-in moat | AVGO.US |
| NVIDIA [[COMPANY:CO15]] | ~$4B CPO commitment [C1]; CPO standard for next-gen NVLink and Ethernet switch ASICs | NVDA.US |
| Marvell Technology [[COMPANY:CO13]] | Custom ASIC design for hyperscalers with integrated CPO optical engine co-design | MRVL.US |

### Value Capture
- **Broadcom:** Gross margin ~37% [C9], market cap $1.59T [C9]. Switch ASIC + CPO co-design is a structural lock-in — whoever defines the CPO switch ASIC standard controls the entire downstream supply chain.
- **NVIDIA:** Gross margin ~56% [C10], market cap $4.45T [C10]. CPO is an architecture bet — NVLink and Ethernet switch ASICs designed natively for CPO lock in the optical supply chain to NVIDIA's ecosystem.
- **Marvell:** Custom ASIC + CPO optical engine co-design positions Marvell as the bridge between the ASIC and photonic layers.

### Disruption Vulnerability
- **In pluggable era:** Switch ASIC suppliers (Broadcom, NVIDIA) are indifferent to optical architecture — they supply ASICs regardless of pluggable vs CPO.
- **In CPO era:** Switch ASIC suppliers become the architectural control point. The ASIC defines the optical interface — CPO co-design is inseparable from ASIC design. This is the highest-value stage in the CPO chain.

### Winners / Losers Through 2036
| Condition | Winners | Losers |
|---|---|---|
| Pluggable dominance | Broadcom, NVIDIA — ASIC revenue independent of optical architecture | No losers at this stage in pluggable era |
| CPO scaling | Broadcom, NVIDIA, Marvell — architectural control point; lock-in compounds | Optical module suppliers — ASIC co-design excludes them from value capture |

### Disruption Vulnerability
