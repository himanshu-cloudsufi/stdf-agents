**Dominant Growth Vectors**

**Passenger BEV** remains the overwhelming demand driver, growing from 1,089 GWh (59% share) in 2026 to 4,312 GWh (59% share) in 2036 [C3]. This reflects the S-curve acceleration phase as battery pack costs fall below $80/kWh in China [[COMPANY:CO1]] and $95/kWh globally by 2026, triggering total-cost-of-ownership parity with ICE vehicles across all major markets [C4]. The 14.7% CAGR is not a linear projection—it embeds the left-skewed adoption pattern where 60% of the 10%→80% transition occurs in the first half of the S-curve.

**Commercial Vehicles** exhibit the highest growth rate at 19.0% CAGR, expanding from 156 GWh to 891 GWh [C5]. This segment includes light commercial vehicles (LCV), medium commercial vehicles (MCV), and heavy commercial vehicles (HCV). The acceleration is driven by total-cost-of-ownership advantages in high-utilization fleets (delivery, logistics, urban freight) where the payback period for electric powertrains has compressed to under 3 years in China and under 4 years in the US and Europe [C6].

**Grid BESS** (Battery Energy Storage Systems) grows from 203 GWh to 982 GWh, a 17.0% CAGR [C7]. This demand is driven by the merit-order displacement of peaker plants and the need for 2-4 hour duration storage to manage solar and wind variability. As SWB systems scale, the economic case for lithium-ion storage strengthens: battery costs below $100/kWh make 4-hour BESS competitive with natural gas peakers on a levelized cost basis, and below $70/kWh, BESS undercuts diesel and jet-fuel peakers entirely [C8].

**PHEV Demand Trajectory (Chimera Dynamics)**

Plug-in hybrid demand grows modestly from 178 GWh to 401 GWh, the slowest CAGR at 8.5% [C9]. PHEVs are chimeras—transitional technologies that extend incumbent economics but cannot compete with pure BEVs on cost or capability once battery costs fall below $90/kWh. By 2030, the total cost of ownership for a BEV will be 15-20% lower than a PHEV in China, Europe, and the US [C10]. PHEV share of total passenger EV demand declines from 14% in 2026 to 8.5% in 2036 as consumers and fleet operators rationally allocate capital to the superior economic choice [C11].

**Methodology & Scope Assumptions**

This forecast uses a **bottom-up segment model** with the following structure:

1. **Vehicle Stock-Flow Dynamics**: Annual sales, scrappage rates, and fleet turnover by vehicle class and region.
2. **Pack Size Evolution**: Average battery pack capacity per vehicle type, adjusted for energy density improvements (Wh/kg rising from 260 in 2026 to 320 by 2036 at the pack level) [C12].
3. **OEM vs Replacement Demand**: New vehicle production demand plus replacement packs for existing fleet (replacement cycle: 8-10 years for passenger, 5-7 years for commercial high-utilization).
4. **Grid BESS Sizing**: Derived from SWB capacity deployment and 2-4 hour duration requirements for peak shaving and frequency regulation.
5. **Datacenter & Telecom UPS**: Tied to compute infrastructure growth (AI training, inference workloads) and 5G/edge network expansion.

**Regional aggregation**: The forecast aggregates China, USA, Europe, and Rest of World. China accounts for approximately 48% of global demand in 2026, declining to 42% by 2036 as Europe and North America accelerate [C13].

**TaaS Scenario Exclusion**: This forecast reflects the **base case** (individual ownership model). The TaaS (Transport-as-a-Service) scenario, where autonomous ride-hailing fleets achieve 40-50% utilization and reduce total vehicle stock by 70-80%, would compress passenger vehicle demand by 60-70% post-2030 but is excluded from this projection per the lithium-ion-demand skill's default output [C14].

- **2030**: Commercial vehicle electrification reaches tipping point (15% of new sales); grid BESS demand surpasses 500 GWh [C16].

**Conclusion**

The 294% demand expansion from 2026 to 2036 is not speculative—it is the deterministic outcome of cost-curve trajectories that have already crossed economic parity in the largest markets. Passenger BEV and commercial vehicle electrification dominate absolute growth, while grid BESS represents the highest-growth rate application outside transport. PHEV demand grows but loses share, consistent with chimera dynamics. The forecast excludes TaaS disruption; if autonomous ride-hailing scales as projected, total vehicle-related demand would compress significantly post-2030, but grid and stationary storage demand remains unaffected. The 7.3 TWh production requirement by 2036 implies a 4x scale-up of global lithium-ion manufacturing capacity from 2025 levels, a trajectory consistent with announced gigafactory pipelines in China, Europe, and North America [C19].
