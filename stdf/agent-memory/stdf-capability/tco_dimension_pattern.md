## TCO Fleet-Average vs Segment-Level Analysis

When analyzing TCO as a capability dimension, fleet-average TCO can produce misleading PARTIAL/NO parity conclusions. Apply this pattern:

**Why:** Fleet-average TCO for EVs includes premium segments (F-150 Lightning, luxury SUVs) that pull the average above ICE. But mainstream purchase decisions are made at segment level. A buyer choosing between a Tesla Model Y and Jeep Grand Cherokee L sees EV TCO 15% below ICE. A buyer comparing Equinox EV vs Equinox ICE sees 20% EV savings. These are the actual decision contexts.

**How to apply:** Always decompose TCO into at minimum 4 segments: compact/subcompact, sedan/midsize, SUV/crossover, pickup truck. Report both fleet-average AND segment-level status. Mark as PARTIAL if fleet-average is above threshold but dominant-volume segments are below threshold.

**Threshold definition:** For TCO, the competitive threshold is NOT the ICE average value. It is: "at or below ICE TCO in the dominant-volume segment for the market being analyzed." In the US (2024), that means compact SUV and midsize SUV — both of which BEVs have already crossed.

**Data sources:** Vincentric annual EV Cost of Ownership Analysis (US market), iSeeCars depreciation data, AAA annual driving costs study.

**EV TCO parity timeline (US):**
- Sedan segment: achieved ~2023
- Compact/midsize SUV: achieved ~2023
- Pickup truck: projected 2027–2028
- Fleet average: projected 2026–2027
