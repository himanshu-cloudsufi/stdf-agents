## Agent Reasoning

The incumbent in this analysis is not a corporation, product line, or discrete industry -- it is human cognitive labor itself, the aggregate of economically productive mental work performed by approximately 1.2 billion knowledge workers globally. Mapping the X-curve therefore requires treating the labor market as the incumbent system undergoing incumbent displacement, with AI/Artificial Labor (AL) as the disruptor driving market-driven disruption powered by S-curve adoption dynamics. The mirror decline does not manifest as plant closures or bond downgrades; it manifests as hiring freezes, entry-level job market collapse, wage deflation, and structural unemployment in knowledge-intensive sectors.

The disruptor S-curve parameters (L=88%, k=0.5903, x0=2028.6) were taken directly from `05a-scurve-fitter.md`. The incumbent share is computed as 100% minus the disruptor's AI cognitive task share at each year, using `lib.scurve_math.xcurve_decline`. At the analysis date (March 2026), the model-derived AI share is 17.6% [model-derived], placing the incumbent share at approximately 82.4% -- the boundary of the "Early volume loss" stage. The crossing from Pre-disruption to Early volume loss occurred in 2025-2026 as the disruptor crossed the 10-15% threshold. This timing is consistent with the observed Stanford AI Index data: employment of software developers aged 22-25 fell approximately 20% from peak (2022-2025), and entry-level customer service headcount fell approximately 11-13% over the same period -- both signals of volume loss that presage the reinforcing decline loop.

The five market trauma mechanisms for human cognitive labor required reframing relative to the standard industrial incumbent analysis. Wage deflation replaces "fixed-cost spread" as the primary analog mechanism: as AI substitutes marginal cognitive units, employers reduce headcount rather than spread fixed costs -- the economic compression is transmitted through employment volume and wage growth suppression, not unit cost inflation. The remaining four mechanisms map directly: investment drought appears as collapse in entry-level hiring pipelines; talent flight inverts (the "talent" being junior workers who cannot compete with AI-augmented seniors); panic pricing appears as AI-driven contract rate compression in the gig and freelance knowledge work sector; and policy lobbying appears as labor regulatory intervention via the EU AI Act, collective bargaining clauses, and proposed AI employment protections. Evidence for all five mechanisms was gathered from primary sources including Stanford ADP payroll study (Brynjolfsson et al., 2025), BLS occupational data, NASSCOM India IT headcount data, and Eurofound collective bargaining reports.

Jevons Paradox is explicitly excluded per the stellar energy / Stellar technology classification of AI/AL. Cheaper AI inference does NOT generate compensating demand for human cognitive workers -- cost-curve dynamics compound into further displacement, not equilibrating demand recovery. The domain disruption file (`01-domain-disruption.md`) confirms the Stellar/AL classification for AI cognitive labor.

### Decline Loop Evidence

The reinforcing decline loop for human cognitive labor manifests across five cascading mechanisms, each self-reinforcing:

**1. Volume loss:** -20% employment for software developers aged 22-25 from 2022 peak to 2025 [observed, Stanford/ADP "Canaries in the Coal Mine," August 2025, T3]; -13% employment for young customer service workers in AI-exposed roles [observed, same source]; US tech job postings -36% vs Feb 2020 [observed, Indeed Hiring Lab, July 2025, T3]; Big Tech new graduate hiring down >50% since 2019, with new grads falling from 15% to 7% of Big Tech hires [observed, SignalFire VC data, 2025, T3]. India's TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL: collective headcount -42,000 over 2 years while revenue grew [observed, NASSCOM/company filings, 2025, T3]. Philippines BPO: 1.8 million workers, 36% at immediate automation risk [observed, IBPAP 2024, T3].

**2. Unit cost increase (analog: per-hire cost escalation):** The AI equivalent of unit cost increase is the rising cost of developing and retaining human cognitive workers who remain competitively productive. Average age of new technical hires has increased 3 years since 2021 [observed, SignalFire, 2025, T3] -- companies pay more for senior talent and eliminate junior pipelines. Infosys deploying 300+ enterprise AI agents achieving 5-15% productivity gains per agent [observed, Infosys Q3 FY2026 earnings call, T3] means remaining human workers must generate proportionally higher output per head to justify employment.

**3. Margin compression (wage bill deflation):** US college wage premium essentially flat since 2010 (NBER 2024 [T1]), with posted knowledge work salaries plateauing since mid-2024 [observed, Dallas Fed, February 2026, T1]. AI skills command a 56% wage premium [observed, PwC 2024, T3], implying a widening bifurcation: non-AI-augmented cognitive workers face wage deflation, AI-augmented workers receive premiums. Productivity growth in AI-exposed industries nearly quadrupled from 7% (2018-2022) to 27% (2018-2024) [observed, Stanford Digital Economy Lab, 2025, T3] while headcounts declined -- the productivity surplus is not flowing to displaced workers.

**4. Facility closures (analog: contract/outsourcing site closures and hiring freezes as structural substitutes):** Salesforce reduced customer support headcount from 9,000 to 5,000 via AI agents [observed, CEO Marc Benioff, podcast 2025, T3]. IBM replaced approximately 8,000 HR roles with AskHR AI chatbot [observed, IBM earnings communications, 2024, T3]. Microsoft cut 15,000 jobs in 2025 (7% of global workforce) with Copilot writing 30% of new code [observed, Microsoft investor communications, 2025, T3]. Duolingo offboarded 10% of content-creation contractors for AI replacement [observed, Duolingo statement, January 2024, T3]. AI specifically drove 54,000 confirmed job eliminations in the US in 2025 [observed, Challenger, Gray & Christmas consulting data, 2025, T3].

**5. Stranded assets (analog: stranded skills and education investment):** Over 40% of recent US college graduates underemployed (Federal Reserve Bank of New York, 2024 [T1]). Only 36% of US adults say college education "very important" -- down from 70% a decade ago [observed, Gallup poll, 2024, T3]. 50 college closures in 2022, rising to 80 in 2023 [observed, Higher Ed tracking data, 2023, T3]. Two-thirds of graduates beginning careers underemployed remain so ten years later [observed, Strada Education/Burning Glass Institute longitudinal study, T3]. The 4-year CS degree represents a 4-year, $80,000-$200,000 investment in skills that AI is rendering partially obsolete before graduation -- a classic stranded asset formation.

### The Deflationary Shock Mechanism

The X-curve dynamics directly answer the deflationary shock question posed by the analysis query. Wage deflation in knowledge work IS the primary deflationary mechanism:

1. **US cognitive labor wage bill:** approximately $6.5T/yr (87 million workers at $75,000 median annual wage [BLS 2024, T1])
2. **Global cognitive labor wage bill:** approximately $30T/yr (1.2 billion workers at $25,000 global average)
3. **At 50% AI substitution (2029 [model-derived]):** effective demand for human cognitive labor falls by approximately 40% from current levels
4. **Wage compression mechanism:** Productivity grows 27% in AI-exposed industries while headcount shrinks -- the surplus flows to capital (AI infrastructure owners) not labor. This is the deflationary transmission: wages stagnate or fall, corporate margins expand, aggregate consumer demand compresses.
5. **Bifurcation accelerates:** AI-skills workers earn +56% premium; non-AI-augmented cognitive workers face declining real wages. The middle of the knowledge worker distribution -- the largest economic class -- is compressed.

This constitutes a demand-side deflationary shock of approximately $3-6T/yr scale by the mid-2030s if wage bill compression tracks the S-curve displacement rate [model-derived, directional estimate]. The mechanism is not a one-time shock but a persistent structural drag: each year's cohort of AI-displaced junior workers reduces the subsequent year's consumer purchasing power base.

### Data Gaps

1. **No direct primary-source time series for AI cognitive task substitution share.** The 9.4% [2025] disruptor share is a constructed proxy (see upstream scurve-fitter). The incumbent share (90.6% at 2025) is the arithmetic complement of this proxy -- it inherits the same measurement uncertainty (approximately ±2-3pp at 2025 anchor).

2. **Entry-level headcount data is sector-specific, not economy-wide.** The Stanford/ADP study (Brynjolfsson et al., 2025) covers a large but not exhaustive sample of payroll records. The -20% software developer decline and -13% customer service decline are observed in ADP-covered employers. Economy-wide figures for all cognitive work categories are unavailable at this granularity.

3. **Wage deflation signal is indirect.** Direct wage deflation (falling nominal wages for knowledge workers) is not yet observed in aggregate data -- what is observed is: (a) flat college premium since 2010, (b) posted salary plateau since mid-2024, (c) headcount reduction without wage cuts (Stanford finding: adjustment via employment, not wages). True wage deflation may lag employment effects by 2-3 years.

4. **China cognitive labor displacement data is limited in primary-source quality.** The Peking University job postings analysis (Li Lixing, 2018-2024) is the strongest available China-specific evidence but covers one recruitment platform. CNBC and Wire China reporting is T3 quality. China BPO/IT displacement data is substantially less comprehensive than India/Philippines data.

5. **Philippines and India BPO displacement analyst projections cited for context only.** The "500,000-800,000 Philippines BPO workers at risk" figure is an analyst projection from investment banker Stephen Cuunjieng -- it has not been used to calibrate any model parameter and is noted as a risk indicator only.

6. **Deflationary shock magnitude is a rough directional computation.** The $3-6T/yr wage bill compression estimate uses BLS median wage applied uniformly to all knowledge workers -- in reality, wage effects will be heterogeneous by sector, seniority, and geography. The figure is directional only, not a precision estimate.
