## Agent Reasoning

This synthesis presented a qualitatively different analytical challenge from all prior STDF pipeline runs in this memory. Every previous analysis involved a physical technology disruptor (BEV, solar, battery, HDT) displacing a physical technology incumbent. Here, the incumbent is not a technology but a human input — cognitive labor — and the disruptor is a Stellar technology (AI inference / Artificial Labor) with zero marginal cost per cognitive output unit. This structural difference required careful framing throughout: the X-curve maps to a labor market decline, not a product market, and the deflationary shock question (the user's central analytical interest) required integrating across the xcurve-analyst's wage bill compression estimates, the cost-fitter's 69.5%/yr cost decline, and the tipping-synthesizer's 2027 central tipping year.

The most significant synthesis decision was resolving the tipping-synthesizer vs. scurve-fitter parameter conflict. The tipping-synthesizer estimated k=0.35 and x0=2027 provisionally (pre-scurve-fitter); the scurve-fitter returned k=0.5903 and x0=2028.6 empirically (R²=0.9910 vs. R²=0.8772 for k=0.35). Resolution per memory pattern from BEV-ICE analysis: defer to the scurve-fitter for S-curve projections, retain the tipping-synthesizer's rupture window (2026–2028, central 2027) for threshold-crossing analysis. These are complementary not contradictory — tipping precedes inflection in canonical STDF, and 2027 tipping central estimate is consistent with 2028.6 inflection (the S-curve is in exponential growth between the tipping point and the inflection). This was documented explicitly in Phase 6 to avoid user confusion.

The deflationary shock framing required integrating four agent outputs across a common analytical thread: cost-fitter (69.5%/yr cost decline, incumbent cost rising at 2.5%/yr), xcurve-analyst ($3–6T/yr wage bill compression, five market trauma mechanisms, 82.4% incumbent share as of March 2026), tipping-synthesizer (universal input displacement mechanism), and domain-disruption (Stellar classification with Jevons excluded — no equilibrating demand rebound). The synthesis Phase 5 and Phase 7 both make this integration explicit, and the Key Conclusion directly answers the deflationary shock question with a single declarative thesis. Per the STDF requirement, language is declarative ("will deliver"), the rupture window is specific (2026–2028), the binding constraint is named (adoption_readiness: skills gap), and the confidence is calculated transparently.

The regional-adopter output produced the most analytically distinctive finding: USA leads over China by 0.9 years, which is a structural deviation from every prior STDF physical technology analysis in this pipeline. This is explained by the Stellar technology characteristic — no manufacturing scale advantage when delivery is cloud compute. The EU's 4.8-year lag behind the USA (k=0.4783 vs k=0.6009) is one of the largest regional S-curve gaps observed in this pipeline and was flagged prominently in Phase 6.

Confidence aggregation used lib.tipping_math.confidence_aggregate across all 11 agents that ran. No agents failed; no CRITICAL criterion failures in any compliance checklist; no weakest-link cap applied. Final confidence 0.841. The principal drags are regional-adopter (0.71, proxy-constructed regional shares without directly observed per-region substitution time series) and domain-disruption + capability (both 0.82, reflecting the novel nature of measuring cognitive labor disruption without established primary-source metrics). The principal supports are cost-fitter (0.91, R²=0.983, 5 T2 data points, T1 cross-validation) and cost-parity-checker + capability-parity-checker (both 0.88).
