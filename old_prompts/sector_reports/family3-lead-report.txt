## 2. THE DISRUPTION: LEAD-ACID REPLACED BY LITHIUM-ION

Every major lead-acid battery application is being disrupted by lithium-ion through two independent mechanisms:

1. Vehicle electrification — as BEV/PHEV adoption rises, the ICE vehicle fleet shrinks, eliminating SLI battery demand at the source (fewer ICE cars = fewer lead batteries needed)
2. Battery chemistry substitution — even in remaining ICE vehicles, Li-ion 12V batteries are replacing lead-acid SLI batteries as they reach cost parity

Combined impact: battery lead demand declines from 10,779 kt (2024) to 2,043 kt (2040) — a 81% decline.

### What's Missing for Bottom-Up Modeling

To replace the top-down residual approach with proper bottom-up disruption modeling, the following data would be needed:

- Sub-application fleet sizes (e.g., how many railway signaling installations globally?)
- Battery replacement cycles per sub-application
- Application-specific Li-ion penetration rates and cost curves
- Regional breakdown (currently Global-only)

## 9. TAAS SCENARIO — AUTONOMOUS VEHICLE IMPACT

Transport-as-a-Service (TaaS) models a future where autonomous ride-hailing replaces private car ownership. Key assumptions:

- TaaS vehicles are 100% BEV with Li-ion 12V auxiliary = zero lead content
- Private fleet collapses as TaaS captures mobility demand
- Only affects D01 (passenger car SLI) and D03 (EV auxiliary)

## 10. GRAND SUMMARY

Total lead demand trajectory (Global):

Peak lead demand: 2024 at 12,470 kt

Three disruption mechanisms, ranked by impact:

1. Vehicle electrification (S1, S2, S3, S4, S5) — ICE fleet shrinks, eliminating SLI and starter battery demand at the source. Largest single impact because it affects both OEM and replacement channels simultaneously.
2. Battery chemistry substitution (S9) — Li-ion 12V replaces lead-acid in remaining ICE vehicles and stationary applications. Accelerates decline even in the ICE fleet that persists through 2040.
3. TaaS acceleration (S10) — Compresses the vehicle electrification timeline by collapsing private fleet ownership. Amplifies mechanism #1.

Data confidence:

- High confidence (66%): D01 + D02 — full bottom-up model with OEM/Replacement split, cost curves, tipping points, and regional granularity
- Low confidence (24%): D11 + D12 — top-down residuals with assumed disruption decay, no application-specific modeling
- Medium confidence (10%): D03–D10 — bottom-up models but simpler (no OEM/Repl split, some using fallback tipping heuristics)

## APPENDIX D: COST FLOOR METHODOLOGY

Disruptor cost curves use exponential decay extrapolation, which can project costs to zero or near-zero. To prevent physically impossible outcomes, a raw-material cost floor is applied BEFORE tipping detection and in all forecast displays.

### Floor Values Applied

| Curve | Floor | Unit | Derivation |
|-------|-------|------|------------|
| Li-ion BEV pack | 25 | $/kWh | Cell raw materials (~$31) minus margin for commodity declines |
| Li-ion 12V SLI | 20 | $/unit | 0.75 kWh x ~$26/kWh cell materials for small format |
| Li-ion 12V EV Aux | 15 | $/unit | 0.5 kWh x ~$26/kWh + simpler BMS materials |
| Li-ion 2W/3W pack | 30 | $/kWh | Cell raw materials + small-pack housing penalty |

All floors are well below current tipping-point prices — tipping years are unchanged.

### Incumbent Post-Tipping Clamping

If the incumbent's 5-year linear trend has negative slope (costs declining), the forecast holds flat at the last historical value. Rationale: the disruption framework predicts incumbents entering a vicious cycle (loss of volume -> loss of economies of scale -> costs should rise or at minimum hold flat). Flat is the conservative assumption.
